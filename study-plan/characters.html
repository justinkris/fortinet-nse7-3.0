Main Characters

Seven voices carry the whole 80-episode arc. Every technology emerges from something one of them said, broke, or refused to sign off on. Study the cast — and half the exam questions will feel like remembered conversations rather than memorised facts.

How to read this page. Each card gives you (a) the one-line hook, (b) who they are inside LoFinance, (c) their role in the teaching frame, (d) the signature moves that let you predict what they'll do, and (e) the way they earn or lose trust with you across seasons.

The main study plan uses these people constantly. If a session feels flat, the answer is usually "which character was missing?" — bring them back in and the scene rebuilds itself.

You — the reader
Intermediate Network Engineer · POV Character
Protagonist 3–5 yrs experience Every episode

Competent and trusted. No longer learning fundamentals — learning how senior engineers think. One day, the engineer Junior looks up to.

Who you are at LoFinance

You joined six months ago from an MSP background. You already know subnetting, basic NAT, what a firewall is — you're here to learn how the enterprise reasons about failure, trade-offs, and design at scale. Penny trusts your ETAs. Cipher trusts your caution. Señor trusts you to try before asking.

Role in the story

You are the reader's avatar — the character whose curiosity drives every scene. When Junior guesses wrong, you're the one who quietly runs the verification command. When Señor asks a Socratic question, it's you he's asking. Your growth arc is the exam itself.

Signature moves

  • Reaches for get and diagnose before reaching for the GUI.
  • Sanity-checks Junior's changes before they hit production.
  • Writes the change record Penny will actually read.

What Claude teaches through you

Senior-level reasoning, design trade-offs, and failure modes. Never fundamentals — the script assumes you know them. When you get something wrong, it's a scenario-level mistake, not a syntax one.

Signature lines

"Before we change it — what does it look like right now?"

"Junior, hold on. Let me see the output first."

Junior
First IT Job · Exam-Question Engine
Foil 6 months in Every episode

Knows enough theory to be dangerous. Asks the questions exam candidates secretly have. His mistakes become exam questions.

Who Junior is at LoFinance

Fresh from a bootcamp and a CCNA. Sharp memory, patchy intuition. He can quote OSPF LSA types but has never watched an area flap in production. Riley loves him. Cipher doesn't.

Role in the story

Junior is the reader's externalised uncertainty. Whatever you're afraid to ask out loud, Junior asks — loudly, in front of Señor. Whatever half-truth you almost believed, Junior states as fact and gets corrected. He is the reason the exam questions land.

Signature moves

  • Answers first, and often wrong — but confidently.
  • Confuses similar features (FGCP vs FGSP, IKEv1 vs IKEv2).
  • Skips the verification step, then blames the platform.
  • Occasionally causes the next episode's outage.

What Claude teaches through Junior

The distractor answers on the exam. Every session ends with 2–4 NSE7-style scenario questions and at least one is born from a Junior mistake in that episode. If you can predict Junior's wrong answer, you can eliminate the tempting distractor.

Signature lines

"Isn't that basically the same as…?"

"I already changed it — was I supposed to check first?"

"Wait, so what does FGSP do differently from FGCP again?"

Señor
Senior Engineer · 30+ years · Cowboy Hat Custodian
Mentor Sombrero Immaculate docs Cowboy Hat Sessions

Sombrero. Chaotic desk, immaculate docs, strange analogies. Never answers — asks the one question that makes it obvious. When the cowboy hat comes out, production breaks.

Who Señor is at LoFinance

Nobody knows his real name or when he arrived. Multiple decades at a tier-1 carrier before Fortinet existed. He designed the original LoFinance topology in one afternoon on a whiteboard nobody was allowed to erase for two years. Now he mostly asks questions.

Role in the story

The Socratic engine. Señor never gives an answer directly — he asks the one question that makes the answer obvious. He is why every technology in the curriculum arrives as a realisation, not a lecture.

Signature moves

  • Speaks in analogies (villages, ledgers, mail routes, kitchens).
  • Answers a question with a better question.
  • Draws boxes on a whiteboard and walks away.
  • Puts on the cowboy hat when he's about to break something on purpose.

The Cowboy Hat protocol

Nine sessions across the curriculum are boss battles. Riley reports the symptom in business language, and Señor has already broken something inside the stated scope. You drive; Claude plays the network. Layered faults are allowed. Debrief is graded like a real senior would grade you.

Signature lines

"What would you check first?"

"Where is the packet at that moment — really?"

"Mm. Interesting. Show me the config, not the theory."

Penny Ledger
Operations Manager · Approver of All Change
Business Zero networking Elite prioritisation

Networking: zero. Business: elite. "How long? How much? Can we do it today?" The reason projects happen.

Who Penny is at LoFinance

Fifteen years running operations for trading desks. She can't ping a router but she can tell you exactly what an hour of downtime costs on a Tuesday morning versus a Friday afternoon. She owns the change window calendar and the budget line item for every firewall you'll ever touch.

Role in the story

Penny drives the why now. Every episode opens with a business event — Penny is the reason it becomes a project. She refuses to let you buy new hardware when a config change will do; she refuses to let you cheap out when the trading floor is at risk.

Signature moves

  • Three questions, in order: how long, how much, can we do it today.
  • Translates engineer-speak into board-speak and back.
  • Vetoes anything that reads as "we could rebuild the whole thing…".

What Claude teaches through Penny

Trade-offs. Design decisions on the NSE7 blueprint (VDOMs vs new box, FortiManager templates vs per-device config, HA topology choices) are always cost/risk calls first, feature choices second. Penny is why you never solve the problem with a bigger appliance.

Signature lines

"How much? How long? Can we do it today?"

"I don't need to understand it. I need to know what happens if we don't."

"That sounds like six weeks. Give me six days."

Cipher
Senior Security Engineer · Chief Objector
Antagonist-ally Favourite word: No Usually correct

Favourite word: "No." Everything is a risk. Always arguing. Usually correct. The engine of every trade-off.

Who Cipher is at LoFinance

Ex-CERT, ex-red-team, now the person who has to sign off on every ruleset that faces the internet or the trading floor. Trusts nothing implicitly — not the vendor, not the appliance, not the previous engineer. Especially not Junior.

Role in the story

Cipher is the friction. Every proposed change gets pushback: what does the log look like, what does the inspection profile allow, what happens if the certificate expires at 3 a.m.? Without Cipher, decisions get lazy. With Cipher, you learn to justify.

Signature moves

  • Reads a policy top-to-bottom before speaking.
  • Turns down "just this one exception" three times before agreeing.
  • Owns the SSL/SSH inspection profile, the IPS profile, and the application-control list — and defends each with data.

What Claude teaches through Cipher

Security-side blueprint topics — SSL inspection, web filter, application control, ISDB, IPS, secure-network use cases. Every trade-off between visibility, latency and risk that shows up on the exam has Cipher's fingerprints on it.

Signature lines

"No."

"Show me the log first. If it's not in the log, it didn't happen."

"That exception lives for exactly one change window."

Riley
Help Desk Lead · Ground-Truth Reporter
Incident opener Business language only Cowboy Hat opener

First to notice, never technical: "The traders can't log in." "VPN feels weird." Every incident begins with Riley.

Who Riley is at LoFinance

Runs a three-person help desk. Sees every user complaint before anyone else. Has excellent instincts and no vocabulary for them — will tell you the network "smells off" and turn out to be right. Owns the internal ticketing queue and the phone.

Role in the story

Riley is the symptom, never the diagnosis. Every incident opens with Riley reporting something in business terms — the printers have become sentient, the exec floor can hear itself in the trading VoIP, the VPN drops on the third packet. Your job is to translate.

Signature moves

  • Reports symptoms; refuses to diagnose.
  • Names the user, the app, and the time — three data points, never more.
  • Is polite enough that you don't realise how urgent it is until you check the SLA.

What Claude teaches through Riley

Symptom → root cause reasoning. Cowboy Hat sessions always open with a Riley report — you never get to skip the "translate the user's words" step. On the exam, scenario stems read like Riley tickets.

Signature lines

"The traders can't log in."

"VPN feels weird."

"The printers on the new VLAN have become sentient."

TAC
Vendor TAC Engineer · Rare Cameo
Cameo Packet-capture first Case number aware

Brilliant. Asks for a packet capture before saying hello. Rare, valuable cameo.

Who TAC is at LoFinance

Not a LoFinance employee — a Fortinet vendor engineer on the other end of a P1 ticket. Anonymous by design (you'll get a different TAC every case; the character is the archetype, not the person). Appears only when the fault is genuinely inside FortiOS or the hardware.

Role in the story

TAC is the outside opinion. When Señor's questions run out, when Cipher has audited the config three times, when Junior has stopped guessing — TAC steps in with a diagnostic move nobody else knew existed.

Signature moves

  • Asks for a packet capture before saying hello.
  • Asks for the exact firmware build, not the version.
  • Names the debug flow filter that changes everything.

What Claude teaches through TAC

The advanced diagnose debug flow / diagnose sniffer packet / diagnose sys ha toolchain — the commands the exam expects you to have watched a senior engineer run. TAC appears sparingly so the tools feel earned.

Signature lines

"Attach the sniffer output and the exact firmware build to the case."

"What did the debug flow filter show?"

"That's a known one. Case number, please."

Marcus
Index Futures Trader · Recurring Business Voice
Recurring Trader-avatar "Is it broken?" Coffee-machine ambusher

The trader down the hall. Started life as pure "is the internet broken" energy — earned a real question in Session 03, and has been the "give the trader a one-sentence answer" device ever since.

Who Marcus is at LoFinance

Index futures trader. Sits on the floor, not in a meeting room. Introduced in Session 03 as a recurring minor character; upgraded to recurring anchor the moment he cornered the student at the good coffee machine on the third floor and asked "where does my order actually go?" — a question the student realised he could not actually answer. By Session 12, Marcus is initiating trading-share sessions from the Parramatta branch, and his flows are the reason a one-directional IPsec policy gets caught.

Role in the story

Marcus is the trader test. Penny represents the business at the management layer, Riley represents users at the help-desk layer — Marcus represents the end user whose P&L is directly wired to whatever you just changed. If you cannot explain your fix to Marcus in one sentence with no jargon, you have not finished thinking about it.

Signature moves

  • Ambushes engineers at the good coffee machine — never at a desk, never on a ticket.
  • Opens with "is it broken?" and follows with the real question three sentences later.
  • His order flow is the running example the whole session ends up tracing.
  • Initiates connections from the branch back to HQ — the direction Junior always forgets to write a policy for.

What Claude teaches through Marcus

The one-sentence, non-jargon translation of every architectural concept. Marcus anchors abstractions to a real end-user — packet path, tunnel initiation direction, session tables, latency budgets — so that "correct" isn't measured by the config, it's measured by whether Marcus's share opens on the first click.

Signature lines

"Where does my order actually go?"

"Is the internet broken?"

"I just need the share. Right now. Now, ideally."

George Kanellis
Head of the Trading Floor · Latency Cost Enforcer
Stakeholder 56 · ex open-outcry Two watches Money-per-microsecond Hates unpriced security

Fifty-six. Twenty years on open-outcry floors before the screens took over — never forgave the screens for being slower than his voice used to be. The one stakeholder in the building who will not accept a technical noun as an answer.

Who George is at LoFinance

Wears expensive shirts badly — sleeves rolled, tie at half-mast by 9:31 AM. Wears two watches, one on Sydney time and one on New York time, and claims he can feel drift between them. Keeps a laminated card in his wallet that reads "11µs = the spread" and shows it to people instead of explaining. Doesn't hate security. Hates unpriced security.

Role in the story

George is the price of the trade-off. Penny drives the why now; George drives the what does it cost per microsecond. He and Cipher have never once agreed in a meeting, and both secretly suspect the other is good at their job. When the two of them are in the same room, a real design decision is about to happen.

Signature moves

  • Refuses "latency-sensitive" as a phrase — demands a number in dollars per unit of time.
  • Shows the laminated card before letting you finish the sentence.
  • Compares two clocks on his wrists mid-conversation and looks disappointed at both.
  • Blocks anything he considers unpriced security overhead; approves anything with a defended cost line.

What Claude teaches through George

The economics half of every inspection decision. Flow vs proxy, cert vs deep, IPS depth, HA failover budget — George is why those choices are measured in cost of delay, not just feature capability. When an exam scenario mentions a market-data feed or a trading application, George's rule applies: what does the delay actually cost. The fastest way to lose him is to say "latency-sensitive." The fastest way to win him is to say what a delay costs.

Signature lines

"11µs is the spread. Tell me what your firewall costs me in those units."

"Don't say latency-sensitive. Say what the delay costs."

"I don't hate security. I hate unpriced security."

Where to Stand Guard

UTM profile placement across NGFW · ISFW · DCFW · DEFW — because "everywhere" is the answer you give when you have traced zero flows.

Cipher counts controls. The network is made of flows.
The whole session in one line: don't reason about the name of a control — reason about the flow underneath it.
01 · Story Recap

The UDIS Memo

Tuesday, 8:12 AM — twenty-three hours after the market-data feed came back from the S25 false positive. Cipher emails the company (CC Penny): a "Uniform Deep Inspection Standard." Enable full SSL inspection, IPS, web filtering, application control and antivirus on every FortiGate — edge NGFW, ISFW, DCFW, branch DEFWs. His logic: "Full inspection saved us on the DCFW yesterday, so it belongs everywhere. One posture. No blind spots."

The trading floor revolts. Enter George Kanellis, Head of the Trading Floor — twenty years on open-outcry floors, two watches (Sydney and New York time), a laminated card that reads "11µs = the spread." His message, relayed by Riley minus the swearing: the desks are eleven microseconds from being uncompetitive, and inspection between two trading servers is money set on fire. Penny cuts in: "Is 'everywhere' four times the safety — or four times the cost for the same safety? Answer me in money."

Señor, feeding the magpie at the window, asks exactly one question and turns back around: "Cipher wants to guard every door. The floor wants the doors to still open. Which doors — and why not all of them?"

02 · Learning Objectives

What This Session Locks In

  • Why "it worked on box A" never implies "it belongs on box B" — control vs threat surface.
  • The difference between genuine redundancy (same flow, same control, twice) and defence in depth (different controls, different catches).
  • Why the DCFW is the sole guard of east-west traffic, not a backup for the edge.
  • Protect-the-client vs protect-the-server — decided by whose key you hold, not where the packet came from.
  • When a branch DEFW needs no UTM — and the single change that flips that.
  • Why the ISFW's low latency fights heavy UTM, and the one flow that justifies paying it anyway.
03 · The Core Principle

Flows, Not Controls

Cipher's memo rests on pattern-matching by label: he sees "full inspection" do good work, so he wants "full inspection" everywhere. The senior move is to refuse to reason about the name of the control and ask instead: which flow, in which direction, protecting what asset, and does any other box already inspect it?

Copy a control without copying its threat surface and you have copied nothing but the cost. Full inspection is expensive — it decrypts, it inspects, it spends CP cycles and adds latency. So where you pay that cost is an architecture decision, not a default.

🧭
Mental Note
"Is this double-dipping?" is never a yes/no question. It is: which flow, which direction, protecting what.

05 · Redundancy vs Depth

The Real Double-Dip

Inbound internet traffic to the data centre crosses both the edge NGFW and the DCFW. Running the identical full-inspection profile on both, for that same north-south flow, is not defence in depth — it is the redundancy the study guide explicitly flags between NGFW and DCFW. Depth means different controls catching different things; this is the same control catching the same thing twice, at double the latency.

East-west traffic is the opposite case. A database replicating to its mirror, a backup job, one trading service calling another — those packets are born and die inside the data centre. The edge NGFW could have every profile enabled and inspect zero of them. So on east-west the DCFW is not a redundant second helping — there was never a first dip. It is the only guard in a room the edge cannot see.

Analogy — No First Dip, No Double Dip
A shared hallway with two guards frisking the same visitor is waste. But a locked interior room only one guard can reach isn't "backup" — it's the only security that room will ever have.
06 · Protect-Client vs Protect-Server

Whose Key Do You Hold?

Two completely different jobs share the name "full SSL inspection." The deciding axis is not "inside vs outside the network" — that's a decoy. It is which asset you're protecting and whose key the FortiGate holds.

Protect the CLIENTProtect the SERVER
DirectionOutbound (your users → internet)Inbound (clients → your server)
What the FGT doesImpersonates the external server; presents its own re-signed cert (FGT's CA) to your userHolds the real server cert + private key; decrypts with the genuine key
Trust requirementEndpoints must trust the FGT's CAFGT is loaded with your server's cert/key ("Protecting SSL Server")
Natural box at LoFinanceEdge NGFW (the front door)DCFW (in front of the trading servers)
Proof it isn't about origin: yesterday's PHP-injection-over-HTTPS came from the internet — yet the fix went on the DCFW, because the DCFW sits in front of the asset being attacked and holds that server's key. Direction-of-user was never the decider.

And because these are different flows, directions, keys and assets, both boxes running "full inspection" is not the double-dipping we warned Cipher about. Same words; different jobs.

07 · The Branch & The Segment

DEFW and ISFW

DEFW — tunnel-only, until it isn't

Parramatta and Chatswood are tunnel endpoints. Every internet-bound packet backhauls through HQ and crosses the edge NGFW, which already reads its letters. Inspecting again at the branch is a double-dip — and a 30–90 series box can't run the edge's posture anyway.

The flip: the instant a branch gets local internet breakout, that traffic never touches HQ. A new unguarded door appears. Now the DEFW must inspect — but the study guide's answer isn't "turn on UTM," it's "re-size the branch to a 100–900 midrange first, then enable it."

Analogy — Hallway vs New Door
Backhaul is a hallway — everyone still passes the HQ guard. Breakout cuts a new door in the wall. Cipher sees a wall and wants a guard; fine, but nobody built the guardhouse yet.

ISFW — fast on purpose

The ISFW exists to contain lateral movement: if a trader's laptop is compromised, it's the wall that stops the infection walking into Finance or Exec. Its gift is low latency — and heavy UTM taxes exactly the high-volume east-west user traffic it's meant to pass cleanly. That's George's microseconds.

So what justifies paying the latency? A flow, not a control name. The LoFinance case comes straight from Session 24: a compromised endpoint inside a trusted VLAN launched an exploit at the trading API. That justifies IPS on the internal wall — scoped to catch a known exploit signature moving laterally between segments, the packet the edge and DCFW will never see. (The guide's other case: an ISFW wearing an NGFW second hat — terminating VPNs, running SD-WAN / secure internet access.)

08 · Configuration Reality

Placement Lives in the Policy

"Where to inspect" is really "which policy, on which box, carries which profile." The profiles are attached at the firewall-policy level:

Attach profiles per policy — the placement decision, in config
config firewall ssl-ssh-profile
    edit "deep-inspect-outbound"      # protect-CLIENT: FGT re-signs w/ its CA
    next
    edit "deep-inspect-trading"       # protect-SERVER: FGT holds real server cert+key
    next
end

config firewall policy
    edit <outbound-users-to-internet>   # on the EDGE NGFW
        set ssl-ssh-profile "deep-inspect-outbound"
        set ips-sensor       "default"
        set av-profile       "default"
    next
    edit <inbound-clients-to-trading>    # on the DCFW
        set ssl-ssh-profile "deep-inspect-trading"
        set ips-sensor       "protect-trading-api"
    next
    edit <isfw-vlan-to-vlan>             # on the ISFW — scoped, lateral-movement only
        set ips-sensor       "lateral-movement"   # NO av/webfilter — keep it fast
    next
end
Verify before you load. get system performance status — check CP/CPU headroom before adding UTM to a box. diagnose sys session list — confirm which box a given flow actually traverses before you decide where to inspect it.
09 · Troubleshooting a Placement Dispute

Trace the Flow, Don't Argue the Control

When two engineers fight over where a profile belongs, don't debate the control. For each disputed link, ask four questions in order:

  1. Which direction? North-south (crosses the edge) or east-west (never does)?
  2. Whose asset? Are we shielding a browser (client) or a box being connected to (server)?
  3. Whose key? Does the FGT re-sign (client) or hold the real key (server)?
  4. Who else already inspects it? If another box on the path already does the job, you're looking at redundancy — pick one primary point.

The answers name the box. "Everywhere" fails every one of these questions at once — which is exactly why it's always the wrong answer.

10 · Common Mistakes

Where Engineers Trip

  • Calling the DCFW "limited." It's a 1000–7000 box with hardware SSL inspection — arguably the most capable inspector you own. You allocate its capacity to east-west; you don't withhold inspection because it "can't." Allocation, not incapacity.
  • Using inside/outside as the client/server axis. The decider is whose key you hold, not where the packet originated.
  • Reading "same control, two boxes" as automatic redundancy. Only redundant if it's the same flow. Outbound-client and inbound-server share a name and nothing else.
  • Loading UTM onto a 30–90 DEFW after breakout. Re-size to 100–900 first.
  • "IPS scanners or similar" on the ISFW. Name the flow (lateral-movement signatures), not a vague control.
11 · Senior Engineering Insight

The Business Sentence

Penny didn't ask for the mechanism. She asked for money. The engineer who's right becomes the engineer in the boardroom by leading with the consequence and letting the mechanism come second — or not at all:

Model close: "Inspecting everything everywhere would cost us money on every trade without catching a single extra attack — so we guard only where the attacks actually travel." That one sentence ends the Cipher-vs-George war: his gaps are imaginary, George's microseconds are real, both settled in Penny's language.
12 · Exam Tips

What the Test Rewards

  • On "should we enable X everywhere?" — the answer is almost always no, place by flow. "Everywhere / uniform / all firewalls" is a distractor pattern.
  • Watch for the redundancy-dressed-as-depth trap: same control + same flow + two boxes ≠ defence in depth.
  • Client vs server inspection is decided by whose certificate/key the FortiGate uses — re-signed CA (client) vs real server key (server).
  • DEFW UTM questions hinge on backhaul vs local breakout.
  • Read your own reasoning, then commit the matching verdict. On T/F, an absolute qualifier ("never / any / always / regardless") is usually the thing that makes it false — and the verdict is the graded answer, not your explanation.
13 · Key Takeaways

Carry These Forward

  • Cipher counts controls; the network is made of flows.
  • DCFW = sole east-west guard (no first dip, no double dip). NGFW = front-door decrypt. ISFW = fast, scoped IPS only. DEFW = tunnel-only until breakout.
  • Same control name ≠ same job — decided by direction, asset and key.
  • Lead with the money; the mechanism comes second.
  • Sign the verdict your argument proves.

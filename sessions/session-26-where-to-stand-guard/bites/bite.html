SESSION 26 · BITE · UTM PLACEMENT

Where to Stand Guard

Five minutes. The placement map, the double-dip rule, and the one sentence for Penny.

Cipher counts controls. You count flows.
The One Idea

Place by Flow, Not by Name

"It worked on the DCFW, so put it everywhere" is Cipher's error. A control matched to one box's threat surface, copied without that surface, copies only the cost. Full inspection is expensive — where you pay is an architecture decision.

The Double-Dip Rule

Redundant or Not?

North-south shared path (internet→NGFW→DCFW): same control, same flow, twice = the real redundancy. Trim to one primary decrypt point.

East-west: the NGFW never sees it. No first dip → no double dip. The DCFW is the sole guard, not a backup.

🚪
Remember
Same control name ≠ same job. Full inspection outbound (protect-client, re-signed cert) and inbound (protect-server, real key) are different flows — not a double-dip.
Client vs Server

Whose Key?

Not "inside vs outside" — that's a decoy. Protect-client = outbound, FGT presents its own re-signed cert = edge NGFW. Protect-server = inbound to your box, FGT holds the real key = DCFW. (S25 proof: PHP-over-HTTPS came from the internet, fix went on the DCFW.)

The Business Line

Say It to Penny

"Inspecting everywhere would cost us money on every trade without catching a single extra attack — so we guard only where the attacks actually travel." Money first. Mechanism second, or not at all.

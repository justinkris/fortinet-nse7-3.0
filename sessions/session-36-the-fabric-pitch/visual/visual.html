VISUAL LEARNING PAGE · SESSION 36

The Fabric Pitch — Seen, Not Said

Long-term memory through pictures: one audit, one tree, one very patient Dave.

Recommended Visual Style

Recommendation: Packet Journey × Enterprise Topology hybrid Session 36's concepts are inherently spatial (a tree with a root, a session travelling through layered firewalls, an outsider at a door) and evidential (which log appears where). A hybrid works best: one hero topology-as-story image, one packet-journey strip for the logging laws, and character-driven scenes for authorization and the business case. Memory-palace and pure-decision-tree styles were considered and set aside — the logging rules only make sense along a path, so the path should be the picture.

All images share the LoFinance house style: flat, minimalistic-but-detailed vector illustration; cream/warm off-white background; coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines; clean bold line work; no gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D; storybook-professional. Recurring cast details: Señor's sombrero, the frozen-money-in-an-ice-cube LoFinance logo, Riley's headset, Junior's overconfidence, the Intermediate Engineer as calm and increasingly trusted.

1 · "Show Me Every Device"
RECOMMENDED ASPECT RATIO 16:9 · PLACE AT TOP OF PAGE
Purpose: anchor the business problem — siloed knowledge under audit pressure

The scene that started everything: the question no single product could answer.

Image Generation Prompt
Flat vector storybook illustration, cream off-white background, coral and muted-gold accents, sage green details, soft brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism. A corporate conference room at a financial firm called LoFinance; the company logo — stacks of banknotes frozen inside a translucent ice cube — hangs on the back wall. Camera angle: eye level from the end of a long table, slight wide shot. On the left, an external auditor: a composed woman in her 50s (Dr. Vasse), grey tailored suit, holding an open leather notebook and an uncapped fountain pen, one eyebrow slightly raised; a speech bubble in clean serif type reads "Show me every device on your network." Across the table, four staff frozen mid-reaction: a young engineer (Junior) clutching useless paper printouts; a security officer (Cipher) with arms crossed and a bead of sweat; an operations director (Penny) glancing sideways at the fifth figure; and the calm Intermediate Engineer, the only one thinking rather than panicking. At the far end, an eccentric senior engineer in a sombrero (Señor) serenely placing a mandarin peel — one unbroken spiral — on the table. Behind the staff, five separate small monitors, each showing a DIFFERENT partial network diagram that visibly does not connect to the others — emphasising siloed knowledge with small gaps of empty wall between screens. Mood: pin-drop silence, gentle comedy under real tension. Visual hierarchy: the auditor's question largest, the disconnected monitors second, the mandarin spiral as a quiet focal joke. Must NOT appear: vendor logos other than the fictional LoFinance mark, dense technical labels, photoreal faces.
Memory anchor: five screens that don't connect = islands of truth; the fabric exists to join them.
Accessibility: an auditor asks a silent room to show every network device while five disconnected diagrams glow behind the staff.
2 · The Tree With One Root
RECOMMENDED ASPECT RATIO 3:2 · PLACE BESIDE TOPOLOGY SECTION
Purpose: lock in tree topology, root-by-upstream-position, and the root's three jobs

The fabric drawn as a living tree — because "root" and "branch" were never metaphors by accident.

Image Generation Prompt
Flat vector educational illustration, cream background, sage-green foliage, soft brown trunk outlines, coral and muted-gold accents, no gradients. A stylised tree drawn UPSIDE-DOWN-root-up in cross-section against a warm sky: at the TOP, the thick trunk-root is a firewall appliance labelled "NGFW — FABRIC ROOT" wearing a tiny golden crown; growing DOWNWARD from it, two sturdy branches end in two smaller firewall appliances labelled "DCFW" and "ISFW"; from those, fine twigs reach down to leaves shaped as end devices — laptops, a printer, a desk phone, and one slightly guilty-looking smart TV leaf glowing faintly. Beside the crowned root, three small hanging signs on golden cords list its jobs in short hand-lettered labels: "TOPOLOGY VIEWS", "CONNECTORS", "AUTHORIZATION". To one side, a small ground-level plaque reads "root = most UPSTREAM, not most traffic", with a faint dotted arrow showing all sap/traffic flowing from leaves upward through branches into the root and out to a stylised cloud labelled "Internet". A watering can labelled FortiAnalyzer sits at the base collecting droplets from every branch through separate independent tubes (each branch has its OWN tube — none pass through the root). Composition: root/crown dominant top-centre, FAZ watering can bottom-left as secondary focus. Must NOT appear: mesh cross-links between branches, real vendor logos, cluttered IP labels.
Memory anchor: crown at the most upstream point; every branch has its own tube to the FortiAnalyzer can — root down, sap still collected.
Accessibility: a tree-shaped network with a crowned root firewall at top, branch firewalls below, device leaves, and independent tubes feeding a FortiAnalyzer watering can.
3 · The Logging Laws — One Session's Journey
RECOMMENDED ASPECT RATIO 16:9 · PLACE IN LOGGING SECTION
Purpose: the packet journey that encodes first-logs, MAC recognition, the NAT exception, and UTM logging

Follow one envelope through three checkpoints and count the stamps.

Image Generation Prompt
Flat vector left-to-right packet-journey strip, cream background, coral/gold/sage palette, soft brown outlines, no gradients. A cheerful anthropomorphic envelope (the session) with little legs walks a winding path through three border-checkpoint booths drawn as friendly customs posts. BOOTH 1 "ISFW": the officer stamps a large coral stamp on a ledger labelled "TRAFFIC LOG #1 — first to handle it" and also snaps a small instant-photo of the envelope's home address tag "10.20.1.55". BOOTH 2 "NGFW": the officer recognises a small MAC-address badge pinned on the envelope reading "from ISFW" and waves it through the traffic-ledger WITHOUT stamping — but this booth does two other things, drawn clearly: (a) it swaps the envelope's address tag for a new public tag "203.0.113.10", and BECAUSE of the swap adds a gold stamp to a second ledger "TRAFFIC LOG #2 — NAT: new identity"; (b) a magnifying-glass inspector at the same booth examines the envelope's contents and writes in a separate green notebook labelled "UTM LOG — logged where inspected". BOOTH 3, drawn faded at the end of the path, is the open internet gate. Below the whole strip, pneumatic tubes carry every ledger page independently down to a large friendly archive machine labelled "FortiAnalyzer — correlates automatically", shown clipping the pages of this one journey together with a single paperclip. A small footnote plaque: "One session. Two traffic logs (first + NAT). One UTM log. Zero duplicates." Must NOT appear: a stamp at booth 2's traffic ledger without the NAT tag-swap shown, tubes routed through booth 2 on their way to the archive.
Memory anchor: the second stamp only exists because the address tag changed — NAT = new identity = new log; the green notebook lives at the booth that looked inside.
Accessibility: an envelope passes three checkpoints; the first logs it, the second logs it again only because it changes the address and separately inspects contents, and all pages feed an archive machine.
4 · Dave at the Door
RECOMMENDED ASPECT RATIO 4:3 · PLACE IN AUTHORIZATION SECTION
Purpose: weld the authorization rule to an unforgettable character moment

"Join Existing Fabric" is a knock, not a key.

Image Generation Prompt
Flat vector character illustration, cream background, coral door, muted-gold and sage details, soft brown outlines, gentle comedy, no gradients. Exterior of a grand but friendly building whose façade is subtly shaped like a firewall appliance, with the LoFinance frozen-money-ice-cube logo above a large coral front door labelled "FABRIC ROOT". On the doorstep stands Dave: an earnest, slightly dishevelled branch-office engineer holding a small firewall appliance under one arm like a puppy, and in the other hand a bouquet of ethernet cables arranged like flowers. A paper tag on the appliance reads "role: Join Existing Fabric ✓ upstream IP: correct ✓". He is pressing a doorbell whose small screen shows "Request pending… (4th attempt)". Through a window beside the door, warm interior light shows the Intermediate Engineer at a desk looking at a queue screen titled "System > Firmware & Registration — 1 pending", finger hovering over an "Authorize" button, expression kind but unhurried. A doormat reads "MEMBERSHIP BY INVITATION". Night sky, one hopeful star. Composition: Dave and bouquet dominant left, the authorization screen visible and legible right. Must NOT appear: an open door, Dave inside, any suggestion the device connected by itself.
Memory anchor: both checkboxes ticked and he is still outside — config makes the request; only the root's authorize button makes a member.
Accessibility: an engineer with a firewall and a cable bouquet waits at a locked "Fabric Root" door while an admin inside considers a pending authorization queue.
5 · Two Doors of Connectors

One rule, two doors: family through the gold door, the world through the green one.

Flat vector illustration, cream background, no gradients, soft brown outlines. Interior hallway of the fabric-root building from image 4, seen straight-on. Two large doors side by side. LEFT door in muted gold, sign "FABRIC CONNECTORS — family only": queueing outside it are friendly personified Fortinet-style appliances drawn generically (no real logos) wearing matching family scarves — a log-archive machine tagged "FAZ", a laptop-shepherd tagged "EMS", a small firewall tagged "FGT", plus two cousins tagged "NAC" and "NDR". RIGHT door in sage green, sign "EXTERNAL CONNECTORS — the rest of the world": queueing are travellers with suitcases — a fluffy cloud with a name-tag "AWS/GCP", a ship's-wheel character tagged "Kubernetes", an ID-badge character tagged "FSSO/RADIUS", and a courier holding a long paper scroll of IP addresses tagged "THREAT FEED"; the courier's scroll visibly refreshes via a small clock icon and feeds through a slot that dispenses a neat labelled brick "resource object" onto a shelf marked "usable in firewall policies". Must NOT appear: real vendor logos, more than ~9 characters total, small unreadable text.
Memory anchor: the sorting sentence above the doors is the exam answer; the scroll-becomes-brick machine is the external feed.
Accessibility: two labelled doors — Fortinet family appliances queue at one, cloud/identity/threat-feed travellers at the other, where a list-scroll becomes a policy object.

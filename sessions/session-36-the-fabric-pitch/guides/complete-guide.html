The Fabric Pitch

"She is not asking about firewalls. She is asking whether the firewalls talk to each other." — Señor

Session Overview & Story Recap

Insurer-appointed external assessor Dr. Imogen Vasse opened LoFinance's compliance review with one question: "Show me every device on your network." The room's silence was the lesson: LoFinance's firewalls, FortiManager, FortiAnalyzer, and endpoints each hold a piece of the truth, and none of them share it. Señor's whiteboard answer — FABRIC — became a full architectural pitch, interrupted mid-session by "Dave," a Vaultline branch engineer who pointed a spare FortiGate at the HQ NGFW and assumed he'd joined. He hadn't. Dr. Vasse left conditionally satisfied: "follow-up review upon implementation." Penny approved the build.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of the Security Fabric and its three pillars: Broad, Integrated, Automated.
  • Describe the fabric's tree topology — root FortiGate vs downstream FortiGates — and why the root is the most upstream device.
  • Identify the core components (FortiGate + FortiAnalyzer) and the role of extended members (EMS/FortiClient).
  • Distinguish fabric connectors from external connectors, including external threat feeds.
  • Predict fabric logging behaviour: single-log rule, NAT exception, UTM logging, and root-failure independence.
  • Explain why downstream devices must be authorized on the root.

1 · The Problem the Fabric Solves

Every standalone firewall's session table is its whole universe. The DCFW sees a packet arrive with a source IP and MAC — it does not know which physical device created the session, which user was behind it, or what the ISFW already inspected. Three firewalls means three private versions of the truth.

🏝️Islands of truth. Every answer to the auditor's question already exists — scattered across six products that don't speak to each other. The fabric is not a new box; it's turning the boxes into one nervous system.

Visibility follows proximity: devices are born at the access layer; the edge only sees their shadows. The ISFW sees the laptop's real MAC, DHCP, and ARP; by the NGFW that identity may be NAT'd away. And the logical end of that reasoning: why be near the source when you can be on it — FortiClient, connected through FortiClient EMS, reports from inside the endpoint (hostname, OS, user, vulnerabilities).

2 · Purpose & the Three Pillars (BIA)

The Security Fabric is Fortinet's cybersecurity mesh platform, powered by FortiOS — convergence and consolidation across the whole portfolio, and open to third parties via connectors. Its three key concepts:

3 · Tree Topology — Root & Downstream

The fabric is a tree, not a mesh. One FortiGate serves as the fabric root — chosen by position in the flow, not traffic volume: the most upstream device toward which every session ultimately drains. For LoFinance that is the HQ NGFW. Downstream FortiGates (DCFW, ISFW) each point at their upstream device.

NGFW ← Fabric ROOT (single pane) / \ DCFW ISFW ← downstream members | | servers users/devices

The root is where the fabric is administered: the Physical Topology and Logical Topology views, fabric connector configuration, and member authorization all live on the root.

4 · Core Components

The minimum viable fabric is FortiGate + FortiAnalyzer. The topology views answer "what exists now"; only FortiAnalyzer answers "what happened last Tuesday" — and auditors live in last Tuesday. Real-time visibility without historical evidence is a pitch; visibility with evidence is compliance. EMS/FortiClient, FortiManager, FortiNDR, FortiNAC and friends extend the fabric further.

Business framing (Penny-approved)"When — not if — we have an incident, regulators will demand a complete record of every device and every session, and the fabric turns that from an unaffordable court date into a ten-minute report."

External Feeds

Point FortiGate at a URL hosting a plain-text list (malicious IPs, domains, hashes, FortiGuard categories). FortiGate fetches it periodically over HTTP, HTTPS, or STIX — or receives webhook updates via the Push API to the REST API. The feed becomes a resource object usable directly in firewall policies: the upstream list changes, the policy's effective contents change, and nobody edits a policy at midnight. (Junior's manual blocklist career: ended.)

6 · Fabric Logging on FortiAnalyzer

The fabric as a whole logs each session once — owned by the first FortiGate that handled it. Fabric members know their peers' MAC addresses; a packet arriving from a fabric peer's MAC generates no duplicate traffic log.

The two principled exceptions

  • NAT: if an upstream member NATs the session, it generates an additional traffic log — the new source IP/port is evidence that exists nowhere else. The NAT log is the Rosetta Stone joining 10.20.1.55:51442 (what the ISFW knew) to 203.0.113.10:8831 (what the internet saw).
  • UTM: UTM events always log wherever the inspection happens. Suppression applies to duplicate traffic logs, never to UTM logs.

FortiAnalyzer correlates traffic and UTM logs into one session record automatically — no extra configuration.

Root failure ≠ dark evidenceEach fabric FortiGate logs to FortiAnalyzer independently and directly. The root coordinates the fabric; it is not a logging relay. Root down at 2 a.m. → the witnesses keep writing.

7 · Joining the Fabric — Authorization

A downstream device sets role Join Existing Fabric and the upstream FortiGate's IP — and then waits. An administrator must authorize it on the root: FortiGate requests appear under System > Firmware & Registration; other fabric products (e.g. FortiNDR) appear as a notification by the alert icon on the root's GUI. If IP-reachability were enough, anyone who could reach the root could pull a map of your entire network. Fabric membership is deep trust — invitation only.

# Root (HQ-NGFW) — Security Fabric > Fabric Connectors > Security Fabric Setup Security Fabric role: Serve as Fabric Root Allow other Security Fabric devices to join: port5 Fabric name: lofinance # Downstream (ISFW / DCFW) — same page Security Fabric role: Join Existing Fabric Upstream FortiGate IP/FQDN: 10.0.12.254 # Then, on the root: System > Firmware & Registration → authorize the pending device

Troubleshooting Workflow (taught)

  1. "Session logged twice / not at all?" Identify the first fabric FortiGate in the path — it owns the traffic log.
  2. Did any upstream member NAT? A second traffic log there is correct, not a bug.
  3. UTM logs belong to the inspecting device — different species from traffic logs.
  4. Root down but logs missing? Root is not a relay — investigate the member↔FAZ path itself.
  5. New device not in the fabric? Check the authorization queue on the root before touching IP config.

Common Mistakes

  • Junior's: counting firewalls to count logs ("two devices = two logs"). The fabric dedupes by design; the exceptions (NAT, UTM) are principled, not random.
  • Dave's: assuming Join Existing Fabric + root IP = membership. No authorization, no fabric.
  • Choosing the root by traffic volume instead of upstream position.
  • Vocabulary: knowing the concepts but missing Fortinet's official triad (Broad · Integrated · Automated).

Senior Engineering Insights

  • "Juniors answer with the box in front of them. Seniors answer with the system." The auditor asked an architecture question, not a firewall question.
  • The strongest business case for visibility is regulatory obligation, not feature value — and it fits in one breath if you practise compression.
  • When evaluating any distributed design, trace who actually talks to whom before predicting failure behaviour — that's how the root-down question answers itself.

Exam Tips

  • Core fabric components = FortiGate + FortiAnalyzer (two-answer MCQ favourite).
  • Memorize BIA verbatim; expect impressive-sounding distractors.
  • Logging scenarios: first device logs; +1 traffic log per NAT; UTM logs at the inspection point; leaf logging survives root failure.
  • Fabric vs external connector sorting: Fortinet product? Fabric connector. Anything else (SDN, FSSO/RADIUS, threat feeds)? External.
  • External feed → periodically fetched (HTTP/HTTPS/STIX or Push API) → resource object in policies.
  • Downstream join is inert until authorized on the root.

Key Takeaways

The fabric turns islands of truth into one nervous system: a tree with a root pane, FortiAnalyzer as its memory, connectors as its senses, deduplicated evidence as its record, and an authorization queue as its front door.

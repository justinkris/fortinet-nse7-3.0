Recommended Style

Why Packet Journey + Analogy

Session 56 has an unusual shape: two problems that look different and turn out to be the same problem. That structure is what needs to be remembered, more than any individual command.

A pure topology diagram would flatten it back into "two links." A pure analogy page would lose the packet-level detail that makes multihop and the policy table make sense. So this page uses a hybrid:

  • Real-world analogy — Penny's two roads — carries ECMP and its honest limitation, because the road metaphor makes the 180-second hole physically obvious.
  • Packet journey — a single TCP SYN travelling from Loopback0 to Singapore — carries update-source, multihop and the policy table, because those three only make sense as things that happen to a packet in sequence.
  • A final convergence panel ties both back to Señor's framing: same problem, different hats.
Alternatives considered and rejected: a Troubleshooting Decision Tree (already covered in depth by the troubleshooting guide — duplication), and a Memory Palace (this session's concepts are causally linked rather than list-like, so a spatial mnemonic would work against the reasoning).

Using These

How to Revise From Images

  1. Look at Panel 1 and say out loud why the second road is empty. If you reach for "ECMP isn't enabled," you've skipped the precondition.
  2. Look at Panel 2 and state the number. If "180" and "half" don't both arrive, re-read the hold timer section.
  3. Walk Panel 3 left to right and name each checkpoint's config line without looking.
  4. Look at Panel 4 and answer: what single question tells you which door? Where does the packet start and where does it leave.
  5. Look at Panel 5 and name the open risk. If you can, you're ready for Session 57.

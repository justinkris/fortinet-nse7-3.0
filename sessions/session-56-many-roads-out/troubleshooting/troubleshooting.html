SESSION 56 · TROUBLESHOOTING · SENIOR METHOD

Two Faults, One Symptom

How a senior engineer separates a policy fault from a TTL fault when both say the same word: Active.

The state tells you the layer. The interfaces tell you the table.

This guide is about thinking, not commands. The commands are here to test hypotheses you formed first.

Premise

Two Faults That Look Identical

Today's technologies fail in two distinct ways, and neither announces itself honestly:

  • ECMP that isn't. Everything is Established, everything looks healthy, and one circuit carries nothing. There is no error anywhere.
  • A loopback peering that won't form. Stuck in Active — the exact same symptom as last session's fault, with a different root cause and a different table.
The governing danger: you have now seen "stuck in Active" resolve to local-in policy (S55) and to firewall policy (S56). If you carry last week's answer into this week's fault, you will verify the wrong table thoroughly and correctly, and find nothing.
Fault A

Symptom: Both Peers Up, One Path Carrying

What the business says: "We're paying for two links and the graph on the second one is flat."

Questions to ask, in order

  1. Is the second session actually Established, or merely configured? (Flat graphs sometimes mean no peering at all.)
  2. Is the prefix present in the database from both peers, or only one?
  3. Do the two paths genuinely tie on every attribute — or did something break the tie above router-id?
  4. Is multipath enabled, and is it the correct knob for the peering type?
Evidence
# 1 — are both sessions real?
get router info bgp summary

# 2 — does the database hold both paths?
get router info bgp network 0.0.0.0/0

# 3 — what actually got installed?
get router info routing-table bgp

# 4 — is multipath configured?
show router bgp | grep multipath
What you seeWhat it means
Database holds one path onlyNot an ECMP problem. The second peer isn't advertising, or an inbound filter dropped it.
Database holds two, routing table holds one, attributes differCorrect behaviour. A tie-breaker fired. Fix the attributes, not the ECMP config.
Database holds two, attributes identical, routing table holds oneMultipath not enabled — or the wrong knob for the peering type.
Two next-hops on the prefixECMP is live. If traffic still looks lopsided, that's hashing, not routing.
◆
The discriminating question
Before touching ECMP config, confirm the two paths actually tie. Enabling multipath on paths that differ changes nothing, and you will spend an hour concluding the feature is broken.
Fault B

Symptom: Loopback Peering Stuck in Active

What the business says: "Singapore isn't up." What you know from the state: transport is failing. Not parameters — transport. The TCP connection is not completing.

Start where you always start. Stuck early = transport. Cycling late = parameters. Established but empty = policy. Active is early. You are looking for something stopping a TCP connection, not something two routers disagree about.

The two questions that structure everything

Señor's method
"Where does the packet start. Where does it leave. Say both out loud."

Answer those aloud — Loopback0 and port2 — and the table you need selects itself. Two different interfaces on the same FortiGate means firewall policy. One interface, terminating on the box, means local-in. This single habit prevents the entire class of error.

The sniffer is the highest-value command in this session's material. It answers "what does the peer see" — which is the only question that matters for update-source, and the one you cannot answer from config alone. SYN going out with nothing coming back also cleanly separates "my side is broken" from "their side is refusing."

◆
The ping trap is worth memorising
A successful ping to the peer loopback proves the route exists. It says nothing about whether a session sourced from your loopback can get there. Different source, different policy match, different result.
Fault C

The One That Isn't a Fault

ECMP is live, both next-hops installed, and traffic is split 70/30 rather than 50/50. Nothing is broken.

ECMP distributes flows, not bytes, by hashing. A small number of large flows will not distribute evenly no matter how correct the routing is. Before investigating, ask: how many distinct flows are we talking about? A trading feed that is three long-lived TCP sessions cannot be balanced across two paths by any hash.

Senior move: set the expectation before deployment, not after the first graph. "Both links will carry traffic; they will not carry equal traffic" is a sentence that prevents a meeting.
Root Causes

Ranked by Likelihood

  1. Missing firewall policy (loopback → physical). The most-missed piece, and the one the study guide calls out explicitly.
  2. update-source not configured. Peer ignores a SYN from an unexpected address.
  3. ebgp-enforce-multihop not enabled. TTL 1 dies before arrival.
  4. No route to the peer's loopback, or a route over only one underlay (which defeats the purpose).
  5. Multipath not enabled where ECMP was expected.
  6. Wrong multipath knob for the peering type.
  7. Peer-side configuration — symmetric requirements not met on their box.
Exam Traps

How This Gets Weaponised

  • Local-in as a distractor. A loopback-peering scenario offering local-in policy as an option. It was right last time. It is wrong here.
  • "The routers are directly connected" — offered as a reason multihop isn't needed. It is exactly the reason it is: the cable is one hop, the loopbacks are not on the cable.
  • "All attributes are identical" without mentioning config. The answer is still one route installed.
  • Same-AS scenarios offering ebgp-multipath. Compare the AS numbers before reading the options.
  • Redundancy questions. "ECMP is configured, a carrier fails, what is the impact before the hold timer expires?" The tempting answer is "none." The correct answer is ≈half of flows.
Method

Your Framework, Applied

1. Restate the business symptom. ("Singapore is down" / "the second link is flat.")
2. Read the STATE first and let it select the layer.
3. Say the two interfaces out loud. Start, and leave. That chooses the table.
4. Gather evidence before changing anything — sniffer before config.
5. One hypothesis at a time, working down the ladder in order.
6. Verify recovery and root cause — Established is not the same as ECMP live.
7. Name the residual risk out loud, in business language.

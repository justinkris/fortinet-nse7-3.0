Core Concept

How the Security Rating Actually Works

The Security Rating audits the entire Security Fabric — not one device. Its architecture follows a pattern you've already seen twice this season:

🧠
Mental Model
Push the work to the edges, pull the verdict to the centre. Stitches: authored on root, executed on leaves. Logging: every member writes to FAZ independently. Rating: every member self-audits, root compiles the score.

Each fabric member checks its own configuration against a fixed set of Fortinet best-practice checks. Results roll up the tree. The root compiles them into one unified score and grade for the whole fabric — because the root is the only device with the whole picture: the tree, the members, the connectors.

Analogy

"Everyone marks their own homework and hands it in — with a rulebook they cannot edit." (Junior, kept because it's accurate.)

Score → Grade → Context

The raw score is points-weighted by check severity and device importance, converted to a letter grade. On its own, a grade is trivia. Two context layers turn it into a decision-making tool:

  • Trend — the rating re-runs automatically on a schedule, so a history exists from the day the fabric went live. A line, not a photograph.
  • Industry percentile — FortiGuard compares your score against similar-sized organizations in your sector.
License dependency: the full check set and the industry percentile comparison both require a FortiGuard Security Rating subscription. Without it, the report degrades to a basic local subset — same asymmetric-degradation shape as the FortiGuard web filter outage from Session 23. Verify contract state with diagnose autoupdate versions, not by touching config.

The score is not click-only, either — it re-runs on its own schedule and can be triggered on demand from the GUI, which matters any time you need fresh proof of a fix before a deadline.

Findings Triage

Beyond Severity-Only Sorting

The findings list is pre-sorted by severity — but severity alone doesn't tell you what to fix first. Three lenses do the real work:

LensQuestionWhy It Matters
ExposureWho can reach this without any prior foothold?Internet-facing + precondition-free findings are categorically worse — anyone can attempt them.
Blast radiusWhat does a successful exploit hand the attacker?Admin access on the edge firewall is the keys to everything.
Cost to fixZero-disruption CLI change, or a change window?Firmware upgrades reboot the box — schedule around business hours, don't wing it on Monday.
Analogy

"Severity tells you how bad the fall is; exposure tells you how many people are standing near the ledge."

Pull a log window long enough to catch rare or periodic flows (month-end batch jobs, weekly backups) before tightening — guessing here creates a delayed, hard-to-diagnose outage.
Automation Detail

The Schedule Trigger — Frequencies and the Clock

The Schedule automation trigger (used for config-backup stitches) offers five frequency options:

Once — one-time execution at a defined future time Hourly Daily Weekly Monthly
Common trap: "Now" is not an option. Schedule is a calendar mechanism — "Once" fires at a future defined time, it doesn't fire instantly on save.

Every stitch is authored on the root FortiGate and pushed to relevant leaves at config time. That means the schedule's time reference — "Daily at 2 AM" — is measured by the root's system clock, regardless of what timezone a downstream member is configured with.

Hook

"Root's ink, root's clock." The stitch is written in the root's ink; it runs on the root's clock.

Common Mistakes

Where Engineers Trip

  • Severity-only triage. Sorting purely red→orange→yellow→white misses that some findings need zero disruption to fix (do them now) while others need a change window (schedule them) — severity alone doesn't separate those.
  • Guess-tightening "all/all" policies. Changing a broad policy without a FAZ log review risks killing a legitimate but infrequent flow — the outage won't appear until the next time that flow was due to run.
  • Treating the score as a photograph. It's a re-running, schedulable process. Waiting passively for the "right" number to appear wastes a deadline that an on-demand re-run could have met.
  • Confusing "Now" with "Once." The Schedule trigger has no instant-execution option.
Senior Engineering Insight

Split the Deliverable, Don't Split the Difference

When two stakeholders want incompatible things from the same artefact, the senior move is rarely to pick a side or negotiate a compromise version that satisfies neither. It's to ask what each stakeholder actually needs the artefact to do, and recognise that a single document can be two different things to two different audiences — a grade and a trend line for the board; a target list for the security team. Splitting by purpose, not by politics, is what let both Penny and Cipher walk away satisfied.

The same instinct applies to remediation timing: fixing the cheap, zero-disruption findings before a deadline doesn't just tidy the report — it changes the number the stakeholder actually sees. Evidence-driven urgency beats waiting for a scheduled process to catch up.

Exam Tips

What NSE7 Wants You to Know

  • The Security Rating computes locally per-device and compiles at the root.
  • Schedule trigger frequencies: Once, Hourly, Daily, Weekly, Monthly — governed by the root's system time.
  • Config-backup results appear under Configuration > Revisions.
  • Full check set + industry percentile require a FortiGuard Security Rating subscription.
  • Manual endpoint quarantine: diagnose endpoint fctems queue-complete-calls Q-<ip> — unsupported on FortiClient (Linux).
Key Takeaways

What to Carry Forward

Session Closed

  • The fabric's recurring design pattern — distribute the work, centralise the verdict — now applies to three features you know: stitches, logging, rating.
  • Triage by exposure, blast radius, and cost-to-fix; severity is the starting sort, not the final word.
  • Evidence (FAZ) before action, always — especially before tightening a broad policy.
  • A number without trend or comparison is trivia; with them, it's a decision-making tool.

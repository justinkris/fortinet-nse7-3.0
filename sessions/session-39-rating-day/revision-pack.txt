------------------------------------------------------------------
QUICK SUMMARY (ONE PAGE)
------------------------------------------------------------------
The Security Rating audits the entire fabric. Each fabric member
self-checks its own configuration against Fortinet best-practice
checks; results roll up to the ROOT, which compiles one unified
score and grade. This is the same architectural pattern already
seen in stitches (authored on root, executed on leaves) and fabric
logging (each member logs independently to FAZ): distribute the
work, centralise the verdict.

A bare score is trivia. Two context layers make it board-usable:
TREND (the rating re-runs automatically on a schedule, so a history
exists) and INDUSTRY PERCENTILE (compared to similar-sized orgs in
your sector). Both the full check set and the percentile comparison
require a FortiGuard Security Rating subscription — without it, the
report degrades, echoing the FortiGuard-dependency pattern from the
Session 23 web-filter outage. The rating can also be triggered
ON DEMAND from the GUI, not just on its schedule.

Findings should be triaged on three lenses, not severity alone:
EXPOSURE (who can attempt it, with what precondition?), BLAST
RADIUS (what does a win hand the attacker?), and COST TO FIX
(instant CLI change vs. needs a change window). Broad "all/all"
firewall policies cannot be safely tightened from config alone —
pull FortiAnalyzer traffic logs first, over a window wide enough to
catch rare/periodic legitimate flows.

The Schedule automation trigger offers five frequencies: Once,
Hourly, Daily, Weekly, Monthly — there is no "Now" option — and the
schedule runs on the ROOT FortiGate's system clock, because stitches
are authored on the root. Config-backup results land in
Configuration > Revisions. Manual endpoint quarantine uses
`diagnose endpoint fctems queue-complete-calls Q-<ip>`, unsupported
on FortiClient (Linux).

Business resolution: a two-stakeholder custody conflict over the
same report was resolved by splitting the artefact by AUDIENCE —
derived metrics (score/trend/percentile) to the board; raw findings
kept internal — and by closing the cheapest, highest-value findings
BEFORE the deadline so the presented number itself improved.

                                                (manual quarantine)

SCHEDULE TRIGGER FREQUENCIES
Once · Hourly · Daily · Weekly · Monthly
(NOT "Now" — no instant-execution option)
Clock reference = ROOT FortiGate's system time.

DEFAULTS / KEY BEHAVIOURS
- Rating re-runs automatically on a schedule AND can be triggered
  on demand.
- Full check set + industry percentile require a FortiGuard
  Security Rating subscription.
- Each fabric member self-audits; root compiles the unified score.
- FortiClient (Linux) does not support remote quarantine.

DESIGN RULES
- Triage findings by exposure > blast radius > cost-to-fix, not
  raw severity order alone.
- Never tighten a broad "all/all" policy from config alone — pull
  FAZ traffic logs first, over a wide-enough window.
- Split board-facing artefacts by audience/purpose, not by picking
  a side in a stakeholder conflict.

------------------------------------------------------------------
FLASHCARDS
------------------------------------------------------------------
Q: Where does the Security Rating's compiled score come from?
A: Each fabric member self-audits its own config; root compiles all
   results into one unified score/grade.

Q: What two context layers turn a bare rating score into something
   a board can act on?
A: Trend (score over time) and industry percentile (vs. similar-
   sized orgs).

Q: What does a FortiGuard Security Rating subscription unlock?
A: The full check set and the industry percentile comparison.

Q: Command to check FortiGuard contract/subscription state without
   changing anything?
A: diagnose autoupdate versions

Q: Three lenses for triaging rating findings beyond severity?
A: Exposure, blast radius, cost to fix.

Q: Why can't you safely tighten an "all/all" policy from config
   alone?
A: Config shows what's permitted, not what's actually used — pull
   FortiAnalyzer traffic logs first, over a window wide enough to
   catch rare/periodic flows.

Q: Five Schedule trigger frequency options?
A: Once, Hourly, Daily, Weekly, Monthly (no "Now").

Q: Whose system clock governs the Schedule trigger's timing?
A: The ROOT FortiGate's — stitches are authored on root and pushed
   to leaves.

Q: Where do Schedule-triggered config backups appear in the GUI?
A: Configuration > Revisions.

Q: CLI command for manual endpoint quarantine via EMS?
A: diagnose endpoint fctems queue-complete-calls Q-<endpoint_ip>

Q: Which FortiClient platform does NOT support quarantine?
A: Linux.

Q: In the fabric's SAML SSO relationship, who is the sole IdP, and
   who are the SPs?
A: Root = IdP. Downstream FortiGates, FortiAnalyzer, FortiManager =
   SPs. (FortiAuthenticator is a separate standalone product, not
   part of this fabric relationship.)

Q: Where do downstream fabric members' files go for FortiNDR
   analysis, and what happens to the user's session while pending?
A: Files transit THROUGH THE ROOT. The user's session is HELD —
   nothing is delivered — until FortiNDR returns a verdict.

PODCAST PROMPT — "THE PACKET PATH", EPISODE 39
------------------------------------------------------------------
Generate a podcast episode for "The Packet Path" titled "Rating
Day." Two hosts, warm and conversational, discussing enterprise
network security with a light narrative frame. Structure:

1. COLD OPEN: Set the scene — a fictional but relatable company,
   LoFinance, has two department heads in conflict over one
   security report. One wants a number for a board presentation.
   The other is protective of what the report reveals about the
   company's weaknesses. Frame this as a familiar tension anyone
   in security operations has felt.

2. THE CORE CONCEPT: Explain how Fortinet's Security Fabric
   computes a "security rating" — each firewall in a corporate
   network audits ITSELF against best-practice checks, and a
   central "root" device compiles everyone's results into one
   score. Use the analogy: "everyone marks their own homework,
   but with a rulebook they can't edit." Explain why a bare score
   is nearly useless without two things: a TREND over time (is it
   getting better?) and an INDUSTRY COMPARISON (how do we stack up
   against similar companies?).

3. THE TRIAGE LESSON: Walk through why a security engineer doesn't
   just fix problems in order of "how scary they sound." Introduce
   three questions: who can even attempt this attack (exposure)?
   what do they get if they succeed (blast radius)? and how
   disruptive is the fix (cost)? Use a concrete example: an admin
   login exposed to the whole internet is fixed immediately because
   literally anyone can try it — versus a firmware update that gets
   scheduled for a weekend because it requires a system reboot.

4. THE EVIDENCE PRINCIPLE: Explain why you should never guess when
   tightening an overly broad security rule — you have to look at
   actual traffic logs first, and look far enough back in time to
   catch rare but legitimate business processes (like a monthly
   report job) that would otherwise get accidentally blocked.

5. JUNIOR'S MISTAKE: Include a moment where a junior engineer
   suggests just waiting for the automatic weekly report to update
   itself before a Thursday deadline — and gets gently corrected:
   these systems usually let you trigger an on-demand refresh
   instead of waiting on a schedule. Frame this as a broader life
   lesson about not waiting on a timer when a button exists.

6. THE RESOLUTION: The two department heads both get what they
   actually needed once someone realized the report could be split
   into two different documents for two different audiences — a
   simple trend line for leadership, and the detailed findings kept
   internal for the security team.

7. TEASER FOR NEXT EPISODE: End with an exciting hook about how
   milliseconds of network delay can cost a financial trading firm
   real money, and next time the show will explore specialized
   hardware chips that firewalls use to process traffic faster than
   software alone ever could.

Tone: curious, warm, a little wry humor, accessible to a general
technical audience without requiring firewall expertise. Keep
technical terms explained in plain language the first time they're
used. Target length: 12-15 minutes of spoken content.

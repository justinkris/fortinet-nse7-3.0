TECHNICAL CONCEPTS COVERED (review only — no new material; listed per drill question)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Q1 — Architecture/roles: NGFW/ISFW/DCFW/DEFW are not fixed hardware SKUs or licensed
    modes; the label describes POSITION IN THE NETWORK + THE TRAFFIC THAT CROSSES IT ON
    THAT PATH, not the box itself. One FortiOS, one hardware family, can wear any of the
    four hats depending on placement. Applied correctly to Parramatta: small branch, edge
    position, primary job = securing the link back to HQ (+ possible direct internet
    breakout) = DEFW.
Q2 — VLAN segmentation mechanism (Session 4 recap): a VLAN only "counts" as segmentation
    when its default gateway lives on the firewall (forcing routing AND policy evaluation
    to occur there), not on a switch. Student additionally and correctly raised an
    operational argument beyond the pure security one: keeping everyone on one flat/large
    subnet increases broadcast-domain noise (ARP, MAC table pressure) as the office scales
    — this is new synthesis, not previously articulated this cleanly, worth tracking as a
    genuine strength (sees BOTH the security and the operational cost of flat networks).
    One precision correction delivered: "routing before policy" reframed more precisely as
    "policy match requires a known destination interface (srcintf/dstintf), so routing must
    resolve first, not because routing categorically 'runs first' as a separate stage."
Q3 — 802.1Q tagging mechanics (Session 4 recap): trunk port terminology, the 4-byte 802.1Q
    tag carrying VLAN ID, and egress-adds/ingress-removes tagging direction relative to the
    FortiGate — all retrieved cleanly and immediately, zero hesitation, matching study guide
    language exactly. No new ground, pure clean retrieval.
Q4 — THE CENTRAL SYNTHESIS OF THE SESSION: "A VDOM does not relocate a physical box."
    Presented as a business scenario (put Parramatta's local firewall presence into a VDOM
    carved from the HQ chassis instead of shipping hardware to the branch). Required three
    guided steps to land: (1) student's first answer defaulted to Session 6/7/8 material
    (resource contention/Override Maximum) — true but answering the wrong question, a
    genuine "reach for the nearest familiar mental model" pattern worth tracking; (2) once
    redirected to "where does the physical box sit," briefly answered "on the branch edge"
    — an actual factual slip (the box in Cipher's proposal is explicitly the EXISTING HQ
    box, not a new branch box) rather than a retrieval-under-pressure issue; corrected
    cleanly and immediately once re-asked; (3) once anchored on "the box is at HQ," student
    self-generated the full consequence chain unprompted — two Parramatta laptops three
    desks apart would have to cross the WAN link twice (out to HQ's VDOM, back again) just
    to talk to each other, correctly framing this as wasted throughput AND (implicitly) a
    single point of failure. This is a genuinely new, high-quality piece of independent
    reasoning, not a repeated Session 6/7 idea — first time this specific "VDOM is bound to
    a chassis, not a location" idea has been taught/tested this course, and the student
    built the real-world consequence himself once the anchor was corrected.
Q5 — VDOM types (Session 6 recap): NAT mode (routed interfaces, own IP scheme, appears as
    a layer-3 hop) vs Transparent mode (layer-2 bridging, invisible to existing addressing,
    no routing) — clean, immediate, correct on both, no hesitation.
Q6 — Inter-VDOM link requirements (Session 7 recap), run as a T/F "requires no further
    config" trap: student answered False correctly and instantly (first correct instant
    T/F call on this exact question-shape after three prior sessions — Session 4, 7, and 8
    — all showing a "correct idea, hesitant/wrong first retrieval" pattern on similarly
    shaped questions; SEE STUDENT LEARNING OBSERVATIONS, this is a meaningfully tracked
    milestone). However the justification was initially incomplete: named "policies" but
    initially proposed "matching IP addressing on both link interfaces" as the second
    requirement (incorrect — VDOM link 0/1 interfaces auto-generate their own addressing at
    creation, this is not something the admin configures) rather than "a route on each
    side." Corrected in two guided steps: first redirected toward "what table does a
    FortiGate consult before sending traffic toward any destination," landed cleanly on
    "the routing table" once framed generically (not VDOM-specific) rather than being told
    directly. Final answer: each VDOM has its OWN independent routing table AND policy
    table; inter-VDOM traffic requires a route AND a policy, configured independently, on
    BOTH sides of the link.
Q7 — STRETCH/SYNTHESIS QUESTION combining Q4 + VLAN mechanics: could Parramatta's local
    VLAN 10 simply be added into HQ's existing Trading VDOM to save setup effort? Required
    the most guidance of the day (three redirects) but never produced a wrong mechanism —
    student's first answer reused the WAN-latency argument from Q4 correctly but incomp-
    letely (true but not the deeper structural reason); second answer correctly identified
    that a raw WAN link doesn't transparently carry dot1q trunk tags (true, relevant, but
    still not the root cause); third answer, once explicitly told to combine "a VDOM only
    sees traffic on ITS OWN chassis's physical interfaces" with "a VLAN tag only has meaning
    on the physical trunk it's declared on," self-generated the full correct synthesis
    unprompted, including correctly naming that Parramatta would need its own firewall
    presence plus a tunnel (a term the student supplied himself, ahead of the curriculum,
    correctly flagging it as "Session 10's territory" without needing to be told) — good
    instinct to recognize the edge of current knowledge rather than bluffing past it.

TROUBLESHOOTING APPROACH TAUGHT
----------------------------------
Not a live-fault session. The METHODOLOGICAL lesson taught (via Q4 and Q7) generalizes
directly to troubleshooting: before evaluating whether a proposed logical solution (a VDOM,
a VLAN membership change) will work, first establish WHERE the physical hardware actually
sits and what physical/Layer-2 reachability actually exists — logical elegance does not
override physical topology. This is presented explicitly as a senior-engineering habit
("check geography before evaluating the logic") and should be tracked as a design-reasoning
principle, not just a fact about VDOMs specifically.

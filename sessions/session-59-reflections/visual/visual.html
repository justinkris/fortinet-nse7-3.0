Visual Learning Page · NSE7 EF 7.6

The Room With Three Drawers

A memory palace for the RIBs, and a journey for the route that travels through them.

Recommended style: Memory Palace + Packet Journey
Why: the three RIBs are already spatial (Señor's filing cabinet), so a palace anchors them permanently — and the RR relay rules are best remembered as a route physically walking from drawer to drawer, being stopped at gates. The mesh-to-star collapse is added as a single before/after image because it is the session's emotional and business anchor.
Why This Style

Choosing The Visual Approach

Three candidate styles were considered for this session:

StyleFitVerdict
Memory PalaceSeñor's three-drawer filing cabinet is already a spatial metaphor the session established organically. A palace makes it permanent.Selected as primary
Packet JourneyThe relay rules are conditional on where a route came from — best learned by following one route through checkpoints.Selected as secondary
Enterprise TopologyNeeded once, for the mesh-to-star collapse. Not enough on its own — a topology diagram cannot show why the mesh existed.Used for one image only
Decision TreeAlready covered thoroughly by the troubleshooting guide. Duplicating it here adds nothing.Rejected
Image 01 · The Anchor

The Whiteboard That Ran Out

This is the session's emotional anchor and should be the first thing recalled. It carries the business case without a single technical label.

s59-img-01-whiteboard-ran-out.png
Junior, a chair, eleven feet of whiteboard, and a physical impossibility.
IMAGE GENERATION PROMPT — 16:9

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork on a warm cream off-white background (#faf5e9), with clean bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D. Muted palette: coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage green, deep navy ink.

Wide interior scene of a modern office corridor outside a glass-walled meeting room, early morning light from tall windows on the left casting long soft shadows. Dominating the composition: an enormous whiteboard wall spanning the full width of the frame. On it are nine hand-drawn circles labelled in neat capitals — SYDNEY, MELBOURNE, BRISBANE, PERTH, AUCKLAND, SINGAPORE, LONDON — arranged around the edges, with ADELAIDE crammed at an awkward diagonal in the bottom-right corner where the artist clearly ran out of room. Between the circles: an overwhelming tangle of straight marker lines connecting every circle to every other circle. The centre third of the whiteboard has degraded into an unreadable muddy grey-brown smear where the lines have crossed each other too many times.

Foreground centre-right: a young man in his early twenties (Junior) with slightly messy dark hair, an untucked shirt, standing precariously on a rolling office chair, one arm outstretched toward the board holding nothing — his hand is empty and open. A black whiteboard marker is captured mid-fall in the air just below his hand, with a small motion arc behind it, about to hit the carpet. His expression is not comic panic but flat, deflated realisation. A single muffin sits forgotten on a side table.

Left foreground, seated at a small table: a neat man in his twenties in a pressed shirt (J.R.) with an open notebook. Visible on the page, written three times and underlined each time, the number 28, and below it 36. His pen is paused.

Text elements: only the city names on the circles and the numbers 28 and 36 in the notebook. No other labels, no captions, no network iconography, no router symbols.

Mood: quiet defeat, absurdity treated seriously, early-morning stillness. Visual hierarchy: the grey smear at the centre of the whiteboard is the true subject; Junior is secondary; J.R.'s notebook is a small third focal point.

MUST NOT APPEAR: any firewall or router hardware, any Fortinet branding, any cables or network cabinets, any speech bubbles, any cowboy hats, any text explaining the concept.

LEARNING PURPOSE: anchor the full-mesh scaling problem to a physical, absurd, memorable failure — the design was unsustainable long before it was technically wrong.

Image 02 · The Memory Palace

The Room With Three Drawers

This is the palace. Every future BGP question should be answered by first walking into this room and deciding which drawer you are standing in front of.

s59-img-02-three-drawers-palace.png
RIB-in, Local RIB, RIB-out — three drawers, two guarded gates, one filing room.
IMAGE GENERATION PROMPT — 16:9

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork, warm cream background (#faf5e9), clean bold soft-brown line work, no gradients, no photorealism, no 3D rendering. Palette: deep navy ink, coral accents, muted gold, sage green.

A cutaway cross-section view of a single long narrow archive room, seen straight-on from the side so the viewer sees the whole room at once, like a theatre stage set or a doll's house. The room is divided left to right into three distinct chambers by two internal doorways.

LEFT CHAMBER: cluttered and chaotic. A wide-open filing drawer overflowing with loose paper slips, some spilling onto the floor, unsorted, some crumpled. A letterbox slot in the far-left wall with more slips tumbling through it. A small engraved brass plaque above the drawer reads RIB-IN. A smaller hand-lettered card beneath reads "everything I was told".

FIRST DOORWAY (between left and middle): a narrow arch with TWO stacked gates, drawn as two separate turnstile barriers one after the other. The first barrier has a small sign reading IN FILTER. The second barrier, immediately behind it, has a sign reading VALIDITY + BEST. Beside the second barrier, a discarded pile of rejected slips on the floor with a small tag reading "next hop unreachable".

MIDDLE CHAMBER: calm and orderly. A single tidy filing drawer, half-open, containing neatly aligned upright cards, each with a small coral tick mark. Brass plaque reads LOCAL RIB. Hand-lettered card beneath reads "what I chose to believe". A narrow chute enters this chamber from a hatch in the ceiling, with a few slips sliding down it; the hatch is labelled REDISTRIBUTE and shows tiny icons of a plug, a road sign, and a compass rose to suggest connected, static, and IGP routes.

SECOND DOORWAY (between middle and right): a single arch with ONE turnstile barrier, but this barrier is noticeably larger, heavier and more ornate than the others, with a brass sign reading OUT FILTER. Hanging from the barrier on small tags, like keys on a rack, are four labels: SPLIT HORIZON, RR RULES, NEXT-HOP-SELF, PREFIX-LIST-OUT.

RIGHT CHAMBER: sparse and deliberate. A drawer containing only a few cards, standing upright and evenly spaced. Brass plaque reads RIB-OUT. Hand-lettered card beneath reads "what I am willing to say". An outbound letterbox slot in the far-right wall, with two or three slips leaving through it.

Standing in the middle chamber, small in scale, facing the heavy OUT FILTER gate with his back three-quarters to the viewer: an older man wearing a large decorated sombrero (Señor), holding a folded worn sheet of paper. He is not gesturing; he is simply looking at the heavy gate. His posture is contemplative.

Text elements permitted: RIB-IN, LOCAL RIB, RIB-OUT, IN FILTER, VALIDITY + BEST, OUT FILTER, REDISTRIBUTE, the four hanging tags, and the three small hand-lettered descriptive cards. No other text.

Composition: strictly horizontal, left-to-right reading order, the three chambers roughly equal in width. Visual weight deliberately increases toward the right-hand gate to signal that it is the important one. Lighting is even and flat throughout.

MUST NOT APPEAR: any computer screens, any network hardware, any arrows drawn as flowchart arrows, any packet or envelope iconography, any modern office furniture, any Fortinet branding.

LEARNING PURPOSE: install a permanent spatial memory of the three RIBs and, critically, make the Local RIB → RIB-out gate visually the heaviest, because that is where almost all BGP policy lives.
Image 03 · The Journey

One Route, Four Checkpoints

Walk a single prefix from Melbourne to Adelaide and watch what stops it, where, and why. The final checkpoint — the client's silence — is the one most people forget.

s59-img-03-route-journey-checkpoints.png
10.99.60.0/24 travels from a client, through the reflector, to another client — and then stops.
IMAGE GENERATION PROMPT — 16:9

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork, warm cream background (#faf5e9), bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, no 3D. Palette: deep navy, coral, muted gold, sage green.

A horizontal journey illustration styled like a vintage travel map or a border-crossing sequence, read left to right, occupying the full width. A single small coral-coloured paper card — the travelling character of the scene, drawn with tiny stylised legs to suggest walking — carries a label reading 10.99.60.0/24. This same card appears five times across the image at different stages of its journey, showing progression.

STAGE 1 (far left): a small sage-green building labelled MELBOURNE with a tag beneath reading CLIENT. The card is emerging from its door. A small gold sign beside the door reads "originated locally — free to leave".

STAGE 2: a border checkpoint booth, modest in size, labelled HQ · RIB-IN. The card passes through easily; a small stamp mark in coral appears on it. Sign reads "valid → believed".

STAGE 3 (visually the largest and most elaborate structure, centre of the image): a grand crossing station labelled HQ · RIB-OUT, with a broad arch and a tall official-looking gate that is standing WIDE OPEN. Above the arch, a carved banner reads LEARNED FROM A CLIENT → REFLECT TO EVERYONE. Beside the gate, a uniformed but friendly official (no face detail needed, simple flat character, wearing a small peaked cap) is pressing two rubber stamps onto the card. The two stamps are clearly visible as they land: one reads ORIGINATOR_ID = MEL, the other reads CLUSTER_LIST. Three roads fan out from the far side of this arch, labelled TO OTHER CLIENTS, TO NON-CLIENTS, TO EBGP — the card takes the first.

STAGE 4: a small checkpoint labelled ADELAIDE · RIB-IN. A flat inspection desk where a magnifying glass hovers over the card's ORIGINATOR_ID stamp. A small green tick and a sign reading "not me → accept". Behind it, a small sage-green building labelled ADELAIDE with tag CLIENT, and the card entering a drawer inside it.

STAGE 5 (far right): the exit side of the Adelaide building. Here the road simply STOPS at a solid brick wall drawn in muted brown, with no gate at all. A single hanging sign on the wall reads RIB-OUT: EMPTY. Below it, smaller: "clients are mute". The coral card is shown on the Adelaide side of the wall, sitting still, with no legs drawn — it has stopped walking.

Along the very bottom of the image, a thin horizontal ribbon banner in muted gold with the compressed rule in small clean capitals: DOWNWARD ALWAYS · SIDEWAYS NEVER · CLIENTS SAY NOTHING.

Text elements permitted: only the labels described above. No paragraphs, no explanation text.

Composition: strict left-to-right, with stage 3 clearly the visual climax (largest, most detailed, most ornate) and stage 5 deliberately anticlimactic — a plain wall where the viewer expects another gate. That anticlimax IS the lesson.

MUST NOT APPEAR: routers, switches, cables, screens, Fortinet branding, human characters other than the single flat checkpoint official, any cowboy or sombrero hats.

LEARNING PURPOSE: make the three relay cases and the client's muteness kinaesthetic rather than memorised. The brick wall at the end is the single most-forgotten fact in the topic; giving it a physical form fixes it.
Image 04 · The Collapse

Mesh Becomes Star

The before-and-after that Penny takes to the board. This is the only image in the set that is a genuine topology diagram, and it earns its place because the arithmetic is the business case.

s59-img-04-mesh-to-star.png
36 conversations, or 9. The same nine sites, the same routes, a different rule.
IMAGE GENERATION PROMPT — 16:9

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork, warm cream background (#faf5e9), bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, no 3D rendering. Palette: deep navy ink, coral accents, muted gold, sage green.

A single image split vertically into two equal panels by a thin vertical muted-gold rule down the centre.

LEFT PANEL, header text in clean small capitals at the top: FULL MESH · 36 SESSIONS. Nine small identical sage-green building icons arranged evenly in a circle, each labelled in tiny capitals: SYDNEY, MELBOURNE, BRISBANE, PERTH, AUCKLAND, SINGAPORE, LONDON, ADELAIDE, and one labelled DR. Every building is connected to every other building by a thin navy line, producing a dense unreadable web across the centre of the circle. The lines should be drawn accurately as a complete graph, dense enough that the centre reads as visual noise. Overlaid faintly in the centre, in large muted-gold outline type, the number 36. At the bottom of the panel, a small coral tag reading "adding one site = touching nine routers".

RIGHT PANEL, header text in clean small capitals at the top: ROUTE REFLECTOR · 9 SESSIONS. The same nine building icons in the same circular positions — but the SYDNEY building is now drawn larger, in muted gold rather than sage green, elevated slightly, and wears a small stylised crown or beacon on its roof, with a small plaque beneath reading RR. Only nine lines exist: one clean coral line from each of the eight outer buildings to the central SYDNEY building. Each outer building has a tiny tag reading CLIENT. The centre of the circle is now open, calm, and empty except for the number 9 in large muted-gold outline type. At the bottom of the panel, a small sage-green tag reading "adding one site = touching two routers".

Across the very bottom of the whole image, spanning both panels, a thin ribbon banner in navy with cream text reading: ONLY THE CENTRE IS ALLOWED TO SPEAK.

Text elements permitted: the two panel headers, the nine city labels (repeated in both panels), the numbers 36 and 9, the RR plaque, the CLIENT tags, the two small bottom tags, and the ribbon banner. No other text.

Composition: perfect symmetry between panels so the eye compares density directly. The left panel must feel oppressive and cluttered; the right must feel calm and spacious. This contrast is the entire point of the image.

MUST NOT APPEAR: any hardware imagery, any people, any Fortinet branding, any arrows or directional markers on the lines, any legend or key.

LEARNING PURPOSE: make the scaling argument instantaneous and pre-verbal. The viewer should feel the difference before reading a single number, and should associate the calm right-hand panel with the phrase "only the centre is allowed to speak".
Image 05 · The Trade

Impossible Becomes Merely Caught

The conceptual heart of the session, and the transferable lesson: relax a structural guarantee and you must install a detector in its place.

s59-img-05-nested-rings.png
Three rings, one question: did this come from me?
IMAGE GENERATION PROMPT — 3:2

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork, warm cream background (#faf5e9), bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, no 3D. Palette: deep navy, coral, muted gold, sage green.

Composition split into two unequal halves by a soft vertical division: LEFT one-third and RIGHT two-thirds.

LEFT ONE-THIRD, headed with small capitals BEFORE: a solid, heavy, unbroken stone wall drawn in muted brown, spanning floor to ceiling, with no door and no gap. In front of it, a single coral paper card with tiny legs has walked up to the wall and stopped. A small carved sign set into the wall reads ONE HOP ONLY. Below the panel, a small caption in navy small capitals: LOOPS WERE IMPOSSIBLE.

RIGHT TWO-THIRDS, headed with small capitals AFTER: the same wall is now shown demolished — drawn as a broken-off stub of wall at each edge with rubble neatly stacked to one side, deliberately tidy rather than chaotic, to signal a deliberate decision rather than an accident. In the wide gap where the wall stood, three concentric circular archways stand one behind the other, receding into depth like nested doorframes, each one smaller than the last.

The OUTERMOST arch is largest, drawn in navy, with a banner across its top reading AS_PATH and a small carved sub-label beneath reading IS MY AS IN IT?
The MIDDLE arch is drawn in muted gold, banner reading CLUSTER_LIST, sub-label IS MY CLUSTER-ID IN IT?
The INNERMOST and smallest arch is drawn in coral, banner reading ORIGINATOR_ID, sub-label IS MY ROUTER-ID IT?

A single coral paper card with tiny legs is shown walking through the outer arch. A second identical card is shown at the middle arch being turned back by a small flat sentry figure with a raised hand, with a small puff of motion lines. A third card lies discarded on the ground just past the inner arch, face down, with a tiny tag beside it reading "dropped silently — no log".

Below the three arches, spanning the right panel, a caption ribbon in navy with cream small capitals: LOOPS ARE NOW MERELY CAUGHT.

In the lower right corner, small and unobtrusive, a hand-lettered note on a scrap of paper reading: "same question at every scale".

Text elements permitted: BEFORE, AFTER, ONE HOP ONLY, the three arch banners and their three sub-labels, the two captions, the discarded-card tag, and the corner note. No other text.

Composition: the demolished wall must read as intentional demolition, not damage. The three arches must be clearly nested and clearly decreasing in scale, with the smallest and most colourful (coral) innermost, so scope maps to size.

MUST NOT APPEAR: any network hardware, any people other than the single flat sentry figure, any Fortinet branding, any explosion or violence imagery, any router icons.

LEARNING PURPOSE: encode the ORIGINATOR_ID versus CLUSTER_LIST scope distinction as a size relationship (router is smaller than cluster is smaller than AS), which is the exact discrimination that costs marks in the exam — and carry the transferable principle that relaxing a structural guarantee obliges you to install a detector.
Image 06 · The Direction

Only One Device Knows

The single most-inverted configuration fact in the topic, given a visual form that makes the inversion feel wrong.

s59-img-06-who-holds-the-list.png
The reflector holds the list. The client is not told, and does not know.
IMAGE GENERATION PROMPT — 4:3

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork, warm cream background (#faf5e9), bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, no 3D. Palette: deep navy, coral, muted gold, sage green.

A quiet two-character scene, medium shot, in a tidy server room with immaculate framed network diagrams on the wall behind.

LEFT: a large muted-gold building-shaped character or podium representing the route reflector, anthropomorphised only slightly — a stylised gold building with a small crown motif on its roof, standing behind a lectern. On the lectern, clearly visible and open, is a leather-bound guest list or ledger. Three names are legibly written in the ledger in a neat hand: MELBOURNE, BRISBANE, ADELAIDE. A small brass plate on the lectern reads THE REFLECTOR DECIDES WHO IS A CLIENT.

RIGHT: a smaller sage-green building-shaped character representing a client, standing simply, holding a single blank sheet of paper in front of it. The sheet is completely empty apart from two short lines of small text, which read: "remote-as 65001" and nothing else. Above this character floats a small thought bubble containing only a question mark. A small tag at its base reads CLIENT — but the tag is attached by a string coming from the LEFT character's lectern, visibly tied there, not attached by the client itself. This detail matters: the label comes from the reflector.

Between the two characters, a one-way arrow in coral pointing from the lectern to the client, with a small label reading DECLARED.

Across the bottom, a navy ribbon with cream small capitals: THE CLIENT DOES NOT KNOW IT IS A CLIENT.

In the lower-left foreground, resting against the lectern and slightly out of the way, a large decorated sombrero hangs on a hook — Señor is not in frame, but he has been here.

Text elements permitted: the three names in the ledger, the brass plate text, the two config lines on the client's sheet, the DECLARED arrow label, the CLIENT tag, and the bottom ribbon. No other text.

Composition: strongly asymmetric — the reflector side should feel weighty, documented, and authoritative; the client side should feel light, blank, and unaware. The string tying the CLIENT tag back to the lectern must be clearly visible, as it is the single most important detail in the image.

MUST NOT APPEAR: any people, any Fortinet branding, any cables in disarray, any cowboy hat, any screens showing output.

LEARNING PURPOSE: fix the configuration direction permanently. Learners who invert this waste hours searching the client for a line that will never exist. The string tying the label back to the reflector makes the correct direction feel structurally obvious.
Recall Sequence

Walking The Palace From Memory

Use this order. It takes about forty seconds once learned and reconstructs the entire session.

  1. The whiteboard. Junior on a chair, grey smear, marker falling. → The mesh does not scale, and the cost is operational.
  2. Walk into the room. Three chambers. Cluttered, calm, sparse. → RIB-in, Local RIB, RIB-out.
  3. Look at the two gates. Two barriers on the left doorway, one heavy ornate barrier on the right. → Filter and validity; then policy. The heavy one is where everything lives.
  4. Follow the card. Melbourne, stamped at HQ, arch wide open, two stamps applied, Adelaide accepts, and then a brick wall. → Relay rules, and clients are mute.
  5. See the demolished wall and three nested arches. Big to small: AS, cluster, router. → AS_PATH, CLUSTER_LIST, ORIGINATOR_ID.
  6. Look at the lectern. The ledger has three names; the client holds a blank page. → The RR declares clients; the client does not know.

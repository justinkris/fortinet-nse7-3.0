Story Recap

The Whiteboard Ran Out

Wednesday, 08:41. Junior arrived early — an event in itself — and spent ninety minutes drawing the full-mesh IBGP topology for nine sites. Sydney. Melbourne. Brisbane. Perth. Auckland. Singapore. London. And Adelaide, squeezed in at a diagonal in the bottom-right corner because he had run out of room.

By the end, the middle third of the eleven-foot board was a grey smear where the marker had crossed itself too many times to stay readable. Standing on a chair, trying to draw the last line — Adelaide to Auckland — he found there was nowhere to put it. The marker slipped and hit the carpet.

"I ran out of board," he said. To nobody.

J.R. had written 28 three times in three places in his notebook, each underlined, as though hoping it would be smaller on the next attempt. He recalculated for nine sites: 36. He always does the arithmetic twice.

Penny arrived carrying her laptop with Señor's yellow sticky note still stuck to the lid. Adelaide is four people and a printer. The quoted change window was eleven hours, touching every firewall LoFinance owns. She needed a sentence for the board.

Riley, who did not know he was about to define the entire session, frowned into his coffee: "HQ already knows all of it. So why can't Sydney just tell Adelaide? Why does Adelaide have to hear it from Perth and Auckland and Brisbane personally?"

The speakerphone, live since 07:00 Singapore time and silent for forty minutes, made a small sound of someone leaning in. Javier Lin: "Because nobody told them they were allowed to listen to a reflection." Click.

Señor pulled a worn, furry-creased sheet from the filing cabinet nobody uses and flattened it with the side of his hand. Three boxes, left to right, arrows between them. RIB-in. Local RIB. RIB-out.

"For two sessions we have been asking what BGP knows. Wrong question. Today we ask where it keeps it. Because Riley has just asked why Sydney cannot repeat itself, and the answer is not in the wire. It is in the drawers."

Concept 01

Why The Full Mesh Existed

The mesh is not a limitation of BGP. It is the bill for a safety rule, and you cannot understand the fix without first respecting what the rule buys.

The rule

IBGP split horizon: a prefix learned from an IBGP peer is never advertised to any other IBGP peer.

Note the precision. This is not the RIP version ("don't advertise back out the interface you learned it on") and it is not "don't send it back to whoever gave it to you." It is scoped by peer type, not by interface or by sender. Learned from any IBGP peer → goes to no IBGP peer at all.

Why the rule exists

AS_PATH is only prepended when a route leaves an AS. Inside AS 65001, therefore, the AS_PATH loop check is structurally blind — every router sees the same AS_PATH, and none of them see themselves in it. Inside the AS, BGP has no map, no hop count, no hold-down timers, and no functioning AS_PATH check. Every loop-prevention tool it owns is inoperative.

Split horizon is the replacement, and it is a strong one: a route can take exactly one IBGP hop, ever. You cannot build a circle out of segments of length one. Loops are made structurally impossible, not merely detected after the fact.

The bill

If nobody may relay, then everybody must hear everything first-hand. Every IBGP speaker needs a session to every other IBGP speaker.

The Number Is Not The Point
36 is survivable. What is not survivable is that adding one branch requires configuring every existing router. A four-person office turn-up becomes a fleet-wide change window. The cost is operational, not computational.
Watch the plane. These lines are not tunnels and this is not a latency problem. Each line is a TCP-179 peering relationship — a control-plane conversation. Traffic flow is a separate question entirely. Confusing the two is the commonest way to answer this wrongly under exam pressure.
Concept 02 · pp. 340–341

Three Drawers, Three Gates

A BGP router does not hold one bucket of routes. It holds three logical tables, and a route moves left to right through them. At each boundary something can stop it, change it, or refuse to pass it on.

RIB-in
Routing information learned from inbound update messages. Unprocessed. Everything my peers told me, before any decision.
IN filter
+ validity
+ best-path
→
Local RIB
What the speaker selects after applying local policy. What I chose to believe. Feeds the routing table.
OUT filter
→
RIB-out
What the speaker selects to advertise to its peers. What I am willing to say — and to whom.

Gate one: RIB-in → Local RIB

Two things guard this boundary, and it is worth holding them separately:

  • The IN filter. Your local policy — prefix-list-in, distribute-list-in, route-map-in. A route can be perfectly valid and still be dropped here because you decided to reject it.
  • Validity and best-path. Of the routes that survive the filter: does the NEXT_HOP resolve? And if several peers advertised the same prefix, which one wins? Only the winner enters the Local RIB.
This is where Session 58's fault lived. Melbourne's Singapore prefix sat in RIB-in showing *i with no > — received, unfiltered, but its next hop (Javier's loopback, 172.20.0.1) was unresolvable. Not valid, therefore not best, therefore never installed. Valid but not best on a single-path prefix means it is not losing a competition — it is ineligible.

Gate two: Local RIB → RIB-out

The Local RIB is what I believe. The RIB-out is what I am prepared to say. They are not automatically the same set, and the gate between them is where almost all of BGP policy actually lives.

ToolQuestion it answers
IBGP split horizonMay I relay this at all, given how I learned it?
RR relay rulesGiven how I learned it and who is asking, may I reflect it?
next-hop-selfDo I rewrite the NEXT_HOP before I say it?
prefix-list-out / route-map-outWhich of my believed prefixes am I willing to mention?
💭
Mental Note
Believing and telling are different drawers. Almost every BGP policy control is the door between them. When you diagnose, name the drawer before you name the cause.

Where redistribution enters

Routes do not only arrive from peers. Connected routes, statics, and prefixes from RIP, OSPF and IS-IS are redistributed into the process after the Local RIB, before the outbound filter. They join the outbound stream at that point and are then subject to the same OUT filter as everything else.

Concept 03 · pp. 338–339

The Reflector Breaks The Rule On Purpose

Here is the move that makes route reflectors comprehensible rather than arbitrary: an RR is not a new mechanism. It is the deliberate removal of a safety rule.

Watch what an RR does for a living. It learns a route from Melbourne, an IBGP peer. It then advertises that route to Brisbane, also an IBGP peer. That is precisely, exactly, the thing split horizon forbids. The RR is authorised to violate the rule that was holding the AS together.

Javier Lin
"Because nobody told them they were allowed to listen to a reflection." — and reflections, of course, are exactly what loops are made of.

Clusters

The AS is divided into clusters. Each cluster is one RR plus its clients. Clients communicate route updates only with the RR in their cluster. The RR communicates with everything outside the cluster — other RRs, border routers, EBGP peers.

FortiGate can be configured as either an RR or a client. The topology collapses from a mesh to a star:

Model9 sitesAdding site 10Routers touched
Full mesh36 sessions+9 sessionsAll 10
Single RR9 sessions+1 session2
Penny's sentence: "Adding an office no longer means re-opening every office." Adelaide costs two routers and a coffee.

A useful comparison — and its limit

If OSPF's designated router came to mind, that instinct is sound: both are concentrators that everyone talks to so that nobody has to talk to each other, and both exist to kill an n2 adjacency problem.

But hold the analogy loosely. A DR does not relax loop prevention — OSPF's loop-freedom comes from every router computing SPF over an identical database, and the DR does not touch that. An RR does relax loop prevention, which is why it needs new attributes that a DR never required. Same shape of solution; a materially more dangerous trade.
Concept 04

What Replaced Impossibility

You have just deleted the mechanism that made loops structurally impossible. Something must replace it — and what replaces impossibility is always detection.

AttributeSet byContainsBehaviour on match
ORIGINATOR_IDThe RR, on reflectionRouter-ID of the client that originally injected the routeA router seeing its own router-ID drops the route — silently, no log
CLUSTER_LISTEach reflecting RRCluster-ID prepended at each reflectionAn RR seeing its own cluster-ID drops the route

The nested rings

Both attributes are AS_PATH's idea, scaled down. BGP solved the intra-AS relaying problem by importing its own inter-AS loop check twice, at two smaller scopes:

ScopeMechanismTest
Between autonomous systemsAS_PATHIs my AS in it?
Between clustersCLUSTER_LISTIs my cluster-ID in it?
Within a clusterORIGINATOR_IDIs my router-ID it?
⚡
Senior Insight
Same question at every scale: did this come from me? If you can hold that one sentence, you can reconstruct all three mechanisms from scratch under exam pressure.
Distinguish them by scope, not by memory. If the question says "the router that originated / injected the route" — that is a single router, identified by router-ID: ORIGINATOR_ID. If it says "the reflecting RR", "between clusters", or "multiple reflectors" — that is CLUSTER_LIST. Read the scope word in the stem and match it.
In a single-RR topology, CLUSTER_LIST is rarely the thing that saves you — plain split horizon on the mute clients stops the route long before it is consulted. CLUSTER_LIST earns its keep in multi-RR designs, where a route could genuinely circulate between clusters.
Concept 05

Three Cases, One Rule

An RR does not reflect indiscriminately. What it does next depends entirely on who it heard the route from.

Learned fromAdvertised toReasoning
A clientEveryone: other clients, non-client IBGP peers, EBGP peersThe client is mute — ordinary split horizon binds it fully. If the RR does not relay this, nobody hears it and the cluster is invisible.
A non-client IBGP peerClients onlyClients peer with nobody but their RR; without this the cluster would be a sealed box learning nothing from outside. But other non-clients are ordinary IBGP peers, so the original rule still binds sideways.
An EBGP peerEveryoneIt was never an IBGP-learned route. Split horizon never applied to it in the first place.
Compress it: Downward (to clients) — always. Sideways (non-client to non-client) — never.
Señor's version
"The reflector speaks freely to its own. To strangers, it only repeats what its own told it."

And the client does nothing at all

This is the piece most often missed. A client that receives a reflected route believes it, installs it, and tells nobody. It has no RR role; plain split horizon binds it completely. Its RIB-out toward any other IBGP peer is empty for that prefix.

💭
The Elegance
The mesh collapses because only the centre is allowed to speak. The clients are deliberately, permanently mute — and that muteness is exactly what makes the star safe.
Configuration

One Command, One Device

HQ-FGT — the route reflector
config router bgp
    set as 65001
    set router-id 10.99.10.1
    config neighbor
        edit "10.99.60.1"                       # MEL-FGT
            set remote-as 65001
            set next-hop-self enable
            set route-reflector-client enable
            set description "MEL-DR (RR client)"
        next
        edit "10.99.70.1"                       # BNE-FGT
            set remote-as 65001
            set next-hop-self enable
            set route-reflector-client enable
            set description "BNE branch (RR client)"
        next
        edit "10.99.80.1"                       # ADL-FGT  <-- the new site
            set remote-as 65001
            set next-hop-self enable
            set route-reflector-client enable
            set description "ADL branch (RR client)"
        next
    end
end
ADL-FGT — the client. Note what is NOT here.
config router bgp
    set as 65001
    set router-id 10.99.80.1

Reading the shape of the command

The direction of route-reflector-client catches almost everybody once. The cure is structural, and it generalises across the whole config neighbor tree:

Everything inside config neighbor / edit <ip> describes the peer at that address, not the box you are typing on. set remote-as 65001 does not mean "I am in AS 65001" — it means that neighbour is. set next-hop-self enable does not mean the neighbour rewrites anything — it means I will, when I talk to that neighbour. So set route-reflector-client enable under 10.99.60.1 is a statement about 10.99.60.1: that neighbour is a client of mine. Only a router with clients can say that.
The client is not configured and does not know. The study guide is explicit: the RR defines which neighbors are the clients. Nothing on ADL-FGT indicates it is a client. If you go looking for the RR relationship on the client, you will find nothing and conclude, wrongly, that it is missing.

next-hop-self is still required

Reflection and next-hop resolution are different drawers. Reflecting a route perfectly does nothing about whether the receiving router can resolve its NEXT_HOP. On an RR that is also the border router, next-hop-self toward clients is almost always necessary — and the trade-off from Session 58 still stands: it removes traffic-engineering options and concentrates the failure domain on the reflector.

Troubleshooting Workflow

Name The Drawer First

  1. Restate the symptom in business terms. "Adelaide cannot reach Singapore" — not "BGP is broken."
  2. Is the prefix present at the client at all? get router info bgp network. Absent and present are completely different investigations from here on.
  3. Absent → check the RR's RIB-out, not the client's RIB-in. get router info bgp neighbors <client> advertised-routes. If the RR is not saying it, nothing at the client will ever explain why.
  4. RR not advertising → is route-reflector-client enable set on the RR under that neighbour? Nothing on the client will tell you. Check the RR.
  5. RR is advertising but the client has nothing → suspect a silent drop. ORIGINATOR_ID matching the client's own router-ID, or CLUSTER_LIST matching in a multi-RR design. No log, no counter, no alarm.
  6. Present but not installed → read the status codes. * without > on a single path means ineligible, not outvoted. Read the NEXT_HOP column and test resolution.
  7. Installed but traffic still fails → you are in the wrong drawer entirely. Go to routing, policy, and return path.
  8. Verify recovery and root cause. Restoring service is not the same as knowing why it broke.
The counter-lesson, carried from Session 58 and doubly true here: session Established, prefixes present, no logs, no alarms, service broken. Absence of errors is not evidence of correctness. Route-reflector loop-prevention drops are completely silent by design.
Common Mistakes

Where People Lose Marks

MistakeCorrection
Configuring route-reflector-client on the clientIt goes on the RR, under each client's neighbour block. The client's config is ordinary.
Believing a client re-advertises reflected routes to other clientsThe client is mute. Only the RR relays.
Answering "nobody" for a route learned from a non-client IBGP peerClients still receive it — otherwise the cluster is a sealed box. Only the sideways direction is forbidden.
Confusing ORIGINATOR_ID with CLUSTER_LISTMatch the attribute to the scope word in the question. Originating router → ORIGINATOR_ID. Reflecting RR or between clusters → CLUSTER_LIST.
Quoting split horizon as "not back to the sender"It is "not to any other IBGP peer." The looser wording is a deliberate distractor.
Explaining full mesh with tunnels and latencyControl plane, not data plane. Each line is a TCP-179 peering, not a traffic path.
Assuming reflection solves next-hop resolutionDifferent drawers. next-hop-self is still usually required.
Reciting Session 58's next-hop fault when told the route is already in the Local RIBRead the state you were given before retrieving the fault you know best. Name the drawer first.
Senior Engineering Insights

What This Session Teaches Beyond BGP

  • Every safety rule has a price, and the price is usually scale. Split horizon buys structural loop-freedom and charges n(n−1)/2. Engineering is choosing which bill to pay, not pretending there is no bill.
  • When you relax a structural guarantee, you must replace it with a detection mechanism. Impossible becomes merely caught. That trade recurs constantly — in HA, in NAT, in caching, in anything that trades certainty for scale.
  • Believing and telling are different drawers. Most policy in most systems lives at the boundary between internal state and external assertion.
  • An individually correct configuration can be architecturally unsustainable. Junior drew a perfect full mesh. Nothing he drew was wrong. The board still ran out.
  • The evidence beats the pattern. Your strongest memories are your most dangerous distractors, because a familiar-looking symptom pulls you toward a fault you already know.
Exam Tips

What They Will Actually Ask

  • Placement of route-reflector-client. Near-certain. Answer: on the RR, per-neighbour. Distractors offer the client, both, and global.
  • Exact split-horizon wording. "Any other IBGP peer" versus "back to the peer that sent it" is a designed distractor pair.
  • n(n−1)/2 arithmetic. Derive by summation if the formula wobbles: for 9 sites, 8+7+6+5+4+3+2+1 = 36.
  • Which RIB is which. "After the inbound filter" = Local RIB. "Before any filtering" = RIB-in. "Selected to advertise" = RIB-out.
  • Relay direction from a non-client peer. Clients only. "Nobody" and "all IBGP peers" are both offered.
  • Attribute scope. Originating router = ORIGINATOR_ID. Reflecting RR / inter-cluster = CLUSTER_LIST.
  • Multi-select questions: run each option as an independent true/false. Do not pick a shape. Do not stop when you have found the number of options the stem asked for — test all of them.
Key Takeaways

Five Things To Carry Forward

  1. Received → believed → willing to say. Three drawers, and policy lives at the last door.
  2. Split horizon costs you the mesh. Nobody may relay, so everybody must hear it first-hand.
  3. A route reflector breaks that rule on purpose and pays for it with ORIGINATOR_ID and CLUSTER_LIST.
  4. Downward always, sideways never — and the client itself says nothing at all.
  5. The command describes the peer, not the box. That one reading habit resolves the entire config neighbor tree.

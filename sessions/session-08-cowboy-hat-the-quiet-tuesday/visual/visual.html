SESSION 8 · VISUAL LEARNING

Cowboy Hat: The Quiet Tuesday

Two failures, two shapes — a bridge that swallowed a boundary, and a ceiling nobody measured.

Recommended Visual Style for This Session

A blended Packet Journey + Troubleshooting Decision Tree approach fits best here. Both tickets are fundamentally about tracing a path (does the traffic actually reach the policy engine?) and both resolve at a specific decision fork the student had to find. A single static topology diagram wouldn't capture the "surprise" of either fault — the value is in seeing the packet's actual route versus its intended route, side by side.

VISUAL 1

The Bridge That Ate the Boundary

The core "aha" of Ticket 1 is spatial: two VLANs that look separate on a topology diagram, secretly fused into one room by a switch construct hiding underneath.

Two Rooms, One Hidden Door
Purpose: cement the mental model that a software switch bridge merges VLAN broadcast domains beneath the level where policy operates — the segmentation "looks" intact on paper but isn't.
Aspect: 16:9Placement: top of Ticket 1 section
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic vector-art illustration, cream/off-white background, coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage green, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework, no gradients, no photorealism, generous whitespace, storybook-diagram hybrid style. Compose two adjacent rectangular "rooms" side by side, labeled subtly with small tags reading "VLAN 10 — Finance" (left room, sage green walls, a simple desk with a document icon and a small lock icon) and "VLAN 30 — Support" (right room, coral walls, a simple desk with a headset icon). Both rooms appear to have solid walls between them at first glance. But drawn faintly BEHIND both rooms, at floor level, is a second, hidden connecting corridor — illustrated as a soft-glowing archway low on the shared wall, drawn in muted gold with a dashed outline to suggest it's invisible from the normal viewing angle, with a small label "OpsBridge" near it in tiny serif text. A single simple line-art figure (representing the support tech) is shown walking through this hidden corridor from the right room into the left room, unaware, while a stylized "policy guard" character (simple, friendly, geometric — a rounded rectangle body with a small shield icon) stands upright and alert at the FRONT door between the rooms (the normal, expected boundary), visibly not noticing anything because nothing ever knocked. Soft directional lighting from upper left, warm and calm despite the subject being a security gap — this should feel instructive, not alarming. No dense technical labels beyond the two room names and "OpsBridge." No FortiGate logos, no real brand marks.
Accessibility description: Two side-by-side rooms labeled Finance and Support, connected by an ordinary front door guarded by a shield character, but also connected by a hidden glowing archway at floor level labeled OpsBridge, through which a figure walks unnoticed.
Anchors: "the policy guard only watches the front door — a bridge digs underneath it."
VISUAL 2

Reading the Session-List Signature

The diagnostic moment itself — recognizing dev=13→13 and policy_id=0 together — deserves its own memory anchor, since it's the exact pattern the student needs to recognize instantly in future scenarios.

One Door, Two Names
Purpose: reinforce that identical ingress/egress interface index is the visual tell of Layer 2 switching versus Layer 3 routing.
Aspect: 3:2Placement: beside the diagnostic-signature explanation
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic vector illustration, same consistent palette as the rest of the set (cream background, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown outlines, no gradients, no 3D). Split the image into two clearly separated panels with a thin soft-brown divider line down the middle, each panel showing a simple parcel/packet icon (a small rounded rectangle with a paper-airplane fold, representing a network packet) approaching a single doorway icon. LEFT PANEL, labeled small in Outfit-style sans-serif "ROUTED": the packet enters through a door labeled with a small tag "9" (sage green) and exits through a DIFFERENT door labeled "11" (coral) on the far side, with a small friendly gate/checkpoint character (geometric, shield icon) standing between the two doors, actively stamping a small ticket held by the packet — the stamp icon shows a numeral like "12" to represent a policy ID. RIGHT PANEL, labeled "BRIDGED": the packet enters through a door labeled "13" and the SAME door, labeled "13" again, appears immediately on the other side with no gap and no checkpoint character present at all — just empty space where the checkpoint would be, drawn with a faint dotted outline to suggest "expected but absent." Use directional motion lines (short soft-gold streaks) to show the packet moving through in both panels. Keep typography minimal — only the door numbers and the two panel labels. No FortiGate branding, no photorealistic elements.
Accessibility description: Two side-by-side panels comparing a packet passing through two different numbered doors with a checkpoint character stamping it (routed), versus a packet passing through the same numbered door twice with no checkpoint present (bridged).
Anchors: "different door in, different door out, someone stamps you — that's routing. Same door twice, nobody's there — that's a bridge."
VISUAL 3

The Ceiling Nobody Measured

Ticket 2's fault is invisible in every config check — the visual needs to show a limit that exists but was never looked at, and the moment it finally gets hit.

The Room That Filled Up Quietly
Purpose: illustrate resource-ceiling exhaustion as a distinct failure category from misconfiguration — everything "looks" fine until the room is physically full.
Aspect: 16:9Placement: top of Ticket 2 section
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic vector illustration, consistent palette (cream background, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism). Show a single simple room, labeled subtly "Guest-WiFi," drawn as a warm, welcoming space with a WiFi-signal icon on the wall. Inside, illustrate exactly 50 small identical rounded-square "session tokens" (simple geometric icons, sage green, arranged in a tidy grid filling the room almost completely, like seats in a small theater) — all 50 slots occupied. At the doorway, draw a small queue of 3-4 additional session tokens in coral (a different color to show they're being rejected) standing outside the door, unable to enter, with a simple "closed" bar/barrier icon at the threshold. Above the room, draw a small thought-bubble or echo shape connecting back to a tiny, identical miniature room labeled "root" with the same "50" numeral floating near it in muted gold, connected to the Guest-WiFi room by a faint dotted line, to visually suggest "this number was copied from somewhere else, not chosen on purpose." Keep the mood calm and instructive, not alarming — this is a measurement problem, not a disaster. Minimal text: only "Guest-WiFi," "root," and the numeral 50 in two places.
Accessibility description: A room fully occupied by 50 identical green session tokens with additional orange tokens queued outside a closed doorway, visually linked by a dotted line to a smaller room labeled root sharing the same number 50, suggesting the limit was copied rather than chosen.
Anchors: "the room wasn't broken — it was full, and the number on the door was somebody else's."
VISUAL 4

The Decision Path, End to End

A single memory anchor tying both tickets to one shared troubleshooting shape: trace the path, read the signature, then know when to stop checking config and start checking resources.

Two Roads, One Map
Purpose: reinforce the unified decision-tree logic taught this session as a single visual the student can recall under exam pressure.
Aspect: 16:9Placement: end of session, before the exam questions
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic vector illustration in the same consistent palette (cream, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, generous spacing, storybook-diagram hybrid). Illustrate a simple branching path/map from a bird's-eye perspective, starting at a single signpost icon labeled "Symptom" at the bottom of the image. The path splits into two forks going upward: the LEFT fork, colored coral, leads uphill toward a small illustrated bridge/archway icon labeled faintly "check the bridge" — representing the Layer 2 bypass investigation. The RIGHT fork, colored sage green, leads uphill toward a small illustrated gauge/dial icon showing a needle near its maximum, labeled faintly "check the ceiling" — representing the resource-limit investigation. Both forks converge again near the top of the image at a single checkmark icon inside a rounded badge, colored muted gold, representing "verified and fixed." Small, simple waypoint markers (dots) are placed along each path at roughly three points to suggest sequential steps without adding dense text. Keep all labeling extremely minimal — a handful of short phrases only, in a clean sans-serif hand-lettered style. The overall feel should be like a gentle trail map or treasure-map illustration, warm and inviting rather than technical or intimidating.
Accessibility description: A trail map branching from a single starting signpost into two paths — one leading to a bridge icon representing checking for Layer 2 bypass, one leading to a gauge icon representing checking resource ceilings — both converging at a checkmark badge at the top.
Anchors: "one symptom, two roads — bridge or ceiling — but both end at the same verified checkmark."

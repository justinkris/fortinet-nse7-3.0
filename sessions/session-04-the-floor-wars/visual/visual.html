SESSION 04 · VISUAL LEARNING · PACKET JOURNEY STYLE

The Floor Wars — Follow the Frame

Recommended style: Packet Journey. This session's core insight — "a VLAN tag is a property of the wire, not the packet" — is best taught by literally following one frame across every hop and watching its tag appear, disappear, and reappear.

Why Packet Journey over other styles: A static topology diagram would show VLAN 5 and VLAN 20 as boxes, but it can't show the moment-by-moment tag lifecycle that trips people up on the exam (ingress-strip vs. egress-add). Walking one frame stage-by-stage makes the "wire property, not packet property" idea physically visible.
1
Frame leaves an exec-floor laptop (untagged)

no VLAN tag The host doesn't know or care about VLANs — it just sends a normal Ethernet frame toward its default gateway.

images/s1-frame-origin.png
A clean, modern isometric illustration of a sleek laptop on a polished executive office desk, glowing warm amber light, sending a single glowing envelope-shaped packet of light toward a network cable disappearing off the edge of the desk. The envelope is plain white with no visible label or tag — emphasize its "blank, untagged" appearance with soft rim lighting. Background subtly shows floor-to-ceiling windows of a Sydney high-rise trading firm at dusk. Style: editorial tech-illustration, warm color palette (ambers, navy, cream), high detail, no text or logos.
2
Switch access port assigns VLAN 5

tag added: VLAN 5 The access port is the ONLY point in this journey where an untagged frame gets its first tag. FortiGate will never do this job.

images/s2-switch-tag.png
An isometric illustration of a physical network switch with a glowing amber luggage-tag labeled "VLAN 5" being mechanically stamped onto a passing envelope-shaped packet of light as it moves through a conveyor-belt-like port on the switch chassis. Small indicator LEDs glow along the switch's front. The tag has a small "5" printed on it in a clean sans-serif font, rendered as a physical hanging tag with string, like airport luggage tags. Style: editorial tech-illustration, warm ambers and deep navy, high detail, no readable logos, no additional text.
3
Frame crosses the dot1q trunk

carrying: VLAN 5 One physical cable, multiple VLANs multiplexed by tag. A VLAN 20 (trading floor) frame could be traveling the exact same wire right alongside it, distinguishable only by its tag.

images/s3-trunk-multiplex.png
A dramatic cutaway isometric illustration of a single thick fiber-optic cable (labeled subtly as "trunk") carrying two distinct streams of glowing light-envelopes side by side inside the same translucent cable sheath — one stream of envelopes tagged with small amber "5" luggage tags, another stream tagged with small blue "20" luggage tags, both traveling in parallel without touching or mixing. Style: editorial tech-illustration, glass/fiber-optic aesthetic, warm amber and cool blue contrast, deep navy background, high detail, no readable logos or extra text.
4
FortiGate strips the tag (reads, doesn't invent)

tag removed FortiGate reads "VLAN 5" and matches it to the VLAN5-Exec interface — but it never adds a tag to an untagged frame arriving here. That job was already done in Stage 2.

images/s4-fortigate-strip.png
An isometric illustration of a sleek dark-navy FortiGate firewall appliance with a glowing blue Fortinet-style shield emblem (generic, non-trademarked shield icon, no logo), shown as a security checkpoint booth. A glowing envelope-packet arrives at an intake slot labeled "VLAN 5," and a small mechanical arm gently removes its amber luggage tag, setting it aside on a tray, before the envelope proceeds further into the booth for inspection. Style: editorial tech-illustration, warm ambers and cool blues, deep navy palette, high detail, no readable text or logos.
5
Policy decision at the checkpoint

Now untagged and purely an IP packet, FortiGate checks the firewall policy: is traffic from VLAN5-Exec to VLAN20-Trading allowed? In our design: deny, logged.

images/s5-policy-checkpoint.png
An isometric illustration inside the FortiGate security checkpoint booth from the previous scene: a glowing rulebook/ledger floats open showing a simple ordered list of policy rules, and a stern but calm guard-like AI silhouette (abstract, non-human, geometric, glowing blue outline) is stamping a red "DENY" mark onto the untagged envelope-packet resting on a small pedestal. A subtle log/receipt printer beside it prints a ticket. Style: editorial tech-illustration, warm ambers and cool blues, deep navy palette, high detail, no readable logos, minimal abstract text only (no real words needed, use symbolic marks).
6
If allowed: FortiGate re-tags on egress

tag added: VLAN 20 For any traffic that IS permitted onward, FortiGate adds the destination VLAN's tag before sending the frame back out toward the trading floor switch — proving the tag belongs to the outbound wire, not the original packet.

images/s6-fortigate-retag.png
An isometric illustration mirroring Stage 4 but reversed: the same FortiGate checkpoint booth now has a mechanical arm attaching a NEW blue luggage tag labeled "20" onto the envelope-packet as it exits through an outbound slot toward a different trunk cable, visually distinct in color coding from the amber "5" tag seen earlier. Style: editorial tech-illustration, warm ambers and cool blues, deep navy palette, high detail, no readable logos or extra text.
7
Trading floor switch strips the final tag

tag removed for delivery The destination access port removes the VLAN 20 tag before the frame reaches its final untagged destination host — completing the round trip from untagged, to tagged twice, back to untagged.

images/s7-final-delivery.png
A wide isometric illustration of a busy 400-desk trading floor bathed in cool blue monitor glow, with a network switch at the edge of frame removing a blue "20" luggage tag from an arriving envelope-packet of light just before it reaches a trading terminal on a desk. Rows of trading monitors with abstract glowing candlestick charts recede into the background. Style: editorial tech-illustration, cool blue palette with warm accent lighting, high detail, no readable logos or extra text, evokes scale and energy of a large trading floor.
Memory hook: tag → strip → decide → (re-tag) → deliver. Say it out loud once. That's the entire mental model for every VLAN-crossing-FortiGate question you'll see on the exam.

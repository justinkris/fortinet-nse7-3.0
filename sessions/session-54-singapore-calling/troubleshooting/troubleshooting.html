SESSION 54 · TROUBLESHOOTING & DESIGN REASONING

How a Senior Engineer Thinks About BGP Boundaries

This session had no live fault — but the reasoning that catches design mistakes before they happen is the same muscle. Use this as a thinking framework for Session 55 onward.

The Question Behind the Symptom

The "symptom" this episode wasn't a broken link — it was a business decision (the Expansion) that silently created a wrong assumption: "we'll just extend our OSPF." Senior engineers catch design mistakes at the decision stage, before they become tickets.

  • Business trigger: new sites under different administration join the network
  • Wrong instinct to watch for: "just add them to the existing routing domain" (Junior's likely move, flagged for S55)
  • Right question: "who administers this network, and do we want to share our full topology with them?"

Questions to Ask Before Configuring Anything

1Is the remote network under the same administration as ours? If yes → IGP (OSPF) is fine. If no → BGP is required.
2Do we want to share our entire topology with them, or only specific reachability? If only specific → BGP, not OSPF, regardless of administration.
3Will we peer with the public internet eventually? If yes → plan for a registered public ASN later. If purely internal/inter-office → private ASN (64512–65534) now.
4Will we have more than one external BGP peer? If yes → we are multihomed. Ask explicitly: could routes from one peer leak to another?
5If leak risk exists → design prefix filters on every outbound advertisement BEFORE the session goes live, not after a leak is reported.

Common Dead Ends

Dead end: "AS_PATH will stop bad routes from leaking between peers." — AS_PATH only stops loops (your own ASN reappearing). It does nothing to stop a legitimate, loop-free route from one peer being re-advertised to another.
Dead end: "More peers automatically means more risk of becoming an ISP." — Multihoming alone doesn't create transit behaviour; the router will only forward routes it's advertising outward. The risk is in the OUTBOUND advertisement policy, not the peer count itself.
Dead end: "We should just pick any number for our ASN." — If there's any chance of ever touching the public internet without translation, an unregistered "made up" number outside the private range risks colliding with a real organisation's registered ASN.

Likely Root Causes (for later sessions)

When a BGP session or route-leak issue appears in a future troubleshooting scenario, these are the categories this session's fundamentals point toward:

  • Loop rejection unexpected: check whether the local AS number appears somewhere in the AS_PATH — likely a misconfigured private ASN reused across sites that should be distinct.
  • Unexpected transit / route leak: check outbound prefix-lists / route-maps on each neighbor — the AS_PATH will look completely valid; only filtering reveals the gap.
  • Peering won't establish (Session 55 preview): often a mismatch in configured AS numbers, not a Layer 3 reachability issue — always verify configured local-as and remote-as expectations first.

Exam Traps to Watch For

Trap: a question describing a multihomed AS with a route-leak symptom will offer "enable AS_PATH checking" as a distractor. The correct fix is filtering.
Correct instinct: when asked "what determines whether a route is accepted," always check BOTH the AS_PATH (loop) AND the configured inbound/outbound policy (filtering) — they are evaluated independently.

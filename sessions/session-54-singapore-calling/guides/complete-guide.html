CONCEPT 1

Why OSPF Stops at the Border

THINK FIRST
  • Why can't you simply add Javier's Singapore routers into your OSPF area?

OSPF is a link-state protocol: every router shares its complete view of the topology with every neighbour, and every router runs the identical SPF calculation over the identical database to reach an identical, non-negotiable answer.

Extending that across an organisational boundary creates two problems:

  • Total exposure — OSPF cannot share a partial map. Javier would see LoFinance's entire internal topology: every VLAN, every DR link, every internal subnet.
  • No policy control — there is no way to say "accept these routes but not those." The algorithm decides. There is no negotiation, only mathematics.
MENTAL NOTE
BGP exists because organisations need routing as policy — "here is what I'm willing to tell you, here is what I'm willing to accept" — not routing as pure geometry.
CONCEPT 2

The Autonomous System — BGP's Unit of Trust

An Autonomous System (AS) is a set of routers and networks under one consistent administration. Internally it's free to run any IGP it likes (OSPF, RIP, etc. — untouched). Externally, it's represented to the world by a single number: the AS number (ASN).

This is why Javier's question was exactly right: before two organisations can speak BGP, each must know who the other is — as a single entity.

Each AS is identified by a unique number, and usually runs its own interior gateway protocol internally. BGP is what connects autonomous systems to each other — it does not replace what runs inside one.
CONCEPT 3

Path Vector — What a BGP Route Actually Carries

THINK FIRST
  • If BGP shares no topology map at all, how does a route describe how it got somewhere?

BGP is a path-vector protocol. Instead of a shared link-state map, every route carries an AS_PATH attribute — the ordered list of every AS number the route has crossed.

Example AS_PATH
AS_PATH: 65100 4826 1299 65200

Read right to left: originated in AS 65200, passed through 1299, then 4826, then handed to you by AS 65100 (Javier's Singapore hub). The route describes a sequence of organisations, not a topology.

CONCEPT 4

Loop Prevention — The Self-Lookup

OSPF prevents loops through synchronized topology — everyone computing on the same map cannot disagree into a loop. BGP has no map, only the AS_PATH list. Its loop-prevention rule is simple:

RULE
If a router receives a route whose AS_PATH already contains its own AS number, it rejects it.

No shared database, no distributed algorithm — just a per-route self-check. "One grep, functionally, replaces an entire distributed algorithm's worth of loop prevention."

ProtocolLoop Prevention Mechanism
OSPFSynchronized topology database — everyone agrees on the map
BGP"Is my own ASN already in the AS_PATH?" — self-defence, per route
CONCEPT 5

Stub, Multihomed, and Transit

AS TypeConnectionsTraffic Carried
StubSingle connection, one other ASLocal traffic only
MultihomedMultiple connections, different ASesLocal traffic only
TransitMultiple connectionsLocal and transit traffic (not originating/terminating locally) — an ISP is the textbook example

By spring, LoFinance will peer with Singapore, London, and an ISP — multiple relationships with multiple different autonomous systems. That makes LoFinance multihomed. It does not automatically make LoFinance transit.

CONCEPT 6 — THE EXAM TRAP

Two Separate Defences

THINK FIRST
  • What stops your FortiGate from advertising Singapore's routes onward to London, turning LoFinance into an accidental ISP?

AS_PATH prevents loops — it does not prevent unwanted transit. Nothing stops a multihomed AS from forwarding routes learned from one peer onward to another peer unless you configure it not to.

Exam trap: a question asking "how do you prevent LoFinance becoming a transit AS" will often dangle "AS_PATH filtering" as a tempting wrong answer. The real mechanism is prefix / route filtering — deliberately controlling what you advertise outward on each session.

Routing loopsAS_PATH self-checkYes — built in

CONCEPT 7

Private AS Numbers

Real, globally-routable AS numbers are assigned by regional registries, the same way public IPs are. Since LoFinance isn't peering with the public internet backbone yet — just Singapore, London, and HQ, privately — a private ASN is the correct choice.

ANALOGY
Private ASNs (64512–65534) are to AS numbers what RFC 1918 (10.0.0.0/8, 192.168.0.0/16) is to IP addresses — reserved ranges usable privately between consenting parties, with no risk of colliding with a real internet entity, unless later connected to the public internet without translation.
EXAM TIPS

Question Patterns to Expect

PATTERN

Given an AS_PATH string, identify whether a router will accept or reject the route — check for the router's own ASN appearing anywhere in the list.

PATTERN

Given a scenario describing multiple EBGP peers, classify the AS as stub / multihomed / transit — the discriminator is always whether non-local traffic passes through.

PATTERN

"How do you prevent an AS from becoming transit" — correct answer is filtering, not AS_PATH.

PATTERN

Identify the private ASN range from a list of distractors (watch for close-but-wrong ranges).

KEY TAKEAWAYS

What to Carry Forward

  • BGP = policy-based routing between independently-administered domains; OSPF = shared truth within one.
  • AS_PATH is BGP's only "map" — an ordered list of autonomous systems a route has crossed.
  • Loop prevention (automatic, AS_PATH) and transit prevention (configured, filtering) are two different tools for two different problems.
  • Private ASNs (64512–65534) are the correct choice for inter-office, non-internet-facing BGP.

SESSION 54 · VISUAL LEARNING PAGE

Nations Negotiating Treaties

Recommended visual style: Real-World Analogy + Packet/Route Journey — chosen because today's core insight is entirely about TRUST BOUNDARIES and WHAT INFORMATION CROSSES THEM, not device topology.

Why this style: Session 54 had zero CLI and zero topology diagrams by design — it was a pure mental-model session. A conventional network diagram would undersell the actual lesson (organisational trust, not wiring). Three visuals below anchor the three biggest ideas: the household/treaty contrast, AS_PATH as a passport, and the mailroom-forwarding trap.

Visual 1 — Household vs. Treaty

Anchors the session's core mental model, repeated by Señor and echoed by Javier Lin later in the season ("And I am a nation").

Image Generation Prompt

The Household and the Treaty Table

Purpose: reinforce the OSPF-vs-BGP trust-model contrast as a single memorable split image.
PROMPT
A split-composition illustration, flat minimalistic vector-art style, cream/off-white background (#faf5e9), coral and muted-gold accents, sage green highlights, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework, no gradients, no photorealism, storybook-educational mood. LEFT HALF ("OSPF — The Household"): A warm, open-plan kitchen table scene. Several router-shaped characters (simple friendly robot-router figures with small antenna and a single glowing "eye" screen showing a network icon) sit around one round table, all looking at ONE large shared map spread flat on the table showing a complete house floor-plan (representing full topology visibility). Everyone at the table can see every room and hallway on the map. Warm lighting, soft sage-green tablecloth, a cup of coffee steaming nearby. Small text label beneath: "ONE MAP, SHARED FULLY." RIGHT HALF ("BGP — The Treaty Table"): A formal negotiating table split by a coral-colored dividing line down the center of the composition. Two separate router-character delegations face each other across the table, each holding only a short, sealed scroll or folder (representing a limited list of destinations they're willing to disclose) rather than an open map. Between them, a small stamped passport-like document passes hands, with a visible chain of wax-seal stamps along its edge (representing AS_PATH). Muted-gold accent lighting, more formal/reserved body language than the left side. Small text label beneath: "ONLY WHAT'S DISCLOSED, STAMP BY STAMP." A thin vertical coral divider separates the two halves. No dense technical labels beyond the two short captions given. No real logos, no photorealistic rendering, no 3D glossy shading. Generous negative space around both scenes so the contrast reads instantly even at small size. This image must NOT include LoFinance branding or character likenesses — it is a general concept illustration, not a story scene.
Aspect ratio: 16:9Placement: Top of "Concept 1" section, immediately after the household/treaty framing
Accessibility description: A split illustration; left side shows router characters sharing one open map at a table, right side shows two delegations exchanging a stamped passport-like document across a divided negotiating table, representing the difference between OSPF's shared topology and BGP's policy-based, limited disclosure.

Visual 2 — The AS_PATH Passport Journey

Makes the abstract AS_PATH attribute concrete and traceable — a route's journey shown as stamps collected in order.

Image Generation Prompt

The Route's Passport

Purpose: turn AS_PATH from an abstract list into a visual, ordered journey a student can "read" left to right (origin to destination) or right to left (as BGP itself reads it).
PROMPT
A flat minimalistic vector-art illustration in a horizontal timeline/journey format, cream background (#faf5e9), coral and muted-gold accents, sage green highlights, soft brown outlines, bold clean linework, no gradients, no photorealism. Show a single small folded paper "route" character (a simple anthropomorphic folded-letter shape with tiny stick legs and a friendly dot-eyed face, holding a small passport booklet) walking left to right across four stepping stones, each stone representing a different Autonomous System, styled as small labeled territory markers: Stone 1 labeled "AS 65200 (origin)," Stone 2 labeled "AS 1299," Stone 3 labeled "AS 4826," Stone 4 labeled "AS 65100 (Javier's Singapore hub)," with a final arrow pointing off-page labeled "→ arrives at LoFinance HQ." At each stone, show a small wax-seal stamp being pressed onto the open passport booklet the character carries, with each stamp visually distinct (different simple geometric seal shapes in coral, gold, and sage green) and appearing in the same left-to-right order as the stones. By the final stone, the passport is shown open with all four stamps visible in a row, so the viewer can see the accumulated AS_PATH at a glance. Include a small thought-bubble above a router-guard character standing just before LoFinance HQ, checking the passport: the guard's eye is a simple magnifying-glass icon scanning down the stamp list, with a small "✕" icon hovering nearby representing the rejection check ("is my own stamp already here?"). No dense technical text beyond the four AS-number labels and the "origin" / "LoFinance HQ" labels. No real logos. Warm, clear, textbook-illustration mood with generous white space between stepping stones so the sequence reads instantly.
Aspect ratio: 21:9 (wide timeline)Placement: Inside "Concept 3 — Path Vector" and "Concept 4 — Loop Prevention" sections
Accessibility description: A small folded-letter character carrying a passport walks across four stepping stones labeled with AS numbers, collecting a stamp at each one in order, arriving at LoFinance HQ where a guard character checks the accumulated stamps against its own identity before allowing entry.

Visual 3 — The Mailroom That Won't Forward Outside Mail

Anchors the exam-critical multihomed-vs-transit distinction and the filtering-not-AS_PATH trap.

Image Generation Prompt

The LoFinance Mailroom

Purpose: make the "multihoming does not equal transit" concept physically intuitive by showing a mailroom that receives mail from two outside partners but does NOT automatically forward mail between them — only a deliberate policy sign changes that.
PROMPT
A flat minimalistic vector-art illustration, cream background (#faf5e9), coral and muted-gold accents, sage green highlights, soft brown outlines, bold clean linework, no gradients, no photorealism, gently humorous office-diagram mood. Center: a small friendly mailroom booth (representing LoFinance HQ's FortiGate) with two labeled inbound mail slots on its left side: one slot labeled "FROM: Singapore (Javier)" with a coral mail tray beneath it stacked with a few envelopes, and one slot labeled "FROM: London" with a muted-gold mail tray beneath it. Both trays feed into the booth. Inside the booth, a small clerk character (simple round-bodied figure, sage-green apron, friendly dot eyes) stands at a sorting desk. On the desk is a prominent sign that can be read clearly: "OUTBOUND POLICY: LOCAL MAIL ONLY" with a small padlock icon next to it — representing default prefix filtering. To the right of the booth, two outbound chutes: one leads to a small "LoFinance HQ" mailbox (representing local traffic delivered), the other chute — leading directly BACK toward the Singapore and London trays as if trying to cross-forward mail between them — is shown BLOCKED by a small red-coral "✕" gate with the policy sign's padlock icon repeated on it, and a faint dotted-line ghost of an envelope bouncing off the gate. Include a small secondary vignette in the top corner: the same booth, but with the sign flipped to read "OUTBOUND POLICY: FORWARD ALL" (padlock now open) — showing envelopes flowing freely from the Singapore tray, through the booth, to a London-bound chute — representing the deliberate, configured choice to become transit. Label this vignette faintly "(only if configured)". No real company logos. No dense text beyond the sign wording and the two source labels already specified. Generous white space, clear left-to-right reading flow, textbook-diagram clarity.
Aspect ratio: 4:3Placement: Inside "Concept 6 — The Exam Trap" section
Accessibility description: A mailroom booth receives mail from Singapore and London trays; a clerk's policy sign reads "local mail only," and a gate blocks mail from being forwarded between the two outside sources unless the policy sign is deliberately flipped to "forward all," illustrating that transit behaviour requires explicit configuration rather than happening automatically from having multiple peers.

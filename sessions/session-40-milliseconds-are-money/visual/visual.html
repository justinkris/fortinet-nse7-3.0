SESSION 40 VISUAL LEARNING PAGE

Milliseconds Are Money: Inside the Silicon

Long-term memory through visualisation — five illustrated anchors for the SPU chapter.

Recommended style: Layer-by-Layer Breakdown + Real-World Analogy hybrid

Why this style: today's content is fundamentally spatial — where chips sit on a board, where the ISF sits between ports and processors, and what changes when it's removed. A layer-by-layer breakdown of the chassis interior encodes the geography, while analogy scenes (toll booth, paperweight, trench coat) anchor the behaviours. Packet-journey style is deliberately held back for Session 41, where the life of an offloaded packet is the actual syllabus.

Visual 1 — The Two Specialists

s40-visual-01-two-specialists.png
The Doorman and the Librarian
Anchor the NP/CP split by problem shape and by physical placement.
Aspect ratio: 16:9 · Placement: top of page, before all other visuals
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic but highly detailed vector illustration, cream off-white background, coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines, clean bold line work, no gradients, no photorealism, storybook-professional style. A cutaway view of a stylised firewall appliance drawn as a grand building interior split into two working areas. LEFT AREA (the entrance): a tall doorway labelled "PORTS" with a queue of small envelope-shaped packets streaming in; a broad-shouldered doorman character in a coral uniform stamped "NP7" checks each envelope with a single rapid glance and waves it through a fast lane painted sage green on the floor; on his belt hang a small padlock-and-key (labelled "IPsec") and a magnifier over an envelope header (labelled "integrity check"); behind him a wall of tiny pigeonholes labelled "SESSION TABLE — 12,000,000" with a few visibly empty slots. RIGHT AREA (deep inside the building): a study desk where a spectacled CPU character in a navy suit sits; chained to the side of the desk by a friendly gold chain is a librarian character labelled "CP9" surrounded by open pattern books ("IPS", "AV", "APP-ID") and holding an unlocked TLS envelope to a lamp (labelled "SSL deep inspection"); the CPU hands the librarian a tall stack of papers with a relieved expression. Between the two areas, a subtle floor inscription: "MOVING vs READING". Composition: eye-level, both areas equal width, generous spacing, clear visual hierarchy. Colour coding: NP world in corals/greens (motion), CP world in navy/gold (study). Must NOT appear: any real vendor logos, dense technical diagrams, gradients, photoreal textures. Learning purpose: viewer should recall that the NP is stationed at the ports doing repetitive per-packet work including IPsec and header checks, while the CP is the CPU's coprocessor for pattern matching and SSL crypto.
Accessibility: A building cutaway — a doorman at the ports fast-tracks envelopes past a 12-million-slot pigeonhole wall, while deep inside a librarian chained to the CPU's desk reads envelope contents under a lamp.
Reinforces: NP at the ports / CP beside the CPU; job-to-chip mapping.

Visual 2 — The Expensive Paperweight

Junior's Cabling Job

Visual 3 — The Toll Booth Removed

s40-visual-03-toll-booth-removed.png
ISF vs NP Direct: Before and After
Encode what the ISF is, what NP Direct removes, and what the removal costs.
Aspect ratio: 16:9 · Placement: beside the NP Direct section
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic detailed vector illustration, cream background, coral/gold/sage palette, soft brown outlines, no gradients, split-panel BEFORE/AFTER composition divided by a thin gold seam. LEFT PANEL "WITH ISF": a stylised highway map inside a chassis — eight front-port ramps at the bottom all feeding into ONE large roundabout building labelled "ISF — INTERNAL SWITCH FABRIC" drawn as a toll plaza with little coin symbols and a queue of envelope-cars pausing at booths; from the roundabout, roads fan out to two NP7 towers and a CPU tower, illustrating any-port-to-any-processor flexibility; a small sign reads "TOLL PAID TWICE: IN + OUT". RIGHT PANEL "NP DIRECT": the toll plaza is gone — demolished, shown as a faint dotted outline with a small "REMOVED" tag; four special ramps labelled X5, X6, X7, X8 now run as straight express lanes directly into the nearest NP7 tower, envelope-cars streaming at speed with motion lines; the other NP7 tower stands separate with its OWN ports, and a broken-bridge icon between the two towers with a "NO BRIDGE" sign; a stopwatch icon in the corner shows a smaller time. Along the bottom of the right panel, a painted road marking: "SAME CHIP IN, SAME CHIP OUT". Colour logic: toll/ISF elements in muted gold; express lanes in coral; towers in navy. Must NOT appear: real highway signage conventions, photorealism, gradients, more than two panels. Learning purpose: viewer should recall that the ISF connects every port to every processor at a latency toll, NP Direct removes it on ultralow-latency ports X5–X8 for lowest latency, and traffic must then enter and exit on the same NP.
Accessibility: Split panel — left shows eight port ramps feeding a toll-plaza ISF that fans out to processors; right shows the plaza demolished, ports X5–X8 running as express lanes straight into one NP7, with a "no bridge" sign between the two NP towers.
Reinforces: ISF toll · NP Direct removal · same-NP in/out rule · flexibility-for-latency trade.

Visual 5 — Reading the Silicon

s40-visual-05-reading-the-silicon.png
The Two-Command X-Ray
Anchor the identification CLI and the 4e36/4e37 mapping against misreads.
Aspect ratio: 3:2 · Placement: end of page, before footer
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic detailed vector illustration, cream background, coral/gold/sage palette, soft brown outlines, no gradients. A workshop bench scene: a mystery FortiGate appliance lies on the bench like a patient, and a large stylised X-ray screen hovers above it showing the chassis interior in soft navy silhouette with two glowing chips visible inside. The X-ray screen displays two clean terminal readouts drawn as elegant paper tags clipped to the screen: TAG 1 reads "diagnose hardware lspci | grep 1a29 → 1a29:4e36" with a decoder ring graphic beside it — a small brass rotary dial where "4e36" aligns with "NP6" and "4e37" aligns with "NP7", the pointer firmly on NP6; TAG 2 reads "get hardware status → ASIC version: CP9". At the bench, the Intermediate Engineer — a competent, focused figure in a sage work apron — adjusts spectacles while reading the dial carefully; a small ghosted thought-bubble behind them shows a crossed-out hasty misread ("NP7?") being erased. Señor in his sombrero watches from the doorway with quiet approval, holding a purchase quote marked "VERIFY FIRST". Small caption plate on the bench: "READ THE HEX, NOT YOUR HOPE". Must NOT appear: real terminal chrome/UI, gradients, photorealism, extra characters. Learning purpose: viewer should recall the two identification commands, the 1a29 vendor ID, the 4e36=NP6 / 4e37=NP7 mapping, and the discipline of verifying silicon before trusting a quote.
Accessibility: An engineer X-rays a mystery firewall on a workbench; a decoder dial maps hex 4e36 to NP6 and 4e37 to NP7 while a crossed-out hasty "NP7?" fades in a thought bubble; a sombrero-wearing mentor holds a quote stamped "verify first".
Reinforces: lspci/1a29 · 4e36 vs 4e37 · get hardware status · verify-before-buy.

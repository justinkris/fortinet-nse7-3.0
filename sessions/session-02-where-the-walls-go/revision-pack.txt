QUICK SUMMARY (one page)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
One firewall is never enough because inspection is not one job.

EDGE vs INTERNAL are different jobs:
- Edge (north-south): faces the internet; enemy = hostile CONTENT;
  needs deep inspection — web filter, app control, IPS (NGFW duties).
- Internal (east-west): faces your own network; enemy = LATERAL
  MOVEMENT; needs least-privilege segmentation at very LOW LATENCY.
- "Enable everything everywhere" = junior. Right inspection at the
  right point = senior. Every engine costs time; ms = money.

THE FOUR BOXES:
- DEFW  · Branch      · FG 30–90     · ≤1 Gbps    · firewall, IPsec
  to HQ, segmentation, integrated W/LAN. NOT for deep analysis.
  Outgrows (breakout/headcount) → step up to 100–900.
- ISFW  · Campus      · FG 100–900   · 1–100 Gbps · micro-segmentation
  (VLANs/VDOMs/zones), low latency, integrated W/LAN, HW SSL.
- DCFW  · Data centre · FG 1000–7000 · 10–1000 Gbps · hyperscale;
  segmentation via VRFs/VDOMs/zones; bundles multiple switch links
  into ONE logical interface (redundancy + speed).
- NGFW  · a ROLE, not a place · FG 100–7000 + VM/Cloud/FWaaS ·
  1–40 Gbps · multilayered security: FW, VPN, D-NGFW, segmentation,
  DDoS, low latency, W/LAN, 5G/LTE, HW SSL, secure SD-WAN,
  hyperscale, ZTNA.

EAST-WEST ICEBERG: inside a DC, server-to-server traffic (market-data
fan-out, replication, backups) dwarfs perimeter traffic — often 10x+.
Spec the DCFW for the traffic you DON'T see leaving. Deep inspection
of traffic entering the DC usually lives on an NGFW gateway in front;
a DCFW can also face the internet directly using VDOMs.

ROLE ≠ MODEL (the chapter's core): a 100–900 ISFW with UTM profiles
enabled IS an NGFW. Same box, same invoice. Real networks = HYBRID
MESH: mixed models/roles, one FortiOS, one FortiManager.

Board-ready line: "We pay for placement, not duplication."

CHEAT SHEET
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Commands (verification anchors):
  get system status                 -> model/series, FortiOS, mode
  get system performance status     -> CPU, memory, throughput, setup rate
  diagnose sys session stat         -> session totals (east-west grows this)
  show firewall policy              -> inspection posture on a path

GUI orientation:
  Policy & Objects > Firewall Policy   -> where security profiles attach
  (Profiles configured per policy; posture = per placement, not global)

Numbers to know cold:
  DEFW  30–90      up to 1 Gbps
  ISFW  100–900    1–100 Gbps
  NGFW  100–7000 + VM/cloud   1–40 Gbps
  DCFW  1000–7000  10–1000 Gbps

Design rules:
  1. Split by threat model, not device.
  2. Inspection posture is a property of the POINT, not the fleet.
  3. East-west >> north-south inside a DC.
  4. Backhauled branch -> inspection lives at HQ.
  5. Local breakout -> inspection must live locally (bigger box).
  6. Role = location + enabled features.
  7. DCFW physical fingerprint: LAG to multiple switches.

Poison words in exam options:
  "replaced" · "unsupported" · "automatically becomes"

FLASHCARDS (Q -> A)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Q: What threat does an edge firewall primarily fight? An internal one?
A: Edge: hostile content (needs deep inspection). Internal: lateral
   movement (needs least-privilege segmentation, low latency).

Q: DEFW — series, throughput, four core functions?
A: FG 30–90; up to 1 Gbps; firewall, VPN (IPsec to HQ), segmentation,
   integrated wireless LAN.

Q: Is a DEFW designed for in-depth security analysis?
A: No. If a branch needs more, step up to the 100–900 midrange.

Q: ISFW — series, throughput, purpose?
A: FG 100–900; 1–100 Gbps; network micro-segmentation with low
   latency, integrated W/LAN, HW SSL inspection.

Q: DCFW — series, throughput, purpose?
A: FG 1000–7000; 10–1000 Gbps; high-speed protection of the data
   centre (hyperscale).

Q: DCFW's physical-design fingerprint?
A: Multiple physical switch links bundled into one logical interface
   (redundancy + high-speed transfer).

Q: NGFW — series span and throughput?
A: FG 100–7000 plus VM/Cloud/FWaaS; 1–40 Gbps average.

Q: Why does NGFW's series span overlap all the others?
A: Because NGFW is a ROLE, not a hardware tier. Location + enabled
   features define the role.

Q: You enable IPS + web filtering on a campus 600F doing segmentation.
   What is it now?
A: It is functioning as an NGFW while remaining a campus-class ISFW.
   Nothing was replaced.

Q: Where does deep inspection happen for a backhauling branch?
A: Centrally at HQ, after the IPsec tunnel — on hardware built for it.

Q: When would a branch need local UTM (and probably a bigger box)?
A: Local internet breakout, or growth beyond "basic" needs.

Q: What is a hybrid mesh firewall scenario?
A: Mixed firewall models carrying distributed roles (segmentation,
   DC, virtual NGFW, D-NGFW) across the network — one FortiOS, one
   management console (FortiManager).

Q: Who usually sits in front of a DCFW, and what's the alternative?
A: An NGFW gateway performing content filtering/IPS for entering
   traffic; alternatively the DCFW faces the internet directly and
   segments itself with VDOMs.

Q: Ratio rule of thumb inside a data centre?
A: East-west (server-to-server) traffic dwarfs north-south — spec for
   the traffic that never leaves the building.

Q: Penny asks why we're buying four firewall types. One sentence.
A: "We buy the right size box for each location and turn security
   roles on where needed — we pay for placement, not duplication."

Generate a 10–14 minute episode of "The Packet Path", a warm,

EPISODE 2: "Where the Walls Go"

COLD OPEN: Tuesday at LoFinance, a quant trading firm in Sydney whose
logo is cash frozen in an ice cube. Junior — one month into his first
IT job — stands frozen at a whiteboard. Four empty boxes in the senior
engineer's handwriting: NGFW, ISFW, DCFW, DEFW. Señor (30 years of
experience, inexplicable sombrero) said only "one firewall is never
enough" and is now in the car park feeding a magpie.

ACT 1 — THE QUESTION: Penny, the operations manager, announces the
growth plan: trading floor doubles, server room becomes a real data
centre, a branch office is coming. She needs to know WHY four kinds of
firewall aren't "buying the same thing four times." Señor's drive-by
question: "Is inspecting traffic between floors the same job as
inspecting traffic from the internet?" Explore the answer: the edge
fights hostile CONTENT (deep inspection); the interior fights LATERAL
MOVEMENT (least privilege, low latency). Two guards, two jobs — a
customs officer opening parcels at the border versus keycard doors
between departments.

ACT 2 — JUNIOR'S MISTAKE: Junior suggests enabling every security
profile on the internal firewall — "more inspection is more security!"
Walk through why this fails at a firm where a millisecond costs more
than a salary: every engine costs time, and inspection posture belongs
to the POINT in the network, not the fleet. Land the line: security at
the expense of performance isn't security.

ACT 3 — THE FOUR BOXES: Tour the reference architecture with the
fortified-city analogy: NGFW = customs house at the main gate; ISFW =
keycard doors between districts (FortiGate 100–900, 1–100 Gbps, low
latency, wireless built in); DCFW = the vault-district gate (1000–7000,
10 to a THOUSAND gigabits, hyperscale, multiple switch links braided
into one logical cable); DEFW = the village outpost (30–90, up to
1 Gbps, a sturdy door and a private courier road — IPsec — back to the
city where the real inspection happens). Include the east-west iceberg:
one order leaving the building rides on a mountain of replication,
backups and market-data fan-out that never leaves at all.

ACT 4 — THE REVEAL: Junior asks the trap question: "What do we buy
when we buy an NGFW?" The twist: you don't. Three of the labels are
ADDRESSES; NGFW is a JOB DESCRIPTION. Enable IPS and web filtering on
the campus ISFW and it IS an NGFW — same box, same invoice. Real
networks are a hybrid mesh: mixed models, mixed roles, one operating
system, one management console. Penny gets her sentence: "We pay for
placement, not duplication."

CLOSE: Recap the ladder (30–90 / 100–900 / 1000–7000, and NGFW
spanning them all), the poison words on the exam ("replaced",
"automatically becomes"), and the senior reflex: direction, change,
role-versus-posture, proportionality.

TEASER: Tomorrow a trader corners our engineer at the coffee machine:
"Where does my order ACTUALLY go?" Nobody has ever answered him
properly. Next time on The Packet Path: "Life of a Packet" — inside
FortiOS, and the first look at the mysterious chips called NP and CP.

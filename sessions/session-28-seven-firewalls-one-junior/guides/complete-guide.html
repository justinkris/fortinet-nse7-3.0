Seven Firewalls, One Junior

FortiManager architecture, ADOMs, and the global database — the day LoFinance stopped managing firewalls one keyboard at a time. Study Guide 02 Central Management, pp. 24–28. Blueprint 2.1.

The real cost of manual management is N devices × M changes — and its casualty is consistency, which in a regulated firm is a compliance finding.

This session is about collapsing that number, without collapsing the walls between two firms that must stay separate.

Story Recap

The pallet arrives at 8:40am

LoFinance has swallowed Vaultline Financial whole — HQ, three branches, and three spares still in boxes. Seven FortiGates arrive shrink-wrapped on a hand-truck. Junior racks the first, opens a Notepad file called vaultline-config-MASTER.txt, and begins pasting a golden config into each firewall by hand, humming, certain that "seven's not that many."

Señor, sombrero on, watches without touching the keyboard. His question is the whole episode: "How many times will you type the syslog server address today? And in April, when it moves to a new subnet? And when Penny buys the next firm?" Junior's smile begins to fail.

Then Penny arrives with the constraint that shapes everything: LoFinance and Vaultline must stay administratively isolated — separate ongoing audits — but they must share one FortiManager, across mixed firmware (some Vaultline spares are 7.4; LoFinance is 7.6). Junior proposes a VL-/LF- naming prefix to keep them apart. Señor: "The prefix is a hope, Junior. Penny did not ask for a hope."

Learning Objectives

By the end you can…

  • Explain why central management exists in terms of cost and compliance, not just convenience.
  • Describe FortiManager's three-layer architecture: global database, local ADOM, device database.
  • Use the direction vocabulary correctly: Retrieve/Import vs Install vs Assign.
  • Decide where to draw an ADOM boundary — and why firmware and site are not the primary axis.
  • State the firmware compatibility window and the reserved g prefix rule.
  • Decide when to use the global database and when doing so would defeat isolation.
💭Think First · The N×M Problem

Junior is counting the wrong number. He sees seven boxes. Señor sees a number that grows over time and quietly destroys something an auditor will ask about.

  • If Junior has 7 firewalls and the standard changes 20 times this year, how many hand-edits has he signed up for — and what's the formula?
  • Beyond the labour, what does every manual touch-point put at risk — and why does that word matter more in a financial firm than anywhere else?
Section 01 · Why Central Management Exists

The number that hurts

The cost of managing firewalls by hand is not the number of firewalls. It is N devices × M changes. Seven firewalls and twenty standard changes a year is 140 individual hand-edits — 140 chances to mistype a syslog IP, or forget a box because Riley called about a printer.

The casualty isn't the afternoon. It's consistency. After a year of hand-config, you can no longer prove that all seven firewalls enforce the same standard. And in a regulated financial firm, "I think they match" is not an inconvenience — it is an audit finding.

🏭 Analogy · The Franchise Recipe
Junior's plan is seven cooks each copying the recipe by hand. Change the recipe and you must visit all seven kitchens. Miss one, and one branch serves the old dish — and the health inspector, arriving unannounced, finds a kitchen that isn't following the standard. FortiManager is the central recipe card that every kitchen reads from: change it once, every kitchen updates, and you can prove to the inspector that all seven are identical.
💰
Stakeholder framing — money leads
"When the auditor asks me to prove all seven firewalls enforce the same standard, hand-configuration means I can't — that's a finding. FortiManager lets me prove it from one source." Consequence first; mechanism second.
💭Think First · Two Copies of the Truth

Once a central box manages a firewall, there are suddenly two copies of that firewall's config in the world. That's not a bug — it's the whole mechanism. But it raises two questions.

  • Which copy is the master and which gets overwritten — and why does a regulated firm need it that way round?
  • Before FortiManager can be the master of a firewall it has never seen, what must happen first, and in which direction does that flow?
Section 02 · The Device Database, Source of Truth & Sync

Retrieve before you rule

Every FortiGate brought under management gets its own device database on FortiManager — FMG's local, authoritative copy of that firewall's actual configuration (interfaces, routing, system settings). Seven managed firewalls means seven device databases.

Precision trap. The device database is the WHAT — the config itself. The WHO (who administers it, who's walled off from it) is the ADOM. Don't blur them.

Once a firewall is managed, FMG's device database is the source of truth. You stop editing the box directly and edit the database instead, letting it push. But FMG cannot be master of a firewall it has never learned — so the very first action is to pull the firewall's existing config up into the device database, so the two are in sync. From that moment, FMG leads.

Reverse Retrieve and Install and you either wipe a firewall you meant to learn, or fail to push a change you meant to deploy. Onboarding runs FGT → FMG first, always.

Section 03 · ADOMs — Administrative Domains

The wall that actually holds

An ADOM (Administrative Domain) is a partition inside one FortiManager that isolates a group of devices: its own administrators, its own policies, its own objects, and — critically for this pallet — its own firmware version. A Vaultline admin scoped to the Vaultline ADOM cannot see an LF- box, because it is not in their ADOM — not because of a prefix and a prayer.

When ADOMs are disabled, you still work in a default ADOM named root, which contains the two databases you'll meet next session: a device layer and a policy layer. The FortiGuard database serves the root ADOM.

Penny's three constraints, one construct

ConstraintSatisfied by
Isolation (separate audits)Two ADOMs: LoFinance and Vaultline, administratively walled off
Mixed firmware (7.4 + 7.6)Each ADOM carries its own firmware version, within the compatibility window
One physical boxBoth ADOMs live inside a single FortiManager

Where to draw the boundary

The primary axis is the administrative + policy unit: who owns it, who audits it, and what policy package it shares. If firewalls behave similarly, they can share a policy package, and belong in the same ADOM. That is a design decision.

Firmware is different — it's a constraint that can force a split. An ADOM has a firmware version and a compatibility window; a device too old for the ADOM's version forces your hand. And site (the org chart) sometimes coincides with a boundary, but never causes it.

🧱
Decision rule
1) Draw ADOMs around administrative/policy units (tenant, audit boundary, shared policy package). 2) Then check firmware fits the version window; if a device is too old, firmware forces a further split. Site is never the cause.

The firmware compatibility window

Rule: a source at version X supports local ADOMs at X, X-1, and X-2. A 7.6 global ADOM supports ADOMs at 7.6, 7.4, and 7.2. Vaultline's 7.4 spares and 7.6 units fit comfortably under a 7.6 umbrella. A 7.0 box would fall outside the window and force a split.

  • What construct lets you define a truly universal object once and share it into multiple ADOMs at once?
  • Junior says "put EVERYTHING there." What specifically breaks if you push tenant-specific policy up into it?
Section 04 · The Global Database

Define once, assign down

The global database (global ADOM) sits above the local ADOMs. You define a common object or policy once at the global layer, then assign it down into multiple local ADOMs simultaneously. Change the syslog server once at the top, and it propagates to every ADOM that inherited it. Junior's 140 finally collapses to 1 for anything company-wide.

Reserved prefix. Global objects carry the reserved letter g. This is hard-blocked, not advisory — you cannot create a local object whose name starts with g-. The reservation keeps "shared-from-above" visually distinct from "local-to-this-ADOM."
GLOBAL DATABASE · company-wide
syslog, NTP, DNS — define once, assign ↓ into every ADOM. Objects prefixed g-.
LOCAL ADOM · LoFinance (7.6)  |  Vaultline (7.4/7.6) · per tenant
Hard-walled tenants: own admins, own policies, own objects, own firmware. Separate audits stay separate.
DEVICE DATABASE · per firewall
FMG's copy of one box's actual config. 7 firewalls → 7 device DBs. Retrieve ↑ to onboard, Install ↓ to push.

When NOT to go global

Push everything into the global database and you dissolve the very wall Penny bought the acquisition isolation for — you may as well run one big ADOM. The global database is shared by design; it cannot be an isolation boundary.

⚖️
The test
"True for literally everyone, or just this tenant?" Everyone → global database. Just them → their ADOM. One big ADOM = sharing, no walls. Separate ADOMs = walls, no sharing. Global-over-local = both.

Common Mistakes

Where this goes wrong

  • Treating a naming prefix as isolation. A prefix is a convention, not an admin boundary. Only an ADOM enforces separation.
  • Reversing Retrieve and Install. Import onboards (FGT→FMG); Install deploys (FMG→FGT). Reverse them and you wipe or fail to push.
  • Softening the g rule. "You shouldn't" is wrong — it's "you cannot." The prefix is hard-reserved.
  • Drawing ADOMs by site. The org chart is not the axis. Administrative/policy units are. Site may coincide, never causes.
  • Over-sharing to the global DB. Tenant-specific policy in the global database dissolves isolation.
  • Guessing the firmware window. It's X, X-1, X-2 — a precise number, not "roughly compatible."
Senior Engineering Insights

What the seniors carry from this

  • Cost is N×M, casualty is consistency. When you brief a stakeholder, lead with the compliance consequence, not the tooling.
  • Enforcement beats convention. If separation matters, it must be structural (ADOM), never a naming hope.
  • Design first, constraints second. Choose ADOM boundaries by administration/policy; let firmware only force extra splits.
  • Share the universal, wall the specific. The global/local split is what lets you have consistency and isolation at once.
Exam Preparation

Ten questions & model answers

Q1
ADOMs are disabled. Which default ADOM are you in, and what two databases does it contain?
root ADOM, containing a device layer and a policy layer. The FortiGuard database serves it.
Q2
A 7.6 global ADOM can assign down to which firmware versions?
Rule: X, X-1, X-2. So a 7.6 global ADOM supports local ADOMs at 7.6, 7.4, and 7.2.
Q3
You try to create an address object named g-DMZ in a local ADOM. What happens and why?
It's rejected — hard-blocked. The g prefix is reserved for global objects so shared-from-above stays distinct from local. Not "discouraged" — disallowed.
Q4
State the direction of Retrieve and of Install.
Retrieve = FortiGate → FMG (onboarding). Install = FMG → FortiGate (day-2 push).
Q5
Penny needs LoFinance and Vaultline admins never to see each other's devices, on one FMG. Name the construct — and why doesn't a naming prefix work?
ADOM. A prefix separates visually but enforces nothing — any admin can still touch a differently-prefixed device. Only an ADOM is a real administrative boundary.
Q6
A Vaultline ADOM holds both 7.4 and 7.6 FortiGates. Permitted? Under what condition, and what breaks it?
Yes, provided both sit within the ADOM's version window (X, X-1, X-2). Under a 7.6 ADOM, 7.6 and 7.4 both fit. A device outside the window (e.g. 7.0) breaks it.
Q7
True/false: "The primary reason to create a separate ADOM is that devices run different firmware."
False. The primary reason is an administrative/policy boundary. Firmware is a valid constraint that can force a split, not the primary reason.
Q8
What lives in the device database, and how many does an FMG managing seven firewalls have?
FMG's authoritative copy of each firewall's actual configuration (the WHAT, not the WHO). Seven — one per managed device.
Q9
The corporate syslog IP must be identical across both ADOMs and changeable in one place. Where do you define it, and what one-word operation pushes it into each ADOM?
Define it in the global database; the operation is Assign (define once, assign down).
Q10
Penny: "Why can't Junior just configure them by hand? It's only seven." One senior sentence — consequence first.
"When the auditor asks me to prove all seven firewalls enforce the same standard, hand-config means I can't — that's a finding. FortiManager proves it from one source." Money/consequence leads; mechanism justifies.
Key Takeaways

Carry these forward

  • Manual management costs N×M; the casualty is consistency → audit findings.
  • Three layers: global DB (company-wide) > local ADOM (tenant/policy wall) > device DB (per box).
  • Retrieve before you rule: FGT→FMG onboarding first, then FMG→FGT installs.
  • Draw ADOMs by administration/policy; firmware only forces splits; site never causes them.
  • Window: X, X-1, X-2. Prefix g is reserved / hard-blocked. Global verb is assign.

Troubleshooting a Link That Is Never Down

How a senior engineer separates four identical-sounding faults without changing a single line of configuration first.

The hardest faults are the ones where nothing is broken.

Baz’s link passed every test anyone had run. This guide is about building the test they had not run, and reading its output honestly.

STEP 01

Symptoms, and What They Refuse to Tell You

Every SD-WAN complaint arrives in the same words. Riley had eleven of them and they all said it was slow, it’s fine now. That sentence is compatible with at least four unrelated faults, and it discriminates between none of them.

What the user saysWhat it could beWhat it is NOT evidence of
“It was slow, now it’s fine”Intermittent brownout on one member; ECMP hashing some sessions onto itAn outage. Nothing went down.
“Freezes then catches up”TCP meeting packet loss — retransmit and recoverBandwidth exhaustion specifically
“Calls sound like the ocean”Jitter and loss on a UDP flow — no recovery mechanismA codec or endpoint fault
“Only some people”Per-session member selection, or a rule matching a subsetA user-specific problem
“Everything went out the wrong link”Rule ordering, member list, or SLA stateA routing failure
The trap that costs the most time: treating an intermittent quality complaint as a fault to be found. There may be no fault. A brownout is a working link performing badly, and if you go looking for something broken you will find nothing and conclude the user is wrong.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
The symptom tells you the experience. It never tells you the layer. Resist the urge to guess the layer from the wording.
STEP 02

The Questions to Ask Before Any Command

  • When, exactly? Penny’s handwritten list clustered at 09:40 and 11:05 on consecutive days. A repeating time signature points at contention, scheduled jobs or carrier congestion — not at configuration, which does not keep office hours.
  • Everyone, or some? If it is some, the difference between the affected and unaffected is your fastest diagnostic. Same subnet? Same application? Same time?
  • What does the affected traffic actually match? Before you look at links, know which rule this traffic hits. A perfectly healthy link is irrelevant if the traffic was never eligible for it.
  • Is anything green that shouldn’t be? If a dashboard disagrees with a user, the dashboard is answering a different question. Find out which one.
  • What changed? And separately — has anything changed at all? Baz’s brownout involved no change on either side. “Nothing changed” is a finding, not a dead end.
Señor’s framing: “Baz is not lying. His link is up. The link being up is the only thing he has ever promised us, and he has delivered it perfectly.” When a monitoring source contradicts a human, work out what question the source was built to answer before you decide who is wrong.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
A repeating time signature is a contention signature. Configuration faults do not observe business hours.
STEP 03

Verification: The Four-Layer Ladder

Climb it in order. Each rung answers a different question, and skipping one is how people end up changing configuration to fix a carrier problem.

Rung 1 — Is it UP?

Reachability
get router info routing-table all

S*    0.0.0.0/0 [1/0] via 203.0.113.1, port1, [1/0]
                [1/0] via 198.51.100.1, port2, [1/0]

Expected: both member routes present. If a member is missing, this is not yet an SD-WAN problem — check the interface, the gateway, and whether the member was configured with a gateway at all.

Rung 2 — Is it IN-SLA?

Quality
diagnose sys sdwan health-check

Health Check(Trading-SLA):
Seq(1 port1): state(alive), packet-loss(0.000%) latency(11.204),
              jitter(1.998), mos(4.38), bandwidth-up(94371), sla_map=0x1
Seq(2 port2): state(alive), packet-loss(5.400%) latency(412.870),
              jitter(38.115), mos(2.71), bandwidth-up(9863), sla_map=0x0

Read it carefully. Note that port2 is state(alive). It is up. It is answering. And its sla_map=0x0 means it satisfies no SLA — latency 412ms against a 150ms threshold, loss 5.4% against 2%. Both facts are true at once, and that is the entire lesson of this session in one line of output.

sla_map is a bitmask. Each bit is one SLA entry under that health check. 0x1 means SLA 1 is satisfied; 0x0 means none are. When you have multiple SLA entries with different thresholds, this is the field that tells you which tier a member currently qualifies for.

Rung 3 — Is it ELIGIBLE?

Read the member list of the rule that matched. A member can be up and in-SLA and still never carry this traffic because it was never offered. There is no command that will tell you this — you have to read the configuration and know which rule matched.

Rung 4 — Is it PREFERRED?

Selection
diagnose sys sdwan service

Service(1): Address Mode(IPV4) flags=0x200
  Gen(2), TOS(0x0/0x0), Protocol(0), Mode(sla)
  Members(2):
    1: Seq_num(1 port1), alive, sla(0x1), gid(0), cfg_order(0), local cost(0)
    2: Seq_num(2 port2), alive, sla(0x0), gid(0), cfg_order(1), local cost(0)

Expected: the member you want at the top with sla(0x1). Members listed with sla(0x0) are still present and still alive — they have simply lost their claim on the traffic.

Supporting commands: diagnose sys sdwan member (per-member state), diagnose sys sdwan health-check status (verdict summary), diagnose sniffer packet (prove which interface traffic actually left by), and diagnose sys virtual-wan-link — the pre-7.x alias that still appears in older docs and some exam material.

🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Read state() and sla() as two independent columns. alive + sla(0x0) is the signature of a brownout and the exact thing routing cannot see.

Every branch that ends in ‘change the configuration’ should be reached by evidence, not by elimination fatigue.
STEP 05

Common Dead Ends and Root Causes

Dead ends

  • Looking for something broken. A brownout is a working link performing badly. There is nothing to find in the logs.
  • Trusting a green health check. It proves your probe, to your target, with your protocol, against your thresholds. Four choices, four ways to agree with the carrier instead of the user.
  • Probing the ISP gateway. Proves the carrier’s router is alive. Says nothing about the path beyond it, which is where congestion lives.
  • Assuming out-of-SLA removed the route. People go hunting for a missing route that was never missing.
  • Tuning thresholds to stop the alarms. Raising latency-threshold to 500ms makes the graph green and the trading desk no happier. The threshold came from the business; changing it is a business conversation.
  • Fixing flap by shortening recoverytime. Backwards. Recovery should be slower than failure.

Likely root causes, ranked

Root causeSignatureFix
Carrier congestion / brownoutalive + sla 0x0, repeating time-of-day patternSteer traffic away; take evidence to the carrier
Probe target too closeExcellent metrics, unhappy usersMove the target past the carrier’s first hop
Probe protocol mismatchedICMP clean, application sufferingProbe with the application’s protocol
Member not in rule’s listHealthy link never usedAdd the member; check ordering
link-cost-factor too broadOut-of-SLA on a metric nobody cared aboutJudge only what matters to that traffic
No gateway on memberNo route generated for that memberSet the gateway — it is a required field
failtime too highUsers feel it for many seconds before failoverfailtime × interval = detection time. Reduce deliberately.
Exam traps in this area: (1) “the route is withdrawn” — it is not; (2) “zone is required on a member” — it is not, interface and gateway are; (3) partial SLA compliance being treated as pass — link-cost-factor is AND; (4) a scenario giving you failtime and interval and expecting seconds — multiply them; (5) adding a member to a zone requiring policy edits — it does not.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
“Nothing changed” is a finding. It points at the environment rather than at your configuration — and it is exactly what a carrier brownout looks like from the inside.

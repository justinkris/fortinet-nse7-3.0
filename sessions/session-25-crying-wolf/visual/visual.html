Crying Wolf

The memory anchors for IPS tuning: the two-edged blade of false positives and false negatives, the envelope-and-letter model of SSL inspection depth, and the packet's journey through flow-based deep inspection.

Anchor 1 · the episode thesis

The two-edged blade

The IPS failed in two opposite directions in one morning. It cried wolf about the market-data feed it could read (false positive), while it could have gone deaf to a real exploit hidden inside encryption (false negative). One blade, two edges.

Anchor 2 · the outage that lies

Every dashboard is green

A false positive is the hardest outage to diagnose because nothing actually broke. The server, network, vendor, and application all report health. Only the IPS security event log tells the truth.

Anchor 3 · depth of inspection

Envelope versus letter

Certificate inspection reads the envelope — the address and postmark (SNI, cert CN/SAN). Full inspection opens it and reads the letter — the decrypted payload. The IPS is a letter-reader, so anything hidden inside encryption always needs full inspection.

Anchor 4 · the packet journey

Flow mode: read it as it streams by

Flow-based full inspection decrypts in-stream and lets the IPS match a pattern as the bytes pass — a small sliding buffer, never the whole file. That's why it's fast enough for a market feed. Antivirus, which must reassemble and hash the whole file, is the one that reaches for proxy mode.

Anchor 5 · the two axes

Depth and mode never collapse

The confusion that catches everyone: assuming "encrypted, therefore proxy." Encryption decides depth. Reassembly decides mode. They are independent axes, and full inspection lives on both modes.

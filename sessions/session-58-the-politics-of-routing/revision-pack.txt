################################################################################
PART 1 — QUICK SUMMARY (ONE PAGE)
################################################################################

WHY BGP EXISTS
Link-state does not scale globally. Not because the map is big — you can buy RAM —
but because every change must reach every router and every router must recompute.
On a global network something is always flapping, and almost all of that
computation produces NO CHANGE in the local forwarding decision. The work isn't
merely expensive, it's wasted: the architecture forces every router to participate
in the details of a problem that isn't theirs.

If Sydney's answer is "the same way out" regardless of Frankfurt's internals, then
Sydney doesn't need Frankfurt's MAP. It needs reachability and direction.

THE THREE FAMILIES
  RIP    distance vector   carries HOW FAR
  OSPF   link state        carries THE MAP
  BGP    path vector       carries WHICH WAY IT CAME (AS_PATH)

BGP throws away the map, uses no hop count, and has no hold-down timers. Loop
prevention is the AS_PATH self-check: reject any route whose AS_PATH already
contains your own AS. This check is LOCAL and INSTANT — no timers, no shared
state — which is exactly what you need once the map is gone.

THE INTERNAL BLIND SPOT
AS_PATH is prepended only when a route LEAVES an AS. Move a route internally and
NOTHING is written. So inside an AS, BGP has: no map, no hop count, no hold-down,
and no working AS_PATH check. Every loop-prevention tool is inoperative.

THE ANSWER: IBGP SPLIT HORIZON
  >>> A prefix learned from an IBGP peer is NEVER advertised to ANY other
      IBGP peer. <<<
Not "not back to the sender" — that's RIP's interface-scoped version and it is
the exam's favourite distractor. BGP's version is peer-type-scoped.
Consequence: a route takes ONE IBGP hop, EVER. You cannot build a circle from
segments of length one, so the loop is IMPOSSIBLE, not detected.

THE EXCEPTION THAT SAVES IT
The rule says nothing about where the route ENTERED. EBGP-learned routes are
freely advertised to IBGP peers — that's a border router's whole job. Only the
SECOND internal hop is blocked.

THE BILL: FULL MESH
Nobody may relay, so everyone hears it first-hand. n(n-1)/2.
8 sites = 28 sessions. The number isn't the point: the NINTH site adds EIGHT
sessions and requires touching EVERY EXISTING ROUTER. A branch turnup becomes a
fleet-wide change window.

THE FAULT: VALID BUT NOT BEST
IBGP PRESERVES the NEXT_HOP unchanged — the border router does not insert itself.
So an internal router is told to send traffic to a loopback in a foreign AS and
cannot reach it. A path with an unresolvable next hop is valid but never best:
it sits in the database forever, never installed, never withdrawn, NEVER LOGGED.
`get router info bgp summary` looks perfect throughout.

FIX: resolve the next hop (IGP/static) or rewrite it (next-hop-self).
next-hop-self goes on the BORDER router, toward IBGP peers, for EBGP-learned
routes. Cost: traffic engineering gone, border becomes a failure domain.

THE SILENT EXPOSURE: ACCIDENTAL TRANSIT
EBGP-learned routes are advertised to OTHER EBGP PEERS by default. Bring up a
second external peer and you silently backhaul traffic between two parties on
behalf of neither — with zero misconfiguration. Prevented ONLY by outbound
filtering (prefix-list-out / route-map-out). NEVER by AS_PATH.

PART 2 — CHEAT SHEET
################################################################################

--- EBGP vs IBGP -------------------------------------------------------------
                          EBGP                    IBGP
  Peers                   Different AS            Same AS
  AS_PATH                 Prepended on exit       Untouched
  Loop prevention         AS_PATH self-check      Split horizon (structural)
  Re-advertise to IBGP?   Yes                     NO
  NEXT_HOP                Set to self on exit     PRESERVED unchanged
  Default TTL             1 (ebgp-enforce-multihop) Multi-hop by nature
  Topology required       Point-to-point          FULL MESH n(n-1)/2

--- AS TYPES (pp.336) --------------------------------------------------------
  Stub         one exit,       local traffic only
  Multihomed   multiple exits, local traffic only     <- LoFinance today
  Transit      multiple exits, local AND other people's traffic (an ISP)
  KEY: multihoming is about NUMBER OF CONNECTIONS.
       transit is about WHOSE TRAFFIC flows through you.

--- FULL MESH SESSION COUNT --------------------------------------------------
  formula   n(n-1)/2
  derive    (n-1)+(n-2)+...+2+1     <- more reliable under pressure
  n=3  -> 3      n=4  -> 6      n=6  -> 15
  n=8  -> 28     n=12 -> 66     n=20 -> 190

--- BGP ATTRIBUTE CATEGORIES (p.343) -----------------------------------------
  Well-known mandatory      ORIGIN, AS_PATH, NEXT_HOP
  Well-known discretionary  LOCAL_PREF, ATOMIC_AGGREGATE
  Optional transitive       AGGREGATOR, COMMUNITY
  Optional non-transitive   MULTI_EXIT_DISC (MED)

--- ROUTE SELECTION TIE-BREAKERS, IN ORDER (p.344) ---------------------------
   1. Highest weight
   2. Highest local preference
   3. Prefer path that originated locally
   4. Shortest AS path
   5. Lowest origin type
   6. Lowest MED
   7. Lowest IGP metric to the BGP next hop
   8. Prefer EBGP over IBGP
   9. If ECMP enabled, insert up to 10 routes
  10. Lowest router ID

--- STATUS CODES (READ THE LEGEND, IT'S PRINTED ON EVERY OUTPUT) -------------
  s suppressed   d damped   h history   * valid   > best   i internal
  *>i  = valid, best, internal          HEALTHY
  *i   = valid, NOT best                FAULT — read the NEXT_HOP column
  absent = never arrived                 filter, split horizon, or not originated

--- COMMANDS -----------------------------------------------------------------
  get router info bgp network                  the DATABASE ("see")
  get router info routing-table bgp            what's INSTALLED ("use")
  get router info routing-table details <nh>   THE DECIDING COMMAND
  get router info bgp neighbors <ip>           remote-as tells you IBGP vs EBGP
  get router info bgp summary                  LOOKS PERFECT DURING THIS FAULT

--- CONFIG: next-hop-self (on the BORDER router, toward IBGP peers) ----------
  config router bgp
      config neighbor
          edit "10.99.60.1"
              set remote-as 65001
              set next-hop-self enable
          next
      end
  end

--- CONFIG: transit prevention (outbound, on every EBGP neighbour) -----------
  config router prefix-list
      edit "LOFI-OWN-PREFIXES"
          config rule
              edit 1
                  set prefix 10.99.0.0 255.255.0.0
                  set action permit
                  set ge 16
                  set le 24
              next
              edit 2
                  set prefix 0.0.0.0 0.0.0.0
                  set action deny
                  set le 32
              next
          end
      next
  end

  config router bgp
      config neighbor
          edit "172.20.0.1"
              set remote-as 65300
              set prefix-list-out "LOFI-OWN-PREFIXES"
          next
      end
  end

--- IMPORTANT DEFAULTS -------------------------------------------------------
  BGP originates NOTHING by default (need network or redistribute)
  BGP ACCEPTS everything by default (filter inbound if you care)
  BGP ADVERTISES EBGP-learned routes to other EBGP peers by default (= transit)
  A DEFAULT ROUTE does NOT satisfy next-hop resolution by default on FortiGate
  No hardcoded limits on neighbors/routes/policies — system memory is the limit

--- DESIGN RULES -------------------------------------------------------------
  * Never rely on AS_PATH to prevent transit. Filter outbound. Always.
  * Apply outbound filters BEFORE the second external peering exists, not after.
  * next-hop-self is right when there is no alternative path anyway. It is wrong
    in a genuine multi-exit topology — you'd be discarding paths you paid for.
  * Full mesh is an operational ceiling, not a technical one. Count the change
    window, not the sessions.


################################################################################
PART 3 — FLASHCARDS
################################################################################

Q: What does BGP carry that OSPF and RIP do not?
A: AS_PATH — the ordered list of autonomous systems the route has already
   crossed. Hence "path vector."

Q: Why can't link-state scale to the internet?
A: Every change must reach every router and every router must recompute SPF —
   and almost all of that computation produces no change in the forwarding
   decision. The work is wasted, not just expensive.

Q: How does BGP prevent loops between autonomous systems?
A: A router rejects any route whose AS_PATH already contains its own AS number.
   Local, instant, needs no shared state or timers.

Q: When is the AS number prepended to AS_PATH?
A: Only when the route LEAVES the AS (EBGP advertisement). Never internally.

Q: Why does AS_PATH fail as loop prevention inside an AS?
A: Nothing is prepended internally, so the attribute never changes and the
   self-check has nothing to detect.

Q: State the IBGP split horizon rule exactly.
A: A prefix learned from an IBGP peer is never advertised to ANY other IBGP peer.

Q: How does IBGP split horizon differ from RIP's split horizon?
A: RIP is interface-scoped and directional (don't send back out the interface you
   learned it on). IBGP is peer-type-scoped: it goes nowhere internally at all.

Q: Why does split horizon make loops impossible rather than merely detectable?
A: A route can take only one IBGP hop. You cannot form a circle from segments of
   length one.

Q: If IBGP routes can't be relayed, how does anything propagate internally?
A: EBGP-learned routes ARE freely advertised to IBGP peers. The rule only blocks
   the SECOND internal hop.

Q: What topology does split horizon force inside an AS?
A: Full mesh — every IBGP speaker peered directly with every other.

Q: How many IBGP sessions for n speakers? For 8?
A: n(n-1)/2. For 8: 28.

Q: What is the real operational cost of full mesh?
A: Adding the ninth site adds eight sessions and requires touching every existing
   router. A branch turnup becomes a fleet-wide change window.

Q: What happens to NEXT_HOP when a route is passed over IBGP?
A: Nothing — it is preserved unchanged. The border router does not insert itself.

Q: A prefix shows "*i" but not ">". There is only one path. What does that mean?
A: It is not losing a selection contest — it is INELIGIBLE. Almost always an
   unresolvable next hop.

Q: What must be true before a BGP path can be installed?
A: The router must have a route in its routing table to that path's NEXT_HOP.

Q: Why is an unresolvable next hop so hard to spot in production?
A: Nothing fails. Session Established, prefix present and valid, no log, no alarm,
   no down state. `get router info bgp summary` looks perfect.

Q: Does a default route satisfy next-hop resolution on FortiGate?
A: No — not by default. Engineers see 0.0.0.0/0 in the output and wrongly assume
   the next hop resolves.

Q: Where is next-hop-self applied, toward whom, and for which routes?
A: On the BORDER router, toward its IBGP peers, for routes learned via EBGP.

Q: What are the two costs of next-hop-self?
A: Traffic engineering is lost (all traffic must transit the border), and the
   border router becomes a concentrated failure domain.

Q: When is next-hop-self the WRONG choice?
A: In a genuine multi-exit topology, where internal routers had better direct
   paths you'd be discarding.

Q: What is the cost of the alternative (static/IGP routes to the next hop)?
A: Foreign addressing leaks into your internal routing — you edit statics at every
   site whenever the peer renumbers.

Q: How does an AS become a transit AS by accident?
A: EBGP-learned routes are advertised to other EBGP peers by default. Add a second
   external peer and you silently backhaul their traffic, with no misconfiguration.

Q: What prevents accidental transit — and what does NOT?
A: Outbound route filtering (prefix-list-out / route-map-out) prevents it.
   AS_PATH does NOT — it prevents loops, which is a different problem.

Q: Difference between a multihomed AS and a transit AS?
A: Multihomed = multiple external connections, still only your own traffic.
   Transit = you carry traffic that neither originates nor terminates with you.

Q: Which attributes are well-known mandatory?
A: ORIGIN, AS_PATH, NEXT_HOP. (LOCAL_PREF is discretionary; MED is optional
   non-transitive.)

Q: Two commands that separate "arrived and rejected" from "never arrived"?
A: get router info bgp network (database) vs
   get router info routing-table bgp (installed).

Q: The single command that ends a valid-but-not-best investigation?
A: get router info routing-table details <next-hop-address>

Q: What does a route sitting in the BGP database rule out immediately?
A: Every advertisement-side hypothesis — split horizon, outbound filters at the
   peer, session state, route reflectors. Telling worked. The fault is local.

Q: Does BGP originate any prefix by default?
A: No. You must use `network` or `redistribute`.

Q: Does BGP accept received prefixes by default?
A: Yes — all of them. Filtering inbound is optional and configured.

Produce a single-host technical storytelling podcast episode, approximately 12-15
minutes, for a show called "The Packet Path." The host is warm, dry, a little
wry, and speaks to an intelligent engineer who does not need to be flattered. Use

EPISODE TITLE: "The Politics of Routing"

COLD OPEN:
Open on a whiteboard wall in a Sydney office, Tuesday morning, 9:14am. Someone has
drawn two circles overnight — Sydney and Singapore — with a line between them and
the number 179 written above it in handwriting nobody recognises. A senior engineer
the team calls Señor is standing in front of it holding a coffee he has clearly
forgotten about, because it is no longer steaming and he is still holding it at
drinking height. He says, to nobody in particular: "OSPF is a family sharing one
map. Everyone sees the same house. Everyone agrees where the kitchen is. If you
move the kitchen, everyone's map updates and everyone believes you — because you
are family." Then, after a beat: "BGP is nations negotiating treaties. Everyone has
a map. Everyone is lying about theirs. Politely."
A speakerphone on the wall crackles. It has been live the whole time. Javier Lin,
in Singapore, four hours ahead: "And I am a nation."

ACT ONE — THE QUESTION NOBODY ANSWERED FOR SIX MINUTES:
A junior engineer arrives late holding a muffin and asks the best question of the
year: "Why don't we just run OSPF everywhere? OSPF works. We built the whole
company on it." Nobody answers for six minutes.
Explain, conversationally, why the question deserves that silence. Walk through
what link-state actually requires: every router holding an identical database,
every change reaching every router, every router recomputing. Then land the real
insight — the one that matters more than "it's too big." On a global network,
something is always flapping. A fibre cut in Brazil. A reboot in Frankfurt. And a
router in Sydney burning CPU to recompute because of Frankfurt will arrive at
exactly the forwarding decision it already had. The computation is not merely
expensive. It is WASTED. The architecture forces every router to participate in
the details of a problem that isn't theirs.
Then the pivot: if the answer is the same regardless of Frankfurt's internals, you
don't need Frankfurt's map. You need reachability and direction.
Introduce the three-family taxonomy as a memorable triple: RIP tells you how far.
OSPF tells you the whole map. BGP tells you which way it came. Distance, map,
path. Emphasise why the AS_PATH loop check is elegant: it's local and instant. No
timers, no hold-downs, no waiting for the network to agree. One router, one
attribute, one decision. Which is exactly what you need when you've deliberately
thrown away the shared map.

ACT TWO — THE BLIND SPOT:
Now the twist that drives the episode. AS_PATH is prepended when a route LEAVES an
AS. Cross a border, get stamped. But move a route INSIDE an AS — from headquarters
to the disaster-recovery site, same company, same AS number — and nothing is
written. The passport stays closed.
Make the listener feel how bad this is. Inside an AS, BGP has no map, no hop count,
no hold-down timers, and now no working AS_PATH check. Every loop-prevention tool
it owns is inoperative.
Reveal the answer as the only remaining option, and make it feel blunt rather than
clever: a prefix learned from an IBGP peer is never advertised to any other IBGP
peer. Not "not back to the sender" — that's the RIP version, and it's the wrong
answer on the exam. It goes NOWHERE internally.
Then the beautiful part: this doesn't detect loops, it makes them impossible. A
route takes one internal hop, ever. You cannot build a circle out of segments of
length one.
Use Señor's line here: "EBGP says: I will tell you where it has been. IBGP says:
I will not tell anyone at all."
Then the bill. If nobody may relay, everybody must hear it first-hand. Full mesh.
Eight sites is twenty-eight sessions. But make clear the number isn't the lesson —
the ninth office adds eight sessions and requires touching every existing router.
Use the line: "Every new office is a phone call to every old office. The ninth
office costs eight conversations, and you cannot skip any of them."

ACT THREE — THE FAULT THAT NEVER RANG AN ALARM:
The business problem. The operations manager, Penny, reports that Singapore's
routes are visible at headquarters but Melbourne cannot reach a single one of
them. A help-desk engineer named Riley supplies the sentence that turns out to be
the entire diagnosis: "I can SEE the route. I ran the command. It's THERE. Why
isn't it a route I can USE?"
Walk the listener through the investigation the way an engineer would actually do
it. The database versus the routing table. The status codes legend — printed on
every output, and worth reading rather than remembering. Star means valid. The
angle bracket means best. Only the best path is offered to the routing table.
Land the deduction slowly: there is only ONE path to this prefix, and it isn't
best. So it isn't losing a competition. It is ineligible.
Then the reveal. IBGP preserves the next hop unchanged — headquarters did not
insert itself. Melbourne is being told to send traffic to a loopback address in
Singapore, in another country, in another AS. And Melbourne has no route to it.
Make the point that this is why the fault is so nasty: nothing fails. The session
is Established. The prefix is present and valid. There is no log, no alarm, no
down state. The summary command looks perfect. And the service is broken.
Give the fix and its cost honestly. next-hop-self, applied on the border router,
toward internal peers, for externally-learned routes. And what it costs: traffic
engineering is gone, and the border becomes a failure domain. Señor's line: "Next-
hop-self is a promise. Do not make it unless you can keep it."

THE MISTAKE (this replaces the usual junior's blunder — be explicit that this one
belongs to the intermediate engineer, and be generous about it):
Mid-investigation, staring at the right evidence, the engineer reached for the
wrong answer: "Do we need headquarters to be a route reflector?" It's a genuinely
understandable guess, because the symptom SHAPE fits — one router has something
another can't use. But the evidence already on screen had ruled it out. The route
was in the database. It had already arrived. Advertisement worked fine. Route
reflectors change who is allowed to TELL — they cannot help a router resolve an
address it can't reach.
Draw the general lesson, because it's the most valuable thing in the episode: most
bad diagnoses aren't wrong facts. They're correct facts applied to the wrong
evidence. So before offering a hypothesis, ask what the output you already have
has ruled OUT. A prefix sitting in the database eliminates every theory about
telling, in one move.
And note, warmly, that the same engineer had — twenty minutes earlier — caught
himself mid-sentence, tested a claim against a fact he already held, and reversed
himself unprompted. Same session, both behaviours. The skill is there. The habit
isn't yet. That gap is what improvement actually looks like.

CLOSING — THE DOOR NOBODY OPENED:
End on the exposure. Bring up a second external peer — London — with a perfectly
correct configuration and no mistake anywhere, and headquarters starts telling
London how to reach Singapore, and Singapore how to reach London. The company is
now carrying traffic between two parties on behalf of neither. Its circuits have
become the backhaul for a conversation that has nothing to do with it.
Señor's line: "Nobody attacked you. Nobody misconfigured anything. You simply did
not say no, and BGP took that as yes."
The defence is outbound filtering. Advertise only what you originate. And note
that it was applied BEFORE London existed rather than after — which is the whole
difference between engineering and cleanup.
Close with the principle: defaults have a direction. BGP's default is to tell. Not
telling is the thing you configure.

TEASER:
Señor writes the number 28 on a sticky note and puts it on the corner of Penny's
laptop. "That is how many phone calls," he says. "Brisbane makes it thirty-six."
Riley asks the question that starts the next episode: "So if headquarters already
knows everything — why can't it just tell everyone else? Why does everyone have to
hear it themselves?"
And Javier, still on the speakerphone, having said nothing for forty minutes:
"Because nobody told them they were allowed to listen to a reflection."
Señor pulls a worn diagram out of a drawer. Three boxes on it: RIB-in. Local RIB.
RIB-out. "Next time," he says, "we stop asking what BGP knows. We ask where it
keeps it."
End the episode there. Do not explain route reflectors.

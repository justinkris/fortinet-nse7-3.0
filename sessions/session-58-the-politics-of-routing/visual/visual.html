RECOMMENDED VISUAL STYLE

Why Packet Journey & Border Crossing

Session 58 has an unusual property: almost every concept is about a boundary and what changes when you cross it. AS_PATH is written at a border. NEXT_HOP is rewritten at a border. Split horizon is a rule about which borders a route may cross and how many times. Transit is what happens when a route crosses two borders it shouldn’t.

A pure topology diagram would flatten all of that into boxes and lines. A packet journey through border checkpoints keeps the transformation visible — you see the passport getting stamped, you see the guard refusing a second internal crossing, you see the address on the envelope being rewritten. The mechanism is the picture.

Alternatives considered and rejected: a Memory Palace would fragment concepts that are causally chained; a Troubleshooting Decision Tree already exists as a separate artefact; a Layer-by-Layer breakdown doesn’t fit — nothing today is layered, everything is sequential and territorial.

🧠
MENTAL NOTE
If you remember only the image of a passport being stamped at the border and left untouched inside the country, you can rebuild split horizon, next-hop-self, and the full-mesh requirement from first principles.

The Two Doctrines — One Map vs Many Treaties

Purpose

Anchors the season’s central contrast before any mechanism is introduced. This is the image that should surface whenever the question is ‘why does BGP exist at all?’

Image Generation Prompt

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork. Warm cream background (#faf5e9). Coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework. No gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D rendering. Generous spacing. Professional but storybook-like, legible enough to function as an educational diagram. Split composition, two halves divided by a vertical seam. LEFT HALF: a warm domestic scene — four or five friendly router characters (simple rounded devices with small eyes and stubby antennae) sitting around a large kitchen table, ALL of them leaning over ONE enormous shared paper map spread across the table. Every router has an identical small copy of the same map in its hands. A cosy lamp, teacups, sage green tones. Label above in clean sans-serif: "OSPF — ONE MAP, ONE FAMILY". A small caption ribbon: "correctness from agreement". RIGHT HALF: a formal diplomatic scene — three router characters seated at separate negotiating desks, each behind a small flag on a stand, each holding its own DIFFERENT folded map held close to the chest, deliberately angled away from the others. Between them, sealed envelopes with wax stamps being passed by a courier. Coral and muted gold tones, more formal architecture with columns suggested by simple lines. Label above: "BGP — MANY MAPS, MANY TREATIES". Caption ribbon: "correctness from provenance". One of the diplomat routers wears a small sombrero. Another has a tiny Singapore flag. The seam between halves is a simple vertical line, not a dramatic crack. LoFinance logo (stacks of banknotes frozen inside a clear ice cube) small in the bottom-left corner. MUST NOT APPEAR: any technical labels like AS numbers or IP addresses, any packet or envelope icons on the left half, any hostility or conflict imagery — the right half is formal and polite, not adversarial.
Aspect ratio
16:9
Placement
Top of the visual page, immediately after the style rationale.
Accessibility
Split illustration: routers sharing one map around a kitchen table on the left, routers at separate diplomatic desks with private maps on the right.
Memory anchor
OSPF shares a map; BGP exchanges treaties. Everything else follows from this.

The Border Stamp — Where AS_PATH Is Written

Purpose

Makes the single most important asymmetry of the session physically visible: the passport is stamped at the border and NOT stamped inside the country. Every downstream concept depends on this.

Image Generation Prompt

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork. Warm cream background (#faf5e9). Coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework. No gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D rendering. Generous spacing. Professional but storybook-like, legible enough to function as an educational diagram. A single continuous horizontal journey read left to right. A friendly envelope character (simple rectangular envelope with small eyes, walking on two short legs, carrying a small passport booklet) travels along a path. STAGE 1, far left: a territory labelled with a simple banner "AS 65300 — SINGAPORE", warm sage tones, a small stylised skyline. STAGE 2: a border checkpoint — a proper barrier gate with a booth, a uniformed router-character officer holding a large rubber stamp, coral accents. The officer is pressing the stamp firmly onto the envelope's open passport, and a clear new entry reading "65300" appears on the passport page. Small sign on the booth: "EBGP — STAMP REQUIRED". STAGE 3: the envelope now inside a large territory labelled "AS 65001 — LOFINANCE", muted gold border line enclosing two buildings labelled simply HQ and MEL. STAGE 4, critically: between HQ and MEL there is NO checkpoint, NO barrier, NO officer — just an open road with a small sign reading "IBGP — NO STAMP". The envelope walks this stretch with its passport CLOSED and unchanged, and a small speech bubble from the envelope says "still says 65300". A magnifying glass hovers over the passport at this point showing the page has exactly one entry. MUST NOT APPEAR: any second stamp anywhere inside AS 65001, any IP addresses, any routing table imagery.
Aspect ratio
16:9
Placement
Second image, introducing the AS_PATH asymmetry.
Accessibility
An envelope character has its passport stamped 65300 at an EBGP border checkpoint, then walks between two internal sites past a sign reading no stamp, passport unchanged.
Memory anchor
The passport is stamped only at the border. Inside the country, nothing is written — which is why the loop check goes blind.

One Hop, Ever — Split Horizon as a Physical Barrier

Purpose

Converts the split-horizon rule from a sentence to be memorised into a spatial fact to be seen. The visual asymmetry between the legal and blocked crossings is the whole lesson.

Image Generation Prompt

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork. Warm cream background (#faf5e9). Coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework. No gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D rendering. Generous spacing. Professional but storybook-like, legible enough to function as an educational diagram. A large rounded territory boundary in muted gold occupies most of the frame, labelled "AS 65001". Inside it, three router characters arranged in a triangle: HQ (top, slightly larger, wearing a small sombrero, positioned near the territory's edge), MEL (bottom left), BNE (bottom right). OUTSIDE the boundary to the upper right, a fourth router labelled SG with a small flag, connected to HQ by a gold dashed line crossing the boundary, labelled "EBGP". From HQ, two confident coral arrows run to MEL and to BNE, each drawn as a solid clean line with a small green tick mark and the word "ALLOWED". Between MEL and BNE, an arrow is drawn but interrupted mid-way by a solid brick wall with a friendly but firm router-character guard standing in front of it, one hand raised palm-out; the arrow is greyed and dashed after the wall. A sign on the wall reads "NO SECOND HOP". A small thought bubble above the guard: "you heard it from inside — you may not pass it on". At the bottom of the frame, a small separate inset panel shows three circular arrows attempting to form a ring but each segment is drawn as a single short stub that cannot connect, with the caption "a circle needs segments longer than one". MUST NOT APPEAR: any AS_PATH or passport imagery (that is image 02's job), any next-hop addresses, any suggestion that HQ→MEL is blocked.
Aspect ratio
3:2
Placement
Third image, immediately following the passport stamp.
Accessibility
Inside an AS boundary, arrows from HQ to MEL and BNE are marked allowed, while an arrow between MEL and BNE is stopped by a wall and a guard reading no second hop.
Memory anchor
A route takes one IBGP hop, ever. Loops are impossible, not detected.

The Ninth Office — Why 28 Is an Operational Number

Purpose

Prevents the full-mesh formula from being remembered as arithmetic. The lesson is what adding one site does to your change window, and that is a picture of eight simultaneous phone calls.

Image Generation Prompt

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork. Warm cream background (#faf5e9). Coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework. No gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D rendering. Generous spacing. Professional but storybook-like, legible enough to function as an educational diagram. Composition in two contrasting panels, left small and right large. LEFT PANEL: three router characters connected by three simple lines, relaxed postures, caption "3 sites — 3 calls". RIGHT PANEL, dominant: eight router characters arranged in a circle, each holding an old-fashioned telephone handset to its ear, connected by a dense but still-legible web of twenty-eight cream-coloured lines. In the centre of the circle, the number "28" rendered large in muted gold serif. Now the key element: a NINTH router character stands just outside the circle, newly arrived, holding a small cardboard box of belongings and looking slightly apprehensive, with EIGHT bright coral lines fanning out from it toward every one of the existing eight — and every one of the existing eight routers is drawn mid-turn, looking toward the newcomer with its telephone raised, interrupted. A small banner across the lower third reads: "the ninth office costs eight conversations". Below that, in smaller text on a torn-paper strip: "and you must touch every router to do it". Penny (a professional woman character with a tablet, calm, business attire) stands at the edge of the frame observing, one eyebrow raised. MUST NOT APPEAR: route reflector imagery of any kind, any hint of the Session 59 solution, mirror or reflection motifs.
Aspect ratio
16:9
Placement
Fourth image, closing the split-horizon consequence arc.
Accessibility

Memory anchor

Valid But Not Best — The Envelope Nobody Can Deliver

Purpose

The session's core troubleshooting fault. Shows that the route is genuinely present and genuinely correct, and still undeliverable — which is exactly why nothing is ever logged.

Image Generation Prompt

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork. Warm cream background (#faf5e9). Coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework. No gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D rendering. Generous spacing. Professional but storybook-like, legible enough to function as an educational diagram. A postal sorting-room scene. LEFT SIDE: a large open filing cabinet or pigeonhole rack labelled "BGP DATABASE — what I have learned", containing two neat envelopes, each visibly clean and intact, each bearing a small green tick and a small tag reading "valid". CENTRE: a router-character postal clerk (Melbourne, small MEL nameplate on the counter) holding one of these envelopes up and squinting at the delivery address, which is written large and legible: "DELIVER TO: 172.20.0.1". The clerk's expression is puzzled rather than alarmed. Above the clerk, a large empty out-tray labelled "ROUTING TABLE — what I can actually use" with visible dust and a small tumbleweed, completely empty. RIGHT SIDE: a wall map the clerk is consulting, and on this map the location 172.20.0.1 is simply NOT DRAWN — a blank cream area with a small question mark, while everything else on the map is detailed. A small sign hangs above the whole scene: "* valid    > best" with the > symbol conspicuously crossed out in coral. In the lower corner, a wall-mounted alarm bell is drawn silent and still, with a small label "no alarm — nothing failed". Riley (a help-desk character wearing a headset) leans in through a doorway saying, in a speech bubble: "I can SEE it. Why can't I USE it?". MUST NOT APPEAR: any broken, torn, or damaged envelope — the route is perfectly valid; any error messages or red warning imagery.
Aspect ratio
3:2
Placement
Fifth image, opening the troubleshooting half of the page.
Accessibility
A postal clerk holds a valid envelope addressed to 172.20.0.1 while consulting a map on which that address does not appear; the outgoing tray is empty and the alarm bell is silent.
Memory anchor
Seeing is the database, using is resolution. A valid route with an unreachable next hop fails silently forever.

Next-Hop-Self Is a Promise — and Accidental Transit

Purpose

Pairs the fix with its cost, and the cost with the exposure it creates. These belong in one frame because both are consequences of the same default: BGP tells unless told not to.

Image Generation Prompt

Flat minimalistic but highly detailed illustrated vector artwork. Warm cream background (#faf5e9). Coral accents, muted gold highlights, sage greens, soft brown outlines, clean bold linework. No gradients, no photorealism, no glossy 3D rendering. Generous spacing. Professional but storybook-like, legible enough to function as an educational diagram. Composition split into an upper band and a lower band. UPPER BAND — THE PROMISE: HQ router character (sombrero) stands in a doorway of the AS 65001 boundary, arms open, addressing MEL who stands inside. A large speech bubble from HQ reads: "AIM AT ME". A coral arrow runs from MEL to HQ, and beyond HQ a gold dashed line continues out to SG. Beneath HQ's feet, drawn subtly in soft brown outline, is a small pedestal with a hairline crack in it and a tiny label "single failure domain". To one side, a small greyed-out alternate road from MEL heading directly toward SG is shown with a barrier and the label "no longer available — traffic engineering lost". Caption ribbon across the band: "next-hop-self is a promise — do not make it unless you can keep it". LOWER BAND — THE EXPOSURE: the same HQ character now standing at a junction between two separate gates, one labelled SG and one labelled LON. Without doing anything active — HQ is drawn shrugging, palms up, expression innocent — a stream of small envelopes flows from the SG gate, straight past HQ, and out the LON gate, and back again. The envelopes are drawn in a neutral colour, clearly not addressed to LoFinance. A small toll booth beside the flow has its window shuttered and a sign reading "nobody paid for this". At the far right of the lower band, a simple gate labelled "prefix-list-out" is shown being lowered, cleanly stopping the pass-through flow while local envelopes continue unaffected. Caption ribbon: "you did not say no, and BGP took that as yes". MUST NOT APPEAR: any depiction of an attack, intrusion, or malicious actor — the transit is entirely accidental and must read as innocent.

HOW TO USE THIS PAGE

The order is the argument

These six images are a chain, not a gallery. Each one is the reason the next one exists:

01 Two doctrines -> WHY BGP exists at all 02 The border stamp -> therefore AS_PATH is written only at borders 03 One hop, ever -> therefore the internal loop check is blind, so split horizon 04 The ninth office -> therefore full mesh, and therefore an operational ceiling 05 Valid but not best -> and meanwhile the next hop was never rewritten 06 Two doors -> so you make a promise, and you close a door you never opened

If you can retell that chain in six sentences without notes, you have the session. If any link breaks, that is the artefact to revisit — images 02 and 03 carry the conceptual load, image 05 carries the troubleshooting load.

MENTAL NOTE
Retelling beats rereading. Walk the six images in order out loud once, and the parts you skip are precisely the parts you don’t yet own.

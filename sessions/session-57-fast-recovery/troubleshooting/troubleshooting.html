SESSION 57 · TROUBLESHOOTING · BGP CONVERGENCE

Troubleshooting Slow Failover

How a senior engineer diagnoses detection latency, half-configured BFD, and the graceful restart conflict.

Most BFD incidents are not BFD misbehaving. They are BFD never having started.

Configured and Up are different facts. The gap between them is where the incidents live.

SymptomsQuestionsCommandsDecision TreeTraps
STEP 01

Symptoms — What You Are Actually Told

Nobody reports "BFD is not Up." They report business symptoms, and the translation from symptom to hypothesis is the skill.

What you hearWhat it might mean
"We lost trades for about a minute and a half"Hold timer expiry — detection never happened faster
"About half the sessions dropped"ECMP with one dead next-hop. Partial failure, not total
"The carrier says the link is up"Layer 1 fine, far-end control plane dead. The classic BFD case
"It drops for a few seconds at random"Over-aggressive detection on a jittery path — a false positive, not a failure
"Everything reroutes when we patch the cluster"Graceful restart missing or not negotiated
"We enabled BFD and nothing changed"Half-configured, or the peer never joined the session
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
A partial outage is harder to detect than a total one. 'The market was weird for a minute' is what 50% packet loss sounds like from a trading desk.
STEP 02

Questions Before Commands

  • Was this detection or reconvergence? If recovery was fast once it started, the fault is in detection. Do not tune route selection to fix a detection problem.
  • How much broke — all or half? Half points at ECMP and one dead path. All points at the session, the tunnel, or the box.
  • Is there anywhere to reconverge to? If not, faster detection changes nothing about availability.
  • What is behind this peer — a single box, or a cluster? Determines whether a drop is more likely a death or a shift change.
  • Do I control both ends? If not, some of the fix is a conversation, not a command.
  • How many hops between the peers? Single-hop and multihop BFD are different features with different tolerances.
The ordering principle: verify the detector is alive before blaming the thing it was supposed to be watching. Most BFD incidents are not BFD misbehaving — they are BFD never having started.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Configured and Up are different facts. The gap between them is where almost every BFD incident lives.
STEP 03

Verification Commands and Expected Output

The first command, always
get router info bfd neighbor

What you want to see: the peer listed with state UP, with the negotiated transmit and receive intervals and the detect multiplier. What tells you the story: the peer absent entirely (BFD never enabled on the interface, or no template match for a multihop peer), or present but DOWN/INIT — which means your side is transmitting and the far end is not completing the handshake.

Negotiated capabilities — did graceful restart actually get agreed?
get router info bgp neighbors 100.64.1.254

Look for the capability list and the graceful restart entry. A locally-configured capability that does not appear as negotiated means the far end declined or never offered it — the same quiet-failure shape as one-sided BFD.

Confirm both halves of the BFD config
show router bgp | grep -A3 "edit \"100.64.1.254\""
show system interface port1
Is there anything to reconverge onto?
get router info routing-table bgp
Dead end to avoid: running diagnose sniffer packet on port 3784 before you have checked get router info bfd neighbor. You will spend twenty minutes proving that your side transmits — which you already knew — instead of ten seconds establishing that the session state is DOWN because the peer never answered.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
get router info bfd neighbor is the first command, not the fifth. It answers 'is my detector alive?' before you investigate anything it detects.

STEP 05

Dead Ends and Exam Traps

  • Tuning BGP timers to fix slow failover. Technically it works and it is almost always the wrong answer — both in production and on the exam. Expect it as a distractor.
  • Assuming configured means running. The single most productive mistake to stop making. BFD in the config with a DOWN session is worse than no BFD, because it produces a false sense of closure on a risk register.
  • Only one of the two BFD locations. Neighbour without interface, or interface without neighbour. Snippet questions love this.
  • Only one of the two graceful restart commands. Same shape, different feature.
  • Treating BFD as an availability control on a single-homed peer. It is monitoring. Say so.
  • Enabling aggressive BFD on a peer behind an HA cluster. You will detect routine failovers as deaths and reroute away from a healthy site. BFD overrules graceful restart.
  • Forgetting who is slower. A hardware-accelerated box peering with a VM cannot set the cadence unilaterally. The slower peer sets the floor.
The senior-engineer version of this whole page: before changing anything, establish whether the problem was detection or reconvergence; then establish whether your detector is alive; then establish whether anyone else has to act. Most of the time the command you need is the first one, and the fix is a phone call.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
A fix that fails quietly, while wearing the clothes of a fix, is worse than no fix — because you stop looking.

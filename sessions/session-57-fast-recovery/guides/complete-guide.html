Fast Recovery

Optimising BGP convergence — timers, BFD, and graceful restart. EF Study Guide 05, pp. 147–151.

A ninety-one second gap cost six figures, and every light in the building was green.

Detection and reconvergence are different problems. This session is about the first one, which is almost always the expensive one.

💭THINK FIRST · THE NOTICING

At 06:41:07 the far-end router at Parramatta died. At 06:42:38 BGP withdrew the routes. In between, half of LoFinance's trades went into a road that ended. Yet the reconvergence onto Chatswood, when it finally happened, took less than a second. Sit with that gap before reading on.

  • If failover onto the second carrier was sub-second, what exactly consumed the other ninety seconds?
  • Riley's dashboard was entirely green throughout. Every light was reporting honestly. Why was every light useless?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Detection. BGP had no idea anything was wrong. The routing table still held Parramatta as a valid next-hop because BGP's only evidence of death is the absence of a keepalive for a full hold interval — 180 seconds by default. Reconvergence was never the bottleneck. Noticing was.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Because they were watching the wrong layer. Interface state reports on the optics and the local port. The far-end router's control plane had died while its line card kept the laser lit. Every green light was truthfully answering a question nobody needed the answer to.
SECTION 01

The Failover Was Instant. The Noticing Was Not.

Friday 06:41. Ninety-one seconds of silence, then a sub-second recovery.

The incident that opened Session 57 is the one every network engineer eventually meets, and it is almost never the failure people prepare for. Nobody cut the fibre. Nobody unplugged anything. The optics stayed lit, the port stayed up, and the monitoring system — which was working perfectly — reported that everything was fine.

What actually died was the control plane of the router at the far end. The line card kept transmitting light. Layer 1 was pristine. But the device behind it had stopped being able to forward anything, and there was no mechanism anywhere in the path whose job it was to ask "is the machine at the other end still thinking?"

Baz's line, and why it was both true and worthless: "Mate, the link is up." He was right. The link was up. Link state is a statement about the physical medium and the local port, and it will remain cheerfully green across a total software failure of the device on the other side.

The arithmetic of the loss is worth stating plainly, because it is the shape of the business case. With ECMP enabled across two carriers, roughly half of outbound flows were hashed onto the dead next-hop. Not all — half. A total outage announces itself in seconds because everything stops. A fifty-percent outage looks, from the trading desk, like the market being strange for a minute and a half.

💭THINK FIRST · TIMERS

BGP is not slow by accident. Its patience is a designed property, and understanding why it was designed that way is what stops you from 'fixing' it with a change that makes everything worse.

  • What is BGP's only evidence that its neighbour is still alive — and therefore its only evidence of death?
  • The obvious fix is to set keepalive to 1 and hold to 3. Failure detected in three seconds. Why does nobody recommend this?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
A KEEPALIVE message arriving on a schedule. FortiGate defaults: keepalive every 60 seconds, hold timer 180 seconds — a deliberate 3:1 ratio, so that two consecutive keepalives can be lost to congestion or a brief control-plane hiccup without tearing down a perfectly healthy session. BGP's evidence of life is a scheduled message; its evidence of death is the absence of that message for a long time.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Two reasons, and both matter. Flap risk: aggressive timers reclassify non-failures as failures. A teardown is expensive — every route from that peer withdrawn, the withdrawal propagated onward, best-path recalculated, FIB rewritten, then the entire table re-advertised on recovery. CPU: BGP runs in the routing daemon, in software, on the CPU — it is not offloaded to the NP. Multiply sixty-times-more-frequent processing across every peering and you degrade convergence precisely when routes are churning and CPU is already scarce.
SECTION 02

Why You Cannot Simply Turn the Timers Down

The first instinct of every engineer who meets this problem is to reach for the timers. It is not a stupid instinct — the hold timer really is what cost ninety seconds. But it is a trade, not a fix, and it is a bad trade.

TimerFortiOS defaultWhat it governs
keepalive60 secondsHow often this router announces it is still alive
holdtime180 secondsHow long silence is tolerated before the peer is declared dead
ratio3 : 1Two keepalives may be lost without consequence
FortiOS CLI — the timers you should usually leave alone
config router bgp
    config neighbor
        edit 100.64.1.254
            set keep-alive-timer 60
            set holdtime-timer 180
        next
    end
end
Analogy

Asking a slow man to run.

  • BGP is deliberately unhurried because tearing down a session is expensive and reversing that decision is more expensive still.
  • Make it check sixty times more often and it does not become alert — it becomes tired, and it makes worse decisions under exactly the load conditions where good decisions matter.
  • The answer is not to make BGP faster. It is to give BGP a separate, cheap messenger whose only job is to sprint.
Exam tip: When a scenario describes slow failover, the tempting answer is a timer value. It is usually the wrong one. The better answers involve BFD not being configured, or being configured in only one of the two required places.

💭THINK FIRST · BFD

If BGP cannot be the fast detector, something else must be. Before reading the answer, specify the requirements yourself — where should it run, how heavy can each check be, and what must it do once it decides the peer is gone?

  • What three properties must a fast failure detector have to solve the Friday problem?
  • The name is Bidirectional Forwarding Detection. What does that first syllable cost you operationally, and what is the failure mode if you get it wrong?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
It must run below BGP and independent of the media, asking whether the far-end device answers rather than whether the interface is up. Each check must be lightweight — a small fixed-format packet doing exactly one job — because that is what makes sub-second cadence affordable. And it must tell BGP to act: BFD's value is not noticing, it is causing the peering to drop and the routes to be withdrawn immediately rather than after the hold timer.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
BFD is a negotiated session between two state machines (Down → Init → Up), not a probe one side fires at the other. Both ends must run it. If the far end does not, the BFD session never reaches Up — and here is the danger: BGP does not care. The peering stays Established, routes flow, the dashboard stays green, and you have closed the risk in your register while remaining on a 180-second hold timer.
SECTION 03

Bidirectional Forwarding Detection — The Seatbelt

Separate the liveness question from the routing protocol, and it becomes cheap enough to ask constantly.

Do NOT include any human characters, any vendor logos, any real product photography, or dense packet-header field diagrams. Purpose: make permanent the idea that BFD separates the liveness question from the routing protocol.

The study guide's own description is worth memorising almost verbatim: BFD acts like a probe checking the status of the BGP peer, it is independent of the media type, and it detects a one-way device failure in less than a second. That phrase — one-way device failure — is Friday morning compressed into four words.

Notice what BFD did not invent. It uses the same logic BGP uses: send at an interval, multiply by three, declare death. The insight is not a smarter algorithm. It is that once you strip the liveness question out of the heavyweight routing protocol and give it its own tiny fixed-format packet, you can afford to ask it two hundred and forty times more often.

Configuration — two places, both required

FortiOS CLI — per neighbour
config router bgp
    config neighbor
        edit 100.64.1.254
            set bfd enable
        next
    end
end
FortiOS CLI — per interface (the half people forget)
config system interface
    edit "port1"
        set bfd enable
        set bfd-desired-min-tx 250
        set bfd-required-min-rx 250
        set bfd-detect-mult 3
    next
end
ParameterDefaultMeaning
bfd-desired-min-tx250 msHow often this end wants to transmit
bfd-required-min-rx250 msFastest interval this end will accept receiving
bfd-detect-mult3Missed intervals before declaring the peer down
Detection time750 mstx interval × multiplier

Multihop — when the peers are not on the cable

BFD was originally designed for two routers on the same subnet. The Singapore peering is loopback-to-loopback across two underlay circuits, which breaks the design's founding assumption: on a directly-connected link, a missing probe means something is genuinely broken. Across hops you do not own, a missing probe might mean somebody else's core had a busy eight hundred milliseconds.

Consequence: multihop BFD needs looser timers than single-hop BFD. This is the same speed-versus-false-positive trade you met with BGP timers, reappearing one layer down.
FortiOS CLI — BFD multihop template
config router bfd
    config multihop-template
        edit 1
            set src 10.99.50.0 255.255.255.0
            set dst 172.20.0.0 255.255.255.0
            set auth-mode md5
            set md5-key <password>
        next
    end
end

config router bgp
    config neighbor
        edit 172.20.0.1
            set bfd enable
            set ebgp-enforce-multihop enable
        next
    end
end
Why MD5 here and not on port1: a probe crossing networks you do not control can be spoofed by someone who does. Single-hop BFD sits on a cable you own; multihop BFD does not.

MENTAL NOTE
Enabled on the neighbour but not the interface — or on your side but not theirs — is a fix that fails quietly while wearing the clothes of a fix.

💭THINK FIRST · NOT EVERYWHERE

BFD is cheap, fast and effective, which makes 'enable it on every peering' feel like obvious good practice. It is not. Three situations should give you pause, and only one of them is about BFD itself.

  • On a peer with no alternative path, what does BFD actually buy you?
  • Fifty branch spokes, all with BFD at default 250 ms. What have you built?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Faster knowledge, not faster recovery. BFD's entire value lies in what you reconverge onto. With a single road, sub-second detection means you learn you are down 179 seconds earlier — a real monitoring benefit, but not an availability control. Anyone justifying BFD on a single-homed peer as an availability measure has confused noticing with fixing.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Four packets per second, each way, per session — two hundred transmits and two hundred receives every second, forever, on the control plane. BFD does not remove the CPU constraint that ruled out aggressive BGP timers; it relocates it. The lever is the same: tune the interval to the value of the route. Sub-second on two carrier peerings carrying the trading floor is money well spent. Sub-second on fifty spokes where two seconds is invisible to the user is CPU spent on nothing.

Why BFD Everywhere Is a Design Error

Peer with no BFD commitmentContract firstYour thresholds are only as good as the worst normal behaviour of a path you do not own

The design rule: match detection speed to the failure you actually expect on that link, not to the fastest number the platform allows.

💭THINK FIRST · GRACEFUL RESTART

Riley's question ends the technical session: BFD handles Baz's router dying. What happens when LoFinance's own HA pair fails over at nine in the morning?

  • Where does the BGP daemon actually run in an HA cluster, and what does the peer at the far end experience during a failover?
  • BFD and graceful restart are configured on the same neighbour. A failover occurs. Which one wins?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
The BGP daemon runs only on the primary. FGCP synchronises the session table, but that table is kernel state for transit traffic. A BGP peering terminates on the box, in a userland process's memory — its state machine, its RIB, its negotiated capabilities. Processes do not fail over. The secondary starts a fresh daemon that has never spoken to anyone, so the peering must rebuild from scratch and the peer withdraws every route. The new primary can forward perfectly; it is routing that announces a death which was really a change of shift.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
BFD wins. Graceful restart is a promise about a session going down gracefully — it needs the peering to survive long enough for the stale-route logic to apply. BFD does not negotiate; it hands BGP a hard verdict that the forwarding path is dead. The session tears down, routes are withdrawn, and graceful restart never gets to speak. You configured a feature and then configured a second feature that silently overrules it.
SECTION 05

Graceful Restart — and the Two Seatbelts Problem

Two tools, same neighbour, opposite instructions. One of them wins.

Do NOT include human characters, vendor logos, or dense CLI text. Purpose: make the tension between the two tools visually unforgettable so the student never enables both aggressively without thinking.

Graceful restart exists to solve a very specific and slightly absurd problem: a healthy cluster, mid-failover, being reported to the world as dead. It works by negotiating a capability in advance — the cluster tells its peer "if my session drops, I am restarting, not dying. Mark my routes stale, keep forwarding on them, and I will be back shortly with a usable table."

FortiOS CLI — both commands are required
config router bgp
    set as 65001
    set router-id 10.99.50.1
    set graceful-restart enable
    config neighbor
        edit 100.64.1.254
            set capability-graceful-restart enable
            set remote-as 65100
        next
    end
end
Common misconception: that set graceful-restart enable alone is sufficient. The global command enables the feature; the per-neighbour capability-graceful-restart advertises it to that peer. A capability nobody negotiated is a capability nobody honours — and the exam likes to show you a snippet with exactly one of the two present.
Analogy

A change of shift, announced as a death.

  • The night guard hands over to the day guard. The building is protected continuously; the forwarding never stops.
  • But the radio goes silent for eleven seconds during the handover, and headquarters declares the post abandoned and reroutes every patrol.
  • Graceful restart is the memo sent in advance: "if you lose my radio, wait — I am changing shift, not evacuating."
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
BFD shouts 'reroute now.' Graceful restart whispers 'do not reroute.' On the same neighbour, they are in genuine tension — and BFD wins.

💭THINK FIRST · REAL TOPOLOGY

A production environment: a 2601F HA cluster in the DC with NP7s and hardware-offloaded IPsec, peering over tunnels to an Active-Passive VM cluster in Sydney and a standalone FortiGate in Melbourne. Two failure scenarios, one neighbour statement.

  • Sydney has an HA failover. Sydney's cluster dies entirely. At second zero, how does the DC distinguish these two events?
  • Which end's hardware sets your BFD timers — the 2601F with NP7s, or the Sydney VM?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
It cannot. That is the whole design problem. At second zero both events look identical from the DC: packets stopped arriving. You cannot configure two different behaviours for two events you cannot tell apart, so the real question is not 'which tool per scenario' but what makes them distinguishable, and how long am I willing to wait to find out? The answer is per-neighbour tuning: on a peer that sits behind an HA cluster, you deliberately relax detection so that a routine failover falls inside the tolerance window rather than outside it.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
The Sydney VM. The 2601F offloads IPsec into NP7 silicon and has control-plane headroom to spare. The VM does encryption, tunnel handling, the BGP daemon and the BFD state machine all in software on shared vCPU. The slower peer always sets the floor — your detection interval is bounded by whichever end struggles to hold the cadence under load, and it is never the one with the better ASICs.
SECTION 06

Applied: DC · Sydney · Melbourne

Three neighbours, three different expected failure modes, three different answers. This is what per-neighbour design judgement actually looks like in production, and it is the reason "enable BFD everywhere" is not a strategy.

PeeringExpected failurePrimary toolBFD posture
DC ↔ CarrierSilent far-end device death, no HA behind itBFDAggressive — buy the 750 ms
DC ↔ Sydney (A-P VM cluster)HA failover far more likely than site lossGraceful restartRelaxed, above realistic failover time — or omitted
DC ↔ Melbourne (standalone)Device or tunnel loss; no failover existsBFDStraightforward — if it is gone, it is gone
The FGCP detail that makes Sydney interesting: FGCP synchronises IPsec security associations, so the tunnel survives a Sydney failover. The BGP daemon does not. You get the slightly eerie situation of an intact tunnel carrying a dead peering — which is precisely why graceful restart, not BFD, is the tool for that neighbour.
The trap on this topology: configuring aggressive BFD on DC ↔ Sydney because it worked so well on the carrier links. Sydney fails over, DC declares death at 750 ms, routes are withdrawn, graceful restart never applies, and traffic reroutes away from a site that was never actually down.

The slower peer sets the floor. A 2601F's NP7s cannot make a VM answer faster.

💭THINK FIRST · TROUBLESHOOTING

Someone reports that failover still took three minutes despite BFD being configured. Before touching anything, decide what you are actually going to check first.

  • What is the very first command, and why that one?
  • BFD is enabled on both the neighbour and the interface, yet the session never reaches Up while BGP stays happily Established. Where is the fault?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
get router info bfd neighbor. The question is not whether BFD is configured — it is whether BFD is Up. Those are different facts, and the gap between them is where almost every BFD incident lives. Verify the detector is alive before you start blaming the thing it was supposed to be watching.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Almost certainly at the far end. BFD is a negotiated bidirectional session; if the peer is not running it, your side has nobody to complete the handshake with. Note that BGP is entirely unbothered by this — which is exactly what makes the failure quiet. At that point it stops being a CLI problem and becomes a conversation with the carrier.

💭THINK FIRST · EXAM & TAKEAWAYS

Ten questions covering the session. Answer before revealing — the reasoning matters more than the letter.

  • Which two places must BFD be enabled on a FortiGate, and what breaks if you do only one?
  • What are the two commands required for graceful restart, and why are both necessary?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Per neighbour under config router bgp, and per interface under config system interface. With only the neighbour half, the interface has no BFD parameters to run with; with only the interface half, BGP is never told to act on BFD's verdict. Either way you get a configuration that looks complete and delivers nothing.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
set graceful-restart enable at the BGP global level enables the capability on this router; set capability-graceful-restart enable under the specific neighbour advertises it to that peer during the OPEN exchange. Graceful restart is a negotiated capability — one side quietly wanting it achieves nothing.

Reading Sealed Mail

Deploying full inspection — certificates, exemptions, TLS version control. The morning everything broke on schedule.

Session Overview

Session 20 chose the weapon; Session 21 fired it — in the wrong order, deliberately. Full inspection went live at 9:00 AM with the FortiGate CA generated but never distributed to clients. Within four minutes, every HTTPS site on the network threw certificate warnings, Marcus's $400k banking settlement was at risk, and Riley's queue hit 51 tickets. The recovery taught the four pillars of deploying full inspection in the real world: CA distribution order, permanent exemptions, inbound vs outbound certificate strategy, protocol port mapping, and TLS version control.

Story Recap

9:04 AM: banking portal red-screens. Marcus runs (unprecedented). Riley's queue melts. Cipher tells users "this is expected behaviour" — accurate, unhelpful. Junior reaches for a full rollback. The student diagnoses the fault from symptom shape alone (all sites + all users = CA missing from client trust stores), verifies it against the certificate viewer's issuer field, falls the network back to certificate inspection, exempts the banking portal, and Marcus clears his settlement at 9:52. Cipher then demonstrates a PHP injection sailing through the inbound portal's IPS unseen — leading to Protecting SSL Server. Cipher also finds SMTPS tunnelled on port 10587 — leading to protocol port mapping. Junior's warehouse scanners (TLS 1.0-only) surface version control. By 4:30 PM the queue holds two tickets, both about the coffee machine.

Learning Objectives

  • Diagnose CA-distribution failures from symptom blast radius.
  • Configure SSL exemptions and explain why some are permanent.
  • Explain certificate pinning and why it defeats full inspection regardless of trust stores.
  • Contrast outbound (re-signing CA) and inbound (Protecting SSL Server) full inspection.
  • Configure protocol port mapping and state its three preconditions.
  • Configure TLS minimum-version control and handle legacy-device exceptions.

1 · The Deployment Disaster: Blast Radius Is Diagnosis

The single most reusable skill from this session: the shape of a certificate symptom identifies the broken layer before you open a single menu.

Symptom shapeBroken layer
Every site, every user, every browser warnsClient trust store — the inspection CA is not trusted
One website warns for everyoneThat site's certificate (expired, wrong name, bad chain)
One device class fails; others fineDevice capability (e.g., TLS version support, missing CA on that platform)

Evidence step: open the browser's certificate viewer. If the issuer field reads your FortiGate CA and the issue timestamp is minutes old, the forging engine is working perfectly — the clients simply don't trust the forger.

Senior lessonThe order of operations IS the deployment plan. Distribute the CA (GPO/MDM), then enable full inspection. Never the reverse.
Fall back ≠ roll backWhen full inspection breaks, retreat one layer to certificate inspection (which needs no client-side trust), not to zero inspection. You keep web filtering and app control alive while the CA push completes.

2 · Exemptions: The Envelopes We Never Open

Exempted traffic receives no inspection of any depth — FortiGate steps out of the TLS conversation entirely; the client negotiates directly with the real server and sees the real certificate. Built-in profiles are read-only: clone before editing.

config firewall ssl-ssh-profile
    edit "LoFinance-deep-inspection"
        config ssl-exempt
            edit 1
                set type address
                set address "portal.meridianbank.com"
            next
        end
    next
end

Fortinet ships the deep-inspection profile with whole web categories pre-exempted: Finance and Banking, Health and Wellness, Personal Privacy. These exemptions are permanent design, not temporary workarounds, for three reasons:

  1. Compliance / data custody — decrypting financial or health traffic mid-path means holding plaintext you are legally and contractually forbidden to hold.
  2. Refusing the capability — if plaintext banking sessions ever exist on a device you administer, you become a suspect in any breach of them. The exemption protects administrators as much as users.
  3. Certificate pinning — see below. Some clients will reject the forged certificate no matter what you push to the trust store.

3 · Certificate Pinning: Checking the Wax Seal

A browser asks: "do I trust whoever signed this certificate?" — a trust-store question, which our distributed CA answers. A pinned application asks a narrower question: "is this the exact certificate (or public key) I was compiled to expect?" The pin lives inside the app, not on the server. The app only ever talks to one server, and its developers knew that server's identity in advance.

The fingerprintCA installed and OS-trusted → browser works, banking app still fails. Trusted is not enough. Pinned apps break under full inspection regardless of trust-store contents. Junior's version: "Browser asks who signed it. App asks: is it literally you."

4 · Inbound vs Outbound: Whose Private Key?

Full inspection always requires possessing a private key. The only question is whose:

Own the clients?Own the server?MethodClient-side pain
OutboundYesNoForge certificates with a re-signing CACA push, trust stores, pinning breakage
InboundNoYesLoad the server's real certificate + private keyNone — clients see the genuine certificate

This table is the profile's scope dropdown:

  • Multiple Clients Connecting to Multiple Servers — outbound. Many internal users, many unknown internet servers, forgery required.
  • Protecting SSL Server — inbound. Many unknown internet clients, one server you own, real key loaded. FortiGate terminates TLS as the server, inspects, re-encrypts onward. Zero client impact; even pinned clients work, because the pinned certificate is the real one.
config firewall ssl-ssh-profile
    edit "LoFi-Portal-Protect"
        set server-cert-mode replace
        set server-cert "LoFinance-Portal-Cert"
    next
end
Why it matteredWith certificate inspection + IPS on the inbound policy, Cipher's PHP code injection over HTTPS produced normal traffic logs and zero IPS events — IPS needs payload, and the payload was sealed. With Protecting SSL Server applied: signature detected: PHP.Code.Injection — dropped.

5 · Protocol Port Mapping: Pigeonholes and Costumes

The proxy-based engine assigns each session to a protocol inspector by destination port, before any decryption. Port 587 → SMTPS inspector; port 443 → HTTPS inspector. A session on port 10587 is never claimed by any inspector — it isn't examined and found innocent; it is never examined at all, even with full inspection enabled. Port mapping registers the new pigeonhole:

config firewall ssl-ssh-profile
    edit "LoFinance-deep-inspection"
        config smtps
            set ports 587 10587
        end
    next
end
The three preconditions (guaranteed multi-select) Protocol port mapping works on an outbound policy only when ALL of: ① full SSL inspection is employed, ② the profile scope is Multiple Clients Connecting to Multiple Servers, and ③ inspection mode is proxy-based. Flow-based inspects all ports regardless — mapping is irrelevant there, and the 10587 evasion fails against a flow-based policy.

Weld the labels: the costume room is proxy (must commit to a protocol impersonation before inspecting); the window is flow (watches every stream on every port, no dispatch needed). Enabling many mappings raises resource usage — enable selectively and monitor logs for unusual ports.

6 · TLS Version Control

config firewall ssl-ssh-profile
    edit "LoFinance-deep-inspection"
        config https
            set unsupported-ssl-version block
            set min-allowed-ssl-version tls-1.2
        end
    next
end
  • CLI-only — these settings do not appear in the GUI profile page. Exam bait.
  • Applies per-protocol: the same knobs exist for SMTPS, POP3S, IMAPS, FTPS.
  • Blocks at the handshake — before any certificate is exchanged. This is why falling back to certificate inspection did not fix the TLS 1.0 scanners.
  • Negotiation logic: a server offering TLS 1.1 and 1.2 is allowed — the session negotiates 1.2, which meets the minimum. Only servers offering nothing at or above the minimum are blocked.
  • Deprecation timeline: SSL 2.0/3.0, TLS 1.0, TLS 1.1 all deprecated (TLS 1.0/1.1 in 2020). TLS 1.2 = broad compatibility; TLS 1.3 = best security and speed.

The legacy-device judgement call

When TLS 1.0-only devices break: never lower the global minimum ("just for" = the two most expensive words in network security). Instead:

  1. Dedicated policy pinned at both ends: source = the legacy device subnet only, destination = the one required site only, with its own profile permitting the old version — logged loudly.
  2. Put the devices on the replacement budget with a date, and write that date into the policy comment. An exception without an expiry is not an exception — it is the new normal.

Verification & Evidence Commands

  • Browser certificate viewer → issuer field (is it your FortiGate CA? when was it issued?).
  • show full-configuration firewall ssl-ssh-profile — confirm exemptions, ports, version settings (CLI-only items won't show in GUI).
  • Forward traffic logs filtered by unusual destination ports (post port-mapping monitoring).
  • IPS security events on the inbound policy after enabling Protecting SSL Server.

Common Mistakes

  • Enabling full inspection before CA distribution (the entire opening disaster).
  • Rolling back everything instead of falling back one layer.
  • Treating banking/health exemptions as temporary embarrassments rather than permanent design.
  • Believing "full inspection enabled" means all ports are inspected — proxy dispatch is port-based.
  • Placing the pin on the server — pinning lives in the client app.
  • Lowering the global TLS minimum to accommodate a handful of legacy devices.
  • Looking for version-control settings in the GUI (CLI-only).

Exam Tips

  • "All sites warn after enabling full inspection" → CA not in client trust stores.
  • Count words decode SNI/SAN: SAN = Single cert, All the Names; many individual certs needing selection = SNI.
  • HTTPS-attack protection under certificate inspection = Limited — not "No", and not conditional on IPS (IPS needs payload).
  • Port-mapping preconditions: full + Multiple-Clients-to-Multiple-Servers + proxy. Flow checks all ports regardless.
  • Min-version stems: check what the session can negotiate, not just the lowest version the server offers.
  • Browser works + app fails after CA push = certificate pinning.

Key Takeaways

  • Full inspection always needs a private key — outbound forges with yours, inbound uses the server's real one.
  • The blast radius of a certificate symptom is itself the diagnosis.
  • Some envelopes must never be opened — and some recipients check the wax seal.
  • Exceptions must be narrow, visible, and temporary, or they become architecture.
  • The costume room is proxy. The window is flow.

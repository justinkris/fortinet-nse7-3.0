Title: 1 · The Ten-Judge Staircase

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, modern editorial style, cream off-white background (#faf5e9), bold clean brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, no 3D. A grand but slightly absurd courtroom rendered as a TEN-TIERED VERTICAL STAIRCASE of judges' benches, viewed straight-on in cross-section so all ten tiers are visible stacked from top to bottom of the frame, each tier smaller and lower than the one above.

Each of the ten benches has a single stylised judge in a muted sage-green robe, and a small coral placard reading, from top to bottom: "1 WEIGHT", "2 LOCAL PREF", "3 LOCAL ORIGIN", "4 AS PATH", "5 ORIGIN", "6 MED", "7 IGP METRIC", "8 EBGP > IBGP", "9 ECMP", "10 ROUTER ID". Only these ten short labels appear as text; no other text in the image.

Two identical-looking envelope-shaped "path" characters stand at the top of the staircase before the first judge: one envelope is coral-coloured and carries a tiny Singapore skyline motif, the other is muted gold and carries a tiny European rooftop motif. Both envelopes have a small "2" stamped on them to show equal AS-path length.

Judges 1, 2 and 3 are asleep or looking bored with their gavels resting — these attributes were never set. Judge 4 (AS PATH) is wide awake, leaning forward, and is holding up BOTH envelopes at exactly equal height in a perfect tie, one in each hand. Judges 5 through 9 are also asleep. Judge 10, at the very bottom, is flipping a large coin, which is the visual punchline.

A thin coral dotted line traces the envelopes' fall from tier 1 all the way down to tier 10, past every sleeping judge, emphasising that the argument descended the entire staircase.

In the lower-left foreground, small and secondary: Señor, a warm-skinned older engineer in a large decorative sombrero and rumpled shirt, watching the staircase with folded arms and a distinctly unamused expression. Beside him a younger engineer in a plain shirt (the Intermediate Engineer) is looking UP toward judges 1 and 2 rather than down at the coin — the compositional hint that the fix lives at the top.

Muted palette: cream, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown line work. Generous white space. Clear enough to read as an educational diagram. Do NOT include: dense technical labels, network device icons, cabling, packet diagrams, gradients, glossy rendering, or any text beyond the ten placards.

Title: 2 · Four Drawers and a Doorman

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, modern editorial style, cream off-white background (#faf5e9), bold clean brown outlines, no gradients, no 3D, no photorealism. A cross-section of a wide wooden FILING CABINET with THREE open drawers arranged left to right, plus a FOURTH separate wall-mounted cabinet below and to the right.

Drawer 1, labelled "RIB-IN" on a small coral plate: crowded with many upright paper folders, including two folders pushed forward and highlighted — one coral, one muted gold — both stamped with an identical small "2". This is the waiting room of candidates.

Between drawer 1 and drawer 2 stands a DOORWAY with a uniformed doorman figure in a sage-green coat holding a clipboard. The clipboard is a tiny version of the ten-item numbered list, unreadable in detail but recognisably a numbered list. Just BEFORE the doorman, on the RIB-IN side, sits a small desk with a rubber stamp and ink pad, and the muted gold folder is being stamped with a large coral "300" — this is the inbound route-map setting local preference. The visual sequence must clearly read left to right: STAMP FIRST, THEN DOORMAN.

Drawer 2, labelled "LOCAL RIB": contains only the muted gold folder, standing alone and upright, with a small coral ribbon. The coral folder is visibly still back in drawer 1, upright, undamaged, waiting — not in a bin.

Drawer 3, labelled "RIB-OUT": contains a smaller selection of folders, with a second smaller doorman or gate between drawer 2 and drawer 3.

Below, connected to drawer 2 by a short arrow, the separate wall cabinet labelled "ROUTING TABLE", containing folders of several different colours to show competing protocols.

Small hand-lettered sign above the cabinet reads: "BELIEVING AND TELLING ARE DIFFERENT DRAWERS". No other text except the four drawer labels and the "300" stamp.

Palette: cream, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown outlines. Do NOT include: server racks, network topology, cables, computer screens, gradients, or characters other than the two doormen.

Title: 3 · The Bucket and the Tap

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, modern editorial style, cream off-white background (#faf5e9), bold brown outlines, no gradients, no 3D. A single wide scene split into two halves by a soft vertical sage-green divider, comparing two ways of getting water into a glass — used here as a metaphor for originating prefixes into BGP.

LEFT HALF, labelled with a small coral placard "REDISTRIBUTE": a person in a plain shirt (Junior, young, slightly overconfident expression) is tipping an enormous galvanised BUCKET over a small drinking glass. Water is sloshing everywhere, overflowing the glass and pooling on the floor. Floating in the spilled water are small labelled paper tags reading only: "MGMT", "DMZ", "GUEST", "VPN", "OOB" — five short tags, no other text. A mop leans against the wall in the background, unused. Expression: dawning realisation.

RIGHT HALF, labelled with a small coral placard "CONFIG NETWORK": the same person, calmer, turning a small brass TAP that fills the glass precisely to the line. A single paper tag hangs on the tap reading "10.55.10.0/24". Nothing is spilled.

BELOW THE TAP, integrated into the right half as the visual punchline: the tap's pipe passes through a small brass INSPECTION VALVE with a magnifying glass built into it, labelled "IMPORT CHECK". Inside the magnifier, two paper cards are shown side by side being compared: one card reads "/16" and the other reads "/24", and a small coral X mark sits between them. A thin dotted line shows that the water has therefore STOPPED before reaching the glass — the tap is on but nothing is coming out.

At the very bottom of the frame, a small hand-lettered sign: "COVERED IS NOT MATCHED".

Palette: cream, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown line work. Generous spacing, storybook-professional. Do NOT include: network diagrams, routers, gradients, glossy rendering, or any text beyond the labels specified.

Title: 4 · The Border Control

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, modern editorial style, cream off-white background (#faf5e9), bold brown outlines, no gradients, no 3D, no photorealism. A stylised CUSTOMS AND BORDER CONTROL scene arranged as a horizontal journey from left to right across three zones separated by two vertical dashed sage-green border lines.

ZONE 1 (left), a warm interior labelled "OUR AS": three small envelope-characters wait in line, each carrying a differently shaped luggage tag. Tags are labelled only: "AS_PATH", "LOCAL_PREF", "MED", "COMMUNITY" — four tags distributed among the envelopes.

BORDER 1 (first dashed line), staffed by a sage-robed officer at a desk: the LOCAL_PREF tag is being cut off with small scissors and dropped into a coral basket beneath the desk labelled with a tiny "HOME" pictogram (a small house symbol, not text). The envelope continues onward without it.

ZONE 2 (middle), labelled "NEIGHBOUR AS": the envelopes continue. Here a second officer at a small side desk removes the MED tag and places it in a drawer labelled with a tiny "ADVICE" pictogram (a small speech-bubble symbol). MED does not continue past this zone.

COMMUNITY passes through untouched and the officer waves it along.

ZONE 3 (right), labelled "THE WIDER INTERNET", drawn as a lighter, more open space with a faint globe outline: only AS_PATH and COMMUNITY arrive.

Across the top of the whole image, a hand-lettered banner reads: "HOW FAR DOES IT TRAVEL?"

Palette: cream, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown outlines. Clean, calm, diagrammatic. Do NOT include: real national flags, passports with identifiable countries, dense text, gradients, network hardware, or any text beyond the specified labels.

Title: 5 · The Gate with One Wicket

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, modern editorial style, cream off-white background (#faf5e9), bold brown outlines, no gradients, no 3D. A dramatic but tidy scene showing a row of TWELVE small houses along a street, each house with a chimney emitting a thin ribbon of smoke, representing twelve advertised prefixes.

In the foreground, a large ornate GARDEN GATE stands across the street. The gate has exactly ONE small open wicket door cut into it, and above the wicket a coral sign reads "10.55.10.0/24 PERMIT". The rest of the gate is solid.

Only ONE house's smoke ribbon passes through the wicket and continues to the right side of the frame. The other ELEVEN smoke ribbons hit the solid gate and curl back on themselves, greyed and dimmed but NOT erased — the houses are still standing, still lit from within, still perfectly healthy. This distinction is essential: the prefixes are not gone, they are merely unspoken.

Beneath the gate, running along the bottom of the entire frame, a faint but legible engraved inscription in the stonework reads: "AND NOTHING ELSE".

To the left of the gate, a small figure of an engineer stands holding a single sheet of paper, looking pleased with themselves, entirely unaware of the eleven curled-back ribbons behind them. Just visible in the shadow of the gate's frame, three small identical keystones are carved with tiny symbols and labelled only: "ROUTE-MAP", "PREFIX-LIST", "ACCESS-LIST" — indicating the same family trait built into all three.

On the right side beyond the gate, one lone recipient figure waves, having received exactly one ribbon.

Palette: cream, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown outlines, with the blocked ribbons in a desaturated warm grey. Do NOT include: fire, damage, alarms, red warning symbols, network equipment, gradients, or any text beyond the three keystone labels, the wicket sign, and the engraved inscription.

Title: 6 · The Rectangle in the Dust

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, modern editorial style, cream off-white background (#faf5e9), bold brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, no 3D. A quiet late-afternoon office scene rendered as a wide horizontal composition.

LEFT THIRD: a help desk corner. Riley, a young engineer with a headset worn loosely around the neck like a scarf, is closing a ticket on a monitor. The monitor shows only a large green tick and the number "94" with a small "ms" beside it — no other screen text. Beside the monitor, a yellow sticky note is stuck to the bezel, and on it in small hand lettering: "ABSENCE OF ERRORS IS NOT EVIDENCE OF CORRECTNESS".

CENTRE: a whiteboard bearing a single dot in the middle with eight straight lines radiating to the edges — the route-reflector star from the previous session, carefully maintained and clean. Standing before it, Junior, a young overconfident-looking engineer, is for once standing calmly with arms at his sides, not gesturing, wearing a small satisfied expression. Beside the whiteboard, Penny, a composed professional woman with a notepad, has written on her pad in small clear hand lettering: "WE ADVERTISE ONLY WHAT WE CAN ACTUALLY REACH".

RIGHT THIRD, and the emotional focus: an old grey filing cabinet that nobody uses, standing slightly apart. On top of it, placed down deliberately and squarely, sits a large decorative SOMBRERO — unworn, at rest. In the dust on the cabinet top beside the sombrero, a clean empty RECTANGLE is visible where something else has just been lifted away. Nothing occupies that rectangle. No hat is shown being carried; the space is the point.

The room's lighting is low and warm, long shadows, end-of-day. Nobody is looking at the filing cabinet.

Palette: cream, coral, muted gold, sage green, soft brown outlines, warm grey shadows. Mood: satisfied, quiet, faintly ominous. Do NOT include: a cowboy hat visible anywhere in frame, Señor himself, dramatic lighting effects, gradients, or any text beyond the two hand-lettered notes and the "94 ms" figure.

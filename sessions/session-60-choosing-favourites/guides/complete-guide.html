Choosing Favourites

BGP attributes, the best-path argument, the network command, and the filters that deny by default.

BGP is never wrong. BGP is only ever badly advised.

A path you did not choose is almost never a fault. It is the sound of nobody in your company having expressed an opinion.

Section 1 · Story recap

The Ticket That Went Around the Planet

Thursday, 06:12. Riley is alone in a dark office with a ticket queue containing exactly one entry, opened at 05:47 by someone with a London email signature — which is strange, because London does not officially exist until next quarter.

TICKET #4471 — Latency to LON-DC: 340ms. Expected: ~90ms. Please advise.

He runs the traceroute himself because he does not believe it. Sydney. Sydney edge. Then 172.20.0.1 — Singapore, Javier's border router. Then a long transoceanic silence. Then London. “It's going around the planet. The wrong way.”

By 08:00 Penny has printed the ticket, which is never a good sign, and J.R. has written 340 and 90 in his notebook and drawn a line between them, twice. Señor is at the whiteboard and is not smiling for the second consecutive day, and people have noticed.

Javier Lin, on the line since 07:00 SGT, delivers one sentence and stops: “My path is shorter. Fewer autonomous systems. Ask your algorithm.”

He is not being difficult. He is being correct — which is worse. There is no fault here. Nothing is misconfigured. Both paths to London are exactly two AS hops long. The route was chosen fairly, by the rules, and the rules produced a disastrous answer.

The shape of the problem
Two candidates, identical on paper, and a hiring committee that has run out of questions.
  • Both paths reach London.
  • Both cross two autonomous systems.
  • Neither carries any expression of preference from anyone at LoFinance.
  • So the decision falls to the last line on the form — and the last line is not about quality at all.
🧠
Mental note
A “wrong” BGP path is usually not a fault. It is an absence of policy. Somebody else's addressing made your routing decision because nobody at your company made it first.

Learning objectives

  • Read the BGP best-path tie-breaker list as an ordered argument, not a settings page.
  • Locate best-path selection precisely in the three-RIB model.
  • Choose between weight and LOCAL_PREF by scope, before considering anything else.
  • Classify any BGP attribute by asking how far it travels.
  • Originate a prefix deliberately with config network, and survive network-import-check.
  • Apply prefix lists per neighbour without silently killing everything else.

Section 2 · Route selection · p.344

The Tie-Breaker List Is an Argument

💭Think first · Before you read the list
Two paths to the same destination. Identical AS path length. No local preference anywhere. No MED. No weight. Nobody has expressed an opinion.
  • If every attribute BGP cares about is equal, what is left to decide with?
  • Is that a fault, or is it something else?
  • Whose decision is it, in practice?
Q1
Eventually, nothing meaningful. The comparison falls to the last tie-breaker: lowest router ID — an identifier chosen by whoever built the neighbouring router.
Q2
Not a fault. Every device did exactly what it was told. It is an absence of policy.
Q3
Somebody else's. Your traffic is being steered by a number in a foreign configuration file.

Señor unfolds a coffee-ringed page and pins it to the board. Ten numbered lines, the first circled twice in red.

#Tie-breakerWhat it really means
1Highest weightThis box's private opinion. Never advertised.
2Highest local preferenceCompany policy, AS-wide.
3Prefer the path that originated locallyOur own prefix beats a learned one.
4Shortest AS pathJavier's ground. Hop count, not distance.
5Lowest origin typeIGP < EGP < incomplete.
6Lowest MEDA neighbour's advice about which of its doors to use.
7Lowest IGP metric to the BGP next hopInternally, which exit is closest?
8Prefer external (EBGP) over internal (IBGP)Get it out of the AS sooner.
9If ECMP enabled, install up to 10 routesSession 56. Equal is permission; enable is instruction.
10Lowest router IDThe coin flip. Decided by someone else.

This is not a list of settings. It is an argument conducted in order. BGP compares two paths one line at a time, top to bottom, and the first line where they differ ends the argument. Nothing below that line is ever read.

Javier's move. “My path is shorter, ask your algorithm” is an invitation to have the argument on line four. On line four we tie — and a tie on line four means the fight continues down to line ten, where we lose to a number we do not control. He chose the battleground. That is the whole trick.

🧠
Mental note
Argue above the line you are losing on. Choosing the battleground is the engineering; the configuration afterwards is trivial.

Section 3 · Locating the algorithm

Where the Argument Actually Happens

💭Think first · Name the drawer
Two paths for London arrive. Neither is filtered. Both are real. The tie-breaker list runs; one wins; the loser does not disappear — it sits in the database forever, valid and unused.
  • Before the algorithm runs, which drawer are both candidates sitting in?
  • After it runs, which drawer does only the winner enter?
  • So best-path selection is a ______ question.
Q1
RIB-in. Everything received and accepted, pre-decision.
Q2
Local RIB. What this router has chosen to believe.
Q3
A RIB-in → Local RIB question. It happens at the doorway, not inside either room.
The three drawers — and the fourth box
   RIB-in  ──[ in filter + best path ]──▶  Local RIB  ──[ out filter ]──▶  RIB-out
   "told me"                                "believe"                       "will say"
                                                │
                                                ▼
                                        ROUTING TABLE
                                  (BGP still has to beat other
                                   protocols on distance to get in)

RIB-in is the queue of candidates. Local RIB is who got in. The tie-breaker list is the doorman. The loser is not deleted — it waits in RIB-in for the winner to die. That is why Session 56's idle carrier path was still visible in get router info bgp network long after it stopped being used.

Two things happen at that same doorway, and their order is the reason any of this works:

Order of operations at gate 1
   RIB-in ──[ inbound route-map: SET attributes ]──[ best-path list: COMPARE ]──▶ Local RIB

Policy runs first. If the comparison ran first and the route-map afterwards, you would be editing the loser's paperwork after the verdict had been delivered — the attribute would change and nothing would happen.

🧠
Mental note
Policy runs first. The doorman reads the result. Attribute manipulation is only meaningful because it is upstream of the comparison.
Watch this boundary. The Local RIB is BGP's own head. The routing table is FortiGate's. They are two separate installations and two separate competitions — a route can win best-path inside BGP and still lose to a static route on distance. Most of the half-inch errors in this topic live here.

Section 4 · The weapon

A Private Opinion vs a Company Policy

💭Think first · Choose by scope
We must win above line four. Line three is unavailable — neither path originated locally. That leaves weight (line 1) and local preference (line 2). One of these is never advertised anywhere at all. The other is carried to every router in your AS and stripped at the AS boundary.
  • Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth and Adelaide all send traffic to London too. Which attribute do they inherit?
  • What would you have to do to achieve the same result with weight?
  • Which line of the list are you now arguing on, and why does Javier stop mattering?
Q1
LOCAL_PREF. It travels inside IBGP updates. Set it once at the border and the entire AS inherits Sydney's opinion.
Q2
Log into every firewall in the estate and set it individually — and then do it again for every site you ever add. Junior can tell you how that ends: you run out of board.
Q3
Line two. Once local preference differs, the argument ends there. Line four — Javier's ground — is never read.
AttributeScopeTo IBGP peers?To EBGP peers?Use it when…
weightThis FortiGate onlyNeverNeverOne box must behave differently without changing corporate policy
LOCAL_PREFThe entire local ASYesStripped at the edgeEveryone inside our AS should agree on the exit
Señor's framing
Weight is a private opinion. Local preference is a company policy.
  • A private opinion changes what you do and nobody else hears it.
  • A policy is circulated internally, everyone acts on it, and it is not shared with customers or suppliers.
  • Both are useful. The design question is only ever: who needs to agree?
SYD-HQ — the fix
config router route-map
    edit "PREFER-EU-TO-LONDON"
        config rule
            edit 1
                set match-ip-address "LONDON-PREFIXES"
                set set-local-preference 300
            next
            edit 2
                set action permit          <-- MANDATORY. Route-maps deny by default.
            next
        end
    next
end

config router bgp
    config neighbor
        edit "172.30.0.1"
            set remote-as 65500
            set route-map-in "PREFER-EU-TO-LONDON"
            set description "EU carrier - LON transit"
        next
    end
end
Verification — get router info bgp network 10.44.0.0/16
BGP routing table entry for 10.44.0.0/16
Paths: (2 available, best #2)
  65300 65400
    172.20.0.1 from 172.20.0.1 (172.20.0.1)
      Origin IGP, localpref 100, valid, external
  65500 65400
    172.30.0.1 from 172.30.0.1 (172.30.0.1)
      Origin IGP, localpref 300, valid, external, best

Riley, refreshing: “94 milliseconds.” Javier, after a pause: “You changed the argument.” Señor: “I did not change the argument. I raised the subject earlier.”

Section 5 · Attribute categories · p.343

Ask How Far It Travels

Four category names, and they look like arbitrary vocabulary until you notice they are the answers to two sequential questions.

The decision tree — do not memorise the table, derive it
  Q1. Must EVERY BGP router understand this attribute?
      │
      ├── YES → WELL-KNOWN
      │         Q2a. Must it be present on every route?
      │              ├── YES → WELL-KNOWN MANDATORY
      │              └── NO  → WELL-KNOWN DISCRETIONARY
      │
      └── NO  → OPTIONAL
                Q2b. May a router that does NOT understand it pass it on?
                     ├── YES → OPTIONAL TRANSITIVE
                     └── NO  → OPTIONAL NON-TRANSITIVE
AttributeCategoryWhy — how far does it travel?
ORIGINWell-known mandatoryEvery route must declare where it came from
AS_PATHWell-known mandatoryLoop prevention. No route may exist without it
NEXT_HOPWell-known mandatoryA route with no next hop is not a route
LOCAL_PREFWell-known discretionaryStripped at the AS edge — so an inbound EBGP route arrives without one. Not always present ⇒ not mandatory
ATOMIC_AGGREGATEWell-known discretionaryOnly present when aggregation happened
AGGREGATOROptional transitiveUseful to know who aggregated; harmless to pass along
COMMUNITYOptional transitiveTags designed to survive the AS boundary. That is its entire purpose
MULTI_EXIT_DISCOptional non-transitiveAdvice for one neighbour about one of your doors. It must not leak to a third AS
Memory line: COMMUNITY travels — that is its job. MED must not — it is private advice. LOCAL_PREF never leaves home.
🧠
Mental note
You already know the travel range of every attribute you have met. The category is downstream of the travel range, not a separate fact to memorise.

Section 6 · Origination · pp.345–347

A Bucket, and a Tap

Three facts about FortiGate's BGP implementation from p.345, and the second is the one people fall over:

  • There are no hard limits — neighbours, routes and policies are bounded only by system memory.
  • By default, BGP originates nothing. You must redistribute, or name prefixes explicitly.
  • By default, all received prefixes are accepted. Filtering is opt-in.
💭Think first · Two ways in
You have only ever seen one method of getting a prefix into BGP: redistribution. Penny now asks you to advertise exactly one subnet to one partner — and nothing else.
  • What does config redistribute connected actually put into BGP on a firewall with management, DMZ, guest wireless, three VPN tunnels and an out-of-band network?
  • So what is redistribution's weakness when the requirement is precise?
  • What would the ideal tool look like?
Q1
All of it. Every connected interface, in one command, with no discrimination.
Q2
It pours a whole category in and leaves you building a route-map afterwards to take back everything you did not mean to say.
Q3
Something that names one prefix, deliberately and auditably. That is config network.
J.R.'s phrasing
Redistribution is a bucket. The network command is a tap.
  • Bucket: fast, wide, and you spend the afternoon mopping.
  • Tap: one named prefix, on purpose, and readable by whoever inherits your config.
  • They are not alternatives to each other in the sense of one being better — they are two independent origination methods for two different intents.
Both methods
config router bgp
    config redistribute "connected"          <-- the bucket
        set status enable
    end
end

config router bgp
    config network                           <-- the tap
        edit 1
            set prefix 10.55.10.0 255.255.255.0
        next
    end
end

The rule attached to the tap

config network advertises the prefix only if an active route with an exact matching destination subnet exists in the routing table. Not the Local RIB — the routing table. This is network-import-check, and it is enabled by default.

“Import” means from outside BGP, into BGP. It is a sanity check on origination: do not tell the world you can reach something you cannot actually reach yourself.

The fault — get router info routing-table all
C       10.55.0.0/16 is directly connected, port12
S       10.99.0.0/16 [10/0] via 10.10.10.1, port3
C       172.30.0.0/30 is directly connected, port5
B       10.44.0.0/16 [20/0] via 172.30.0.1, port5, 00:41:22

  config network says:  10.55.10.0 255.255.255.0    ← /24
  routing table has:    10.55.0.0/16                ← /16

  COVERED is not MATCHED.  Prefix AND mask, exactly.
  Result: nothing advertised. No log. No error. No alarm.

Reachability is fine. Traffic flows, hosts ping, nothing is broken at the data plane. BGP simply declines to say the sentence, silently.

FixScopeRisk
Add a matching static routeOne prefixLow. Fixes the specific case and documents intent for whoever reads the config in a year.
set network-import-check disableGlobal — every network statement on the boxHigh and silent. Twelve prefixes today, twenty next quarter, one of them a typo or a decommissioned subnet, all announced with total confidence.
🧠
Mental note
The check is not in your way. The check is the difference between advertising a subnet and advertising a rumour. Prefer the narrow fix — Session 15's rule: the least powerful tool that meets the requirement.

Section 7 · Prefix lists · p.348

The Family of Implicit Denies

Prefix lists select which prefixes cross a gate, per neighbour, in either direction. prefix-list-in filters what you accept from a peer; prefix-list-out filters what you are willing to say to a peer.

💭Think first · The trap
LoFinance originates twelve prefixes. The intent was: “also announce the London subnet to this partner.” The engineer writes a prefix list containing one permit rule and applies it outbound.
  • What happens to the other eleven prefixes?
  • Which drawer are they in, and which drawer do they never reach?
  • Where else in FortiOS have you already met this exact behaviour?
Q1
They are denied. Anything that does not match an entry in a prefix list is denied by default.
Q2
They are still in the Local RIB — valid, best, installed, believed. They simply never reach RIB-out for this neighbour. Believing and telling are different drawers.
Q3
Route-maps. Access lists. Every filtering construct in FortiOS ends in an implicit denial. Learn the family and no individual member can catch you.
Correct — with the terminator
config router prefix-list
    edit "LON-PARTNER-OUT"
        config rule
            edit 1
                set prefix 10.55.10.0 255.255.255.0
                set action permit
            next
            edit 2
                set prefix 0.0.0.0 0.0.0.0
                set le 32
                set action permit          <-- "and everything else"
            next
        end
    next
end

config router bgp
    config neighbor
        edit "172.30.0.1"
            set prefix-list-out "LON-PARTNER-OUT"
        next
    end
end
ToolPowerCan modify attributes?Reach for it when…
Access listSimple prefix matchNoA blunt, exact permit/deny is genuinely all you need
Prefix listPrefix match + ge/leNoYou need mask-length ranges but no attribute changes
Route mapMatch objects + set clausesYesYou must change something — e.g. set local preference
🧠
Mental note
Ask of any filter with only permit lines: not “what does this allow?” but “what does this silently kill?”

Section 8 · Method

How a Senior Engineer Approaches This

Symptom: traffic takes a strange path
1. Is BGP even choosing?      get router info bgp network <prefix>
                              Two paths, or one?
2. READ THE HEADER LINE       "Paths: (2 available, best #2)"
                              The whole diagnosis is in that one line.
3. Walk the tie-breaker list TOP-DOWN comparing the two paths.
   Find the FIRST line where they differ. That line, and only that
   line, decided it. Everything below it was never read.
4. Differ nowhere?            You fell to line 10 — lowest router ID.
                              NOT A FAULT. An ABSENCE OF POLICY.
5. Fix ABOVE the line you were losing on. Never argue on the
   opponent's line.
6. Verify TWO things:  the attribute changed in the output, AND
                       the word "best" moved.
Symptom: a prefix is not advertised at all
1. Is it originated?     config network present? redistribute enabled?
2. If network command:   get router info routing-table all
                         EXACT prefix AND mask, and ACTIVE.
3. Originated but silent? prefix-list-out / route-map-out on THAT
                          neighbour — and the implicit deny.
4. Remember which side is broken: it can be in the Local RIB and
   perfectly healthy while being invisible to one specific peer.

Common mistakes

  • Treating the tie-breaker list as configurable settings. It is a comparison order, and you influence it by changing inputs, not by reordering it.
  • Setting weight when the whole AS needs to agree. Works on one box, silently does nothing everywhere else.
  • Assuming “covered by a supernet” satisfies network-import-check. It does not. Exact prefix and mask.
  • Writing a filter with one permit line and expecting the rest to pass. The implicit deny is the most examined behaviour on p.348.
  • Disabling a global safety check to fix one prefix. The blast radius outlives the change window.
  • Reading a diagnosis into a symptom you recognise. Name the drawer before you diagnose — state first, then hypothesis.
Exam technique carried out of this session: on any multi-select, score first, count last. Write down the state from the stem, mark every option TRUE/FALSE against that state independently, then count your TRUEs and compare to the number requested. The count is a checksum, not a stopping condition. Too few means you rejected something for being unfamiliar rather than false; too many means one option is true in general but not true of this state.

Section 9 · Exam & takeaways

What Fortinet Will Actually Ask

TrapThe half-inch that decides it
First tie-breakerWeight, not local preference. Local pref is second.
Weight propagationNever advertised anywhere — not even to IBGP peers. Local pref is the one that travels internally.
LOCAL_PREF categoryWell-known discretionary. Not mandatory — it does not survive the AS boundary, so it cannot always be present.
MED categoryOptional non-transitive. Advice for one neighbour; must not be passed onward.
COMMUNITY categoryOptional transitive — travelling is its purpose.
All attributes equal, ECMP offLowest router ID. With ECMP on, up to 10 routes.
network not advertisingExact prefix and mask must be an active route in the routing table. No error is logged.
network-import-checkEnabled by default. Disabling is global, and makes FortiGate advertise all prefixes in the BGP network table regardless of active routes.
Prefix list, one permit ruleEverything else is denied.
Which tool can modify attributes?Only the route map. Access lists and prefix lists filter only.

Key takeaways

  • BGP is never wrong. It is badly advised. Look for missing policy before looking for faults.
  • The tie-breaker list is an argument in order. The first difference ends it; nothing below is read.
  • Argue above the line you are losing on. Choosing the battleground is the skill.
  • Best-path selection is a RIB-in → Local RIB event. Policy runs first; the doorman reads the result.
  • Scope selects the tool. Who needs to agree? One box → weight. One AS → local preference.
  • Ask how far an attribute travels and its category answers itself.
  • Redistribution is a bucket; config network is a tap.
  • Every FortiOS filter ends in an implicit deny. Learn the family, not the members.
  • Prefer the narrow fix. A static route documents intent; a global switch removes a safety net silently, forever.
Business outcome. London latency 340ms → 94ms. No new circuits, no new hardware, no vendor call — one route-map on one firewall. Penny's sentence for the board: “We advertise only what we can actually reach.” Riley closed #4471 at 11:04 with the resolution note: “Nobody at LoFinance had ever expressed a preference.”

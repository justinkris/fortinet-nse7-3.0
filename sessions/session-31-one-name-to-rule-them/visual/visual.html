🎨 VISUAL LEARNING · SESSION 31

One Name to Rule Them

Normalized interfaces as a translation layer · per-platform & per-device mapping

Recommended style: The Name Journey — a single abstract name entering a translation layer and emerging as a different real port on each machine.
Why this style: the whole concept is one input (an abstract name) resolving to many outputs (real interfaces) via a lookup, plus a precedence rule when two lookups compete. A journey-through-a-translation-layer visual makes the "one name in, many ports out" mechanic and the per-device-wins gate physically visible — far more memorable than a static box diagram. Four placeholders below: the failure, the translation layer, the precedence gate, and the case-sensitivity trap.

Accessibility: One gold "WAN" token enters a switchboard whose lookup panel maps it to port1/port3/wan1, each cord reaching the matching firewall, which keeps its original port name.

3 · The Precedence Gate

When two mappings compete for the same box, the specific one wins.
Specific Beats General
images/s31-precedence-gate.png
Image placeholder — 3:2
Aspect 3:2 Placement: in the Precedence section Anchor: per-device wins (longest-prefix shape)
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalist vector illustration, cream background, bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients. Centrepiece: a single firewall appliance (a recabled "Vaultline HQ 100F", small "emergency recabled" sticky-note on it) approached by TWO competing signposts converging on one gate in front of it. Left signpost, broad and pale (drawn larger but faded, representing the general rule): "PER-PLATFORM · all 100F · WAN → port1". Right signpost, small but bright and sharp (drawn crisp and bold, representing the specific rule): "PER-DEVICE · THIS box · WAN → port5". At the gate, a turnstile clearly admits the PER-DEVICE (port5) signpost — its cord passes through to the appliance and lands green on a port labelled port5 — while the PER-PLATFORM (port1) cord is politely stopped at the turnstile with a small "overridden here" tag (not an error, just superseded). A subtle side-caption panel draws the analogy: a tiny routing-table icon showing "/32 beats /24", labelled "longest-prefix match — same idea". Palette: faded muted blue for the general rule, vivid coral/gold for the specific rule that wins, sage green landing. Small frozen-money-cube logo in a corner. Visual hierarchy: the turnstile gate (centre), the winning per-device signpost (brightest), the routing analogy (small, bottom-corner). No clutter, no code. Purpose: teach that per-device overrides per-platform because specific beats general — the same principle as longest-prefix-match in routing.
Reinforces: per-device wins, and the portable master pattern (specific beats general = longest-prefix routing).
Accessibility: Two mapping signposts reach one firewall; the specific per-device rule (port5) passes the gate and wins while the general per-platform rule (port1) is superseded, echoing longest-prefix routing.

4 · The Silent Killer

The failure that looks correct: one shift key between working and outage.
WAN Is Not wan
images/s31-case-trap.png
Image placeholder — 3:2
Aspect 3:2 Placement: in the Case Sensitivity section Anchor: case-sensitive; two different objects
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalist vector illustration, cream background, bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients. Two nearly-identical keys lie side by side on a workbench, each labelled in monospace on its bow: one reads "WAN" (uppercase), the other reads "wan" (lowercase). A rule-card's keyhole is shaped for the uppercase "WAN" key. The lowercase "wan" key is being inserted by a slightly panicked Junior (Friday, sleeves up) — it looks like it should fit, everything appears correct, but it stops just short and a small coral error banner reads "interface not found". Above the scene, a single dramatic shift-key (a keyboard SHIFT key drawn oversized) hovers like the hinge of the whole problem, with a thin caption ribbon: "one shift key between working and outage." To reinforce that these are TWO OBJECTS not one, draw the two keys casting two clearly separate shadows, each shadow labelled "object". Palette: neutral cream and soft brown for the near-identical keys, coral (#e07856) reserved only for the error banner and the oversized shift key, muted gold accent on the rule-card. Small frozen-money-cube logo in a corner. A calm mentor ribbon (Señor) reads "the machine will not forgive the difference." Visual hierarchy: the two near-twin keys (centre, the whole point), the oversized shift key (top, the cause), the error banner (small but sharp). No code editor, no clutter, generous spacing. Purpose: teach that normalized interface names are case-sensitive — WAN and wan are two different objects, and a case mismatch reproduces the SAME "interface not found" even when everything looks right.
Reinforces: case sensitivity as the silent, "looks-correct" failure — the two-shadows detail cements "two different objects."
Accessibility: Two near-identical keys labelled WAN and wan cast two separate shadows; the lowercase key fails to open a rule shaped for uppercase, producing "interface not found," with an oversized shift key hovering as the cause.

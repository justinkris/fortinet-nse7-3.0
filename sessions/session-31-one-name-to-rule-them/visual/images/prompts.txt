Title: The Package That Only Speaks One Dialect

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, cream off-white background (#faf5e9), clean bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism, storybook-but-technical. Centre: a single glowing policy-package scroll or card labelled "RULE: incoming = port3" in a monospace tag, held by a young overconfident engineer character (Junior — short tousled hair, rolled-sleeve shirt, a small pinned "install preview: 3 changes" screenshot trophy visible on a corkboard behind him). From the card, three delivery arrows shoot toward three firewall appliances drawn as friendly labelled boxes in a row: a "Vaultline 100F" whose WAN port glows and is labelled "port1", a "LoFinance 200F" whose WAN port glows labelled "port3", and a "Parramatta 60E" whose WAN port glows labelled "wan1". The arrow to the 200F (port3) lands with a green check. The arrows to the 100F and 60E hit red barriers with a small error banner reading "interface not found". Every appliance clearly has an internet cable plugged into its glowing WAN port (visual proof the hardware is fine). Muted colour palette: sage green for the success path, coral (#e07856) for the two failures, muted gold accents on the LoFinance ice-cube logo (a stack of banknotes frozen in an ice cube) small in a corner. A speech ribbon from an off-panel mentor (Señor, sombrero just visible at frame edge) reads "the package is monolingual." Visual hierarchy: the single card is largest, the three divergent port names are the second focal point. No dense technical labels beyond the port names and the error banner. Do NOT show any code editor, do NOT show gore or clutter, keep generous white space. Purpose: teach that the install failed on NAME RESOLUTION (the port name differs per model) and NOT on hardware — the cables are all plugged in.

Title: One Name In, Many Ports Out

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, cream background, bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients, no photorealism. Left: a single clean rule-card labelled "RULE: incoming = WAN" where WAN is drawn as a gold abstract token (a keystone or medallion, deliberately NOT a physical port), emphasising it is a role, not a cable. A confident intermediate engineer character (neat, competent, trusted — the student stand-in) hands this one card into the mouth of a central machine drawn as an elegant "translation layer": a horizontal switchboard/loom device with three labelled patch cords emerging from its right side. The switchboard has a visible lookup panel with three rows: "100F → port1", "200F → port3", "60E → wan1". Each patch cord leads to the matching firewall appliance on the right (Vaultline 100F, LoFinance 200F, Parramatta 60E), each now landing with a green check on its correctly-named WAN port. Crucially, above each appliance a tiny persistent label shows the box STILL keeps its own physical name (the translation did not rename it). Palette: gold for the abstract WAN token, sage green success cords, coral used only as a warm accent on the switchboard frame, muted blue for the machine body. Small LoFinance frozen-money-cube logo in a corner. A calm mentor (Señor in sombrero) stands to one side with a raised finger and a small ribbon: "the name is not the intelligence — the translation is." Visual hierarchy: the gold WAN token (largest, leftmost), the three-row lookup panel (centre), the three green landings (right). No code editors, no clutter, generous spacing. Purpose: teach that a normalized interface is ONE abstract policy-layer name plus a per-box mapping that resolves it, substituted only at install, and that it never renames the real port.

Title: Specific Beats General

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, cream background, bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients. Centrepiece: a single firewall appliance (a recabled "Vaultline HQ 100F", small "emergency recabled" sticky-note on it) approached by TWO competing signposts converging on one gate in front of it. Left signpost, broad and pale (drawn larger but faded, representing the general rule): "PER-PLATFORM · all 100F · WAN → port1". Right signpost, small but bright and sharp (drawn crisp and bold, representing the specific rule): "PER-DEVICE · THIS box · WAN → port5". At the gate, a turnstile clearly admits the PER-DEVICE (port5) signpost — its cord passes through to the appliance and lands green on a port labelled port5 — while the PER-PLATFORM (port1) cord is politely stopped at the turnstile with a small "overridden here" tag (not an error, just superseded). A subtle side-caption panel draws the analogy: a tiny routing-table icon showing "/32 beats /24", labelled "longest-prefix match — same idea". Palette: faded muted blue for the general rule, vivid coral/gold for the specific rule that wins, sage green landing. Small frozen-money-cube logo in a corner. Visual hierarchy: the turnstile gate (centre), the winning per-device signpost (brightest), the routing analogy (small, bottom-corner). No clutter, no code. Purpose: teach that per-device overrides per-platform because specific beats general — the same principle as longest-prefix-match in routing.

Title: WAN Is Not wan

Prompt:
Flat minimalist vector illustration, cream background, bold soft-brown outlines, no gradients. Two nearly-identical keys lie side by side on a workbench, each labelled in monospace on its bow: one reads "WAN" (uppercase), the other reads "wan" (lowercase). A rule-card's keyhole is shaped for the uppercase "WAN" key. The lowercase "wan" key is being inserted by a slightly panicked Junior (Friday, sleeves up) — it looks like it should fit, everything appears correct, but it stops just short and a small coral error banner reads "interface not found". Above the scene, a single dramatic shift-key (a keyboard SHIFT key drawn oversized) hovers like the hinge of the whole problem, with a thin caption ribbon: "one shift key between working and outage." To reinforce that these are TWO OBJECTS not one, draw the two keys casting two clearly separate shadows, each shadow labelled "object". Palette: neutral cream and soft brown for the near-identical keys, coral (#e07856) reserved only for the error banner and the oversized shift key, muted gold accent on the rule-card. Small frozen-money-cube logo in a corner. A calm mentor ribbon (Señor) reads "the machine will not forgive the difference." Visual hierarchy: the two near-twin keys (centre, the whole point), the oversized shift key (top, the cause), the error banner (small but sharp). No code editor, no clutter, generous spacing. Purpose: teach that normalized interface names are case-sensitive — WAN and wan are two different objects, and a case mismatch reproduces the SAME "interface not found" even when everything looks right.

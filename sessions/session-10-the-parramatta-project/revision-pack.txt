------------------------------------------------------------------
QUICK SUMMARY (one page)
------------------------------------------------------------------
Parramatta opens Monday and needs the HQ trading feed securely. With
two sites, all topology names collapse into "one tunnel" — but the
pattern is chosen at site two because it becomes the template.

FULL MESH: every site tunnels to every other = n(n-1)/2 tunnels.
3 sites=3, 5=10, 10=45, 20=190. Each tunnel = phase 1 + phase 2 +
policies both ends + a tunnel slot. Branch-model FortiGates support
fewer tunnels than campus models, so the smallest box caps a mesh.
Benefit: direct spoke-to-spoke paths, no hub dependency.

HUB-AND-SPOKE: n-1 tunnels, linear growth, cheap branch hardware.
Costs land ONLY on spoke-to-spoke traffic: (1) double WAN traversal
latency; (2) the hub must DECRYPT then RE-ENCRYPT every spoke-to-
spoke packet (each tunnel = its own SA, its own keys) — the hub
becomes a bottleneck and a single point of failure.

THE DECIDING CRITERION: match the topology's weakness against the
ACTUAL traffic pattern. LoFinance's feed lives at HQ; all critical
traffic is branch<->HQ, which rides the direct tunnel. Hub-and-
spoke's weakness lands where LoFinance has no traffic. HQ = hub,
Parramatta = spoke #1.

REDUNDANCY LAYERS: device (HA cluster), link (dual ISP), tunnel
(IPsec tunnel aggregation — bind two tunnels into one virtual
interface, "LACP for tunnels"). Algorithm choice matters: per-packet
round-robin across unequal-latency links reorders packets (jitter,
glitchy real-time feeds). Use redundant (active/backup) or L3/L4
(per-flow hashing — each flow pins to one tunnel).

PHASE 1 vs PHASE 2: phase 1 = secure authenticated channel between
the firewalls; phase 2 = negotiates what's carried, including
SELECTORS (declared src<->dst subnet pairs). One phase 1 carries
multiple phase 2 selectors. No selector match = never enters the
tunnel (dropped pre-encryption). "A trunk link, but over a tunnel."
Selectors gate entry; firewall policies still inspect — two fences.

CHEAT SHEET
------------------------------------------------------------------
TUNNEL COUNT FORMULAS
  Full mesh:        n(n-1)/2
  Hub-and-spoke:    n-1

STUDY-GUIDE TOPOLOGIES (p.195 diagram)
  Site-to-Site | Hub-and-Spoke | Dual Hub-and-Spoke | Full Mesh (ADVPN)

DESIGN CRITERIA (p.194)
  - Reliable/secure algorithms
  - Scalability for future growth
  - Firewall model at each site (branch = fewer tunnels than campus)
  - Number of tunnels depends on traffic type inside them
  - Robust authentication + appropriate encryption standards
  - (Session lens) WHERE THE TRAFFIC ACTUALLY FLOWS

IPSEC TUNNEL AGGREGATION (p.199-200)
  GUI: VPN > VPN Tunnels (add tunnel as aggregate member)
  CLI:
    config system ipsec-aggregate
        edit "IPSEC_AGG"
            set member "Tunnel1" "Tunnel2"
            set algorithm <redundant|round-robin|L3|L4|weighted-round-robin>
        next
    end
  Algorithms:
    round-robin          per PACKET            (reordering risk)
    L3                   per L3-header flow    (flow-pinned)
    L4                   per L4-header flow    (flow-pinned)
    redundant            active/backup
    weighted-round-robin round-robin w/ weights (per-packet)

PHASE 2 SELECTORS (p.198-199)
  GUI: VPN > VPN Tunnels (phase 2 selectors, editable names)
  One phase 1 -> multiple phase 2 selectors. Most flexible selector
  type = address subnet. IPv4 and IPv6 supported.
  All-zero (0.0.0.0/0) selectors: used in migrations / overlapping-
  subnet partner scenarios, but risk invalid paths when advertising
  many routes — prefer routing protocols to scope allowed subnets.

KEY BEHAVIOURS
  - Each tunnel = its own SA = its own keys. Hub decrypts +
    re-encrypts all spoke-to-spoke transit traffic.
  - Selector-unmatched traffic is dropped BEFORE encryption — it is
    not a policy deny and not a routing failure.
  - Aggregate failover: if one member tunnel fails, another takes
    over (redundancy is the point of aggregation).

DESIGN RULES
  - The pattern is chosen at site two.
  - Judge topology weaknesses against the real traffic pattern.
  - Redundancy is layered: device / link / tunnel.
  - Never per-packet balancing for latency-sensitive flows.
  - Segmentation continues across the WAN via selectors.

------------------------------------------------------------------
FLASHCARDS (Q -> A)
------------------------------------------------------------------
Q: Tunnels needed for a 10-site full mesh?
A: 45. Formula n(n-1)/2.

Q: Tunnels needed for 10 sites hub-and-spoke?
A: 9. Formula n-1.

Q: Two costs hub-and-spoke imposes on spoke-to-spoke traffic?
A: Double WAN traversal (latency) + hub decrypt/re-encrypt burden.

Q: Why must the hub decrypt spoke-to-spoke traffic?
A: Each tunnel is a separate SA with separate keys; spokes never
   share keys.

Q: What does the hub become as spokes multiply?
A: A bottleneck and a single point of failure.

Q: Why can't a branch FortiGate anchor a large full mesh?
A: Branch models support fewer tunnels than campus models — the
   smallest box caps the design.

Q: The design criterion that chose hub-and-spoke for LoFinance?
A: Traffic pattern — the trading feed lives at HQ, so all critical
   traffic is branch<->HQ and rides the direct tunnel.

Q: FortiOS feature binding multiple IPsec tunnels into one virtual
   interface?
A: IPsec tunnel aggregation (config system ipsec-aggregate).

Q: The five aggregation algorithms?
A: round-robin, L3, L4, redundant, weighted-round-robin.

Q: Which algorithm risks out-of-order delivery and why?
A: round-robin (and weighted-round-robin) — per-packet balancing
   across unequal-latency paths reorders packets.

Q: Which algorithms keep a flow on one tunnel?
A: L3 and L4 (header hashing pins each flow).

Q: Phase 1's job?
A: Establish the secure, authenticated channel between endpoints
   and negotiate keys/policies for the SAs.

Q: Phase 2's job?
A: Negotiate what the tunnel carries — selectors defining which
   network segments are encrypted and allowed in.

Q: How many phase 2 selectors per phase 1?
A: Multiple — one phase 1 can carry many phase 2 selectors.

Q: What happens to traffic matching no selector?
A: It never enters the tunnel — dropped before encryption.

Q: Do selectors replace firewall policies on tunnel interfaces?
A: No. Selectors gate entry; policies still inspect/log. Two fences.

Q: The session's selector analogy?
A: "A trunk link, but over a tunnel" — phase 1 = trunk, each
   selector = an allowed VLAN (matching src<->dst subnet pairs).

Q: When do topology names start meaning different things?
A: At three or more sites — but choose the pattern at site two.

PODCAST PROMPT — "The Packet Path", Episode 10
------------------------------------------------------------------
Generate a 10-14 minute podcast episode script for "The Packet
Path," a narrative networking-education show. Two hosts: a warm,
curious NARRATOR and a dry, technically precise ANALYST. Episode
title: "The Parramatta Project."

STORY TO TELL: LoFinance (a Sydney trading firm whose logo is
stacks of money frozen in an ice cube) is opening its first branch
in Parramatta, live Monday. COO Penny fires her three famous
questions in one breath: "How long? How much? Can we do it today?"
The branch needs HQ's real-time trading feed, securely. Junior (the
overconfident helpdesk kid) tapes three terrible connection ideas
to the whiteboard — one involves internet-facing RDP — and Señor
(the eccentric senior engineer in a sombrero) silently turns them
face-down and hands our protagonist, the intermediate engineer, the
marker: "Two sites. One network. Draw it before you cable it."

TECHNICAL CONTENT TO TEACH THROUGH THE STORY:
1. With two sites, every topology name is the same one tunnel — but
   the pattern chosen at site two becomes the template.
2. Full mesh scales at n(n-1)/2 tunnels (3 sites=3, 10 sites=45!)
   and branch-model firewalls can't hold that many tunnels; hub-and-
   spoke scales at n-1.
3. Hub-and-spoke's hidden tax: spoke-to-spoke traffic crosses the
   WAN twice AND the hub must decrypt then re-encrypt every packet,
   because each tunnel has its own keys. The hub becomes a
   bottleneck and single point of failure.
4. THE BIG LESSON: judge a topology against the real traffic
   pattern. LoFinance's feed lives at HQ, so all critical traffic
   is branch-to-HQ — hub-and-spoke's weakness lands where there is
   no traffic. That's why it wins.
5. Redundancy in layers: HA clusters, dual ISPs, and IPsec tunnel
   aggregation ("LACP for tunnels") — and the trap that per-packet
   round-robin over unequal-latency links reorders packets and
   makes real-time feeds glitch. Use redundant or L3/L4 per-flow.
6. Phase 1 builds the authenticated channel; phase 2 selectors
   declare exactly which subnet pairs may enter. Use the student's
   own analogy: "a trunk link, but over a tunnel." A tunnel is a
   declared pipe, not an open hose.

JUNIOR'S MISTAKE (include it): Junior insists the tunnel is "just
open — everything at Parramatta can reach everything at HQ, that's
the point of a tunnel." Señor's one-word rebuttal: "Phase two."
Walk through why the receptionist's PC can never touch the trading
VLAN: the pair was never declared as a selector.

MEMORABLE ANALOGIES TO USE: the garden hose vs the declared pipe;
trunk-over-a-tunnel; LACP for tunnels; "the pattern is chosen at
site two."

END WITH THIS TEASER: The hardware ships Wednesday. Junior arrives
with a printed 2014 blog post recommending IKEv1 aggressive mode
"because it's faster." Señor folds it into a paper aeroplane, bins
it, and says: "Faster — and it shouts your name across the internet
while it does it. Sit. We talk about handshakes. The modern kind."
Next episode: "Handshakes, Modern Style" — IKEv2.

TONE: warm, funny, story-first, but technically rigorous. All facts
above are accurate — do not alter the technical claims.

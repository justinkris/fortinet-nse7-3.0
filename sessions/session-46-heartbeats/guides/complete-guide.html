Heartbeats, Ethertypes, and Two Boxes That Both Work

A cluster formed perfectly and still failed. The fault was in what shared the wire.

A false failover is worse than a real one — you took the outage and got nothing for it.

SESSION OVERVIEW

The cluster wasn't broken. It was deafened by its own chatter.

The second FortiGate 900G arrived on a Monday. Same model, same firmware, correctly licensed — everything Session 45 said it had to be. Junior cabled two heartbeat links direct-connect, typed the HA stanza himself, and the cluster formed. And then it began to bicker: sync status flickering, three help-desk tickets reading "internet went weird for a second", and Cipher walking past the rack to deliver a four-word incident report — "That rhythm is wrong."

This session is about the two things that go wrong after a cluster forms correctly. First: FGCP's own signalling — which ethertypes carry what, where they physically travel, and what happens when you make the control plane share a wire with bulk data. Second: Penny's objection that she now owns two firewalls and one of them does nothing — answered not with active-active, but with virtual clustering and VDOM partitioning.

Learning objectives. Identify the three FGCP ethertypes and what each carries · sniff heartbeat traffic on the correct interface · explain the default session-synchronisation path and the false-failover risk it creates · configure dedicated session-sync interfaces and predict their fallback behaviour · define virtual clustering and its constraints · configure VDOM partitioning with per-vcluster priority · size a partitioned cluster correctly.

Story recap in one line. A correctly-built cluster produced intermittent micro-outages; the heartbeat was alive but starving, and the fix was to give session synchronisation a road of its own — after which Penny got both boxes working without buying a third.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
A cluster that forms is not a cluster that works. Formation is a checklist; behaviour is an engineering problem.

💭 THINK FIRST · ETHERTYPES

Junior went diagnostic before touching configuration — genuinely the right instinct. He ran a sniffer for the heartbeat ethertype, saw nothing at all, and concluded the heartbeat was dead. He was about to reboot a production firewall on the strength of that silence. Before you read on, work out what is wrong with his evidence.

  • FGCP does not travel over IP. What does it travel over, and how many distinct ethertypes are in the family?
  • Junior sniffed port1 and saw nothing. What has he actually proven?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Three: 0x8890 (heartbeat, NAT/route mode), 0x8891 (heartbeat, transparent mode) and 0x8893 (configuration synchronisation). FGCP is Ethernet-typed, which is why an IP-based capture filter would never see it.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
Nothing. He has proven only that heartbeat frames do not traverse port1 — which is expected, because they only ever traverse interfaces declared as hbdev. Absence of evidence gathered from the wrong place is not evidence of absence.
SECTION 01

FGCP speaks its own language — three ethertypes

The FGCP family: two ethertypes keep you alive, one keeps you identical.

FortiGate HA uses FGCP for all HA-related communication, and that traffic travels over the links you designate as heartbeats. The important structural fact — the one the exam leans on — is that FGCP uses its own Ethernet type rather than the IP protocol. It is not TCP, not UDP, not ICMP. If you build a capture filter around IP, you will never see it.

EthertypeCarriesWhen
0x8890HeartbeatNAT / route mode cluster
0x8891HeartbeatTransparent mode cluster
0x8893Configuration synchronisationAlways

The heartbeat ethertypes do far more work than the name suggests. They are responsible for discovering other FortiGate devices in the same HA group, electing the primary, synchronising other data, and detecting when a device fails so traffic can be redistributed. Everything that makes a cluster a cluster, rather than two firewalls in a rack, rides on those frames.

Analogy

88-90 keeps you alive. 88-93 keeps you identical.

  • The heartbeat family is a pulse — small, constant, and meaningful only because it is on time.
  • Config sync is a photocopier — bursty, larger, and meaningful only because it is accurate.
  • Different jobs, different tolerances. That distinction is the whole of Section 02.
FortiOS CLI — sniff the heartbeat (NAT/route mode)
diagnose sniffer packet port3 "ether proto 0x8890" 4
Verification discipline. Before you interpret a sniffer's silence, confirm you are on an interface listed in hbdev. Run show system ha and read the set hbdev line first. Silence from the wrong port is not a finding.
Common mistake — the one that cost Junior an hour. Sniffing a data interface for 0x8890, seeing nothing, and diagnosing a dead heartbeat. A close second: hedging away from a correct first instinct. 0x8890 and 0x8891 are both heartbeat — they differ by operating mode, not by function.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Verify your instrument before you trust its result. A negative capture on the wrong interface is not evidence.

💭 THINK FIRST · SESSION SYNC

Junior repoints the sniffer at port3 and the heartbeat is there — a steady drumbeat, every second, both directions. Alive. Which makes the incident worse, not better: the cluster is bickering while its heartbeat is perfectly healthy. The answer is hiding in something that was never on the ethertype list.

  • Session synchronisation has no ethertype of its own in that list. Given the cables that physically exist between these two boxes, what path is it forced to take?
  • A heartbeat is a small frame whose only job is to arrive on time. What happens to it when it shares a wire with a flood of session-table data at market open?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
By default, session synchronisation occurs over the HA heartbeat link. Nobody configured a dedicated path, and the only other cables are carrying customer traffic — so it has nowhere else to go.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
It is delayed or dropped. The cluster interprets missing heartbeats as a dead partner and fails over. Nothing was actually wrong: this is a false failover, an outage manufactured entirely by the redundancy mechanism.
SECTION 02

The heart and the mouth should not share a throat

Session sync on the heartbeat wire: the control plane starved by the data it is trying to protect.

When session synchronisation is enabled, you may select dedicated interfaces for it. By default, session synchronisation occurs over the HA heartbeat link. That default is the entire fault in this episode.

Fortinet's own guidance is unambiguous about why: session synchronisation requires high bandwidth, and when it is not separated from the heartbeat it degrades the heartbeat's reliability. With a very large number of sessions to synchronise, the result is potential loss of heartbeats and false failover. Dedicated links reduce the bandwidth demand on the heartbeat interface and improve the efficiency and performance of the cluster.

FortiOS CLI — give session sync its own road
config system ha
    set session-pickup enable
    set session-sync-dev "port5" "port6"
end

Two behaviours to hold onto, because both are examinable:

  • Multiple interfaces → load balancing. If you select more than one interface, session synchronisation traffic is load balanced among them.
  • Total failure → fallback. If all the session synchronisation interfaces become disconnected, session synchronisation reverts to using the HA heartbeat link.
The sneakiest thing in this lesson. That fallback is graceful — you do not lose session sync. But it silently returns you to the exact configuration that caused tonight's incident, and nothing alarms you. Monitor the dedicated sync interfaces explicitly, or you will rediscover this fault at the worst possible hour. Any fallback that does not alarm is a fault waiting to recur.
Senior insight. A false failover is worse than a real one. In a real failover you took an outage and bought availability with it. In a false failover you took the outage and got nothing.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Never let a latency-critical control plane share bandwidth with a throughput-hungry data plane. The heart and the mouth should not share a throat.

💭 THINK FIRST · VIRTUAL CLUSTERING

Penny arrives with coffee and an objection: she now owns two 900Gs and one of them does nothing, while the Trading and Compliance VDOMs both run hot on the same box. She refuses to buy a third firewall. Junior's instinct is active-active. Yours should not be — and the reason reaches back to Session 45.

  • Why does active-active fail to solve the specific problem Penny described?
  • Virtual clustering runs in active-passive mode only. So how can both boxes possibly carry real traffic at the same time? What word has to change meaning?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
In active-active the primary owns the virtual MACs, so every packet still arrives at the primary first; selected sessions then take an extra hop out to the secondary and back. It adds a toll booth without moving the front door — and the front door was Penny's actual problem.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
'Primary' stops being a property of the box and becomes a property of the VDOM. Each member is simultaneously primary for some VDOMs and secondary for others. The cluster is still active-passive; it is just active-passive several times over, in opposite directions.
SECTION 03

Virtual clustering — primary becomes a property of the VDOM

Both boxes wear a crown — for different territories. The cluster is still active-passive.

Virtual clustering is essentially a cluster of FortiGate devices operating with multiple VDOMs enabled — an extension of FGCP rather than a separate protocol. It inherits every standard HA requirement (same model, firmware, licensing, drive configuration, operating mode; matching group ID, group name, password and heartbeat settings) with one change: the maximum HA group ID value is seven.

ConstraintValueWhy it matters
HA modeActive-passive onlyJunior will try to type a-a here. It is wrong.
Maximum virtual clusters30Sizing ceiling for large multi-tenant designs
Maximum HA group ID7Lower than standard HA — classic exam detail
MembersTwo FortiGatesFor more than two with VDOMs, look to FGSP instead

For load-balancing sessions there are two options within a cluster: active-active mode, or — within active-passive — VDOM partitioning. Penny's problem is a territory problem, not a shared-work problem, which is why partitioning is the correct instrument.

Scaling note. If you need a cluster of more than two FortiGates operating with multiple VDOMs, virtual clustering is not the answer. Consider designs that avoid multiple VDOMs in one cluster, or use standalone session synchronisation with FGSP.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Virtual clustering does not make active-passive into active-active. It makes 'primary' a per-VDOM role, so active-passive can point in two directions at once.

💭 THINK FIRST · VDOM PARTITIONING

You now have the concept. The mechanism is the interesting part — Fortinet had four primary-election criteria available and chose exactly one for this job. Then there is the bill, which is the part Penny must hear before she signs.

  • Of the four election criteria — connected monitored interfaces, HA uptime, priority, serial number — which one does VDOM partitioning use, and why that one specifically?
  • NGFW-2 fails completely. What happens to the Compliance VDOM's traffic, and what does that imply about how you must size these two firewalls?
Q1 — MODEL ANSWER
Priority, set per vcluster. It is the only criterion that is administratively assigned: interface state and uptime are facts the network hands you, and serial number is stamped at the factory. If you want to choose a winner, you must use the dial you control.
Q2 — MODEL ANSWER
The surviving device processes all traffic for all VDOMs. Therefore each member must be sized for 100% of total load, not 50%. VDOM partitioning delivers utilisation of the second box — it does not deliver capacity from it.
SECTION 04

VDOM partitioning — and the bill that comes with it

To configure VDOM partitioning you set one cluster device as the primary for some VDOMs and the other as primary for the rest. You control the distribution of traffic by adjusting which device is primary for each VDOM, and set priority determines the role of the cluster device for each VDOM — highest priority wins that vcluster.

FortiOS CLI — NGFW-1
config system ha
    set mode a-p
    set vcluster-status enable
    config vcluster
        edit 1
            set override disable
            set priority 200
            set vdom "VDOM1" "root"
        next
        edit 2
            set override disable
            set priority 50
            set vdom "VDOM2"
        next
    end
end
FortiOS CLI — NGFW-2 (mirrored priorities)
config system ha
    set mode a-p
    set vcluster-status enable
    config vcluster
        edit 1
            set override disable
            set priority 100
            set vdom "VDOM1" "root"
        next
        edit 2
            set override disable
            set priority 200
            set vdom "VDOM2"
        next
    end
end

Read the two stanzas side by side and the pattern is obvious: vcluster 1 goes to NGFW-1 (200 beats 100), vcluster 2 goes to NGFW-2 (200 beats 50). Same two boxes, opposite outcomes, decided independently per cluster. Note also that the root VDOM must be assigned somewhere — here it travels with VDOM1 in vcluster 1.

The sizing trap. Two members each running at 45% under normal load looks like a healthy design and is not one. During a failover the survivor inherits everything — its own load plus its partner's — and saturates. You get packet loss and dropped sessions during precisely the incident the cluster was purchased to survive. The insurance policy pays out at half value on the one day you claim.
Analogy

It is not two boxes' worth of firewall. It is one box's worth, running in two places.

  • Utilisation is how much of what you bought is currently doing something.
  • Capacity is how much load the design can survive losing a member.
  • VDOM partitioning improves the first and changes nothing about the second.
Business translation for Penny. "Both firewalls now carry production traffic, so nothing sits idle — and either one can run the entire firm on its own if the other dies. That second property is why they are the size they are."
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Utilisation is not capacity. Size every cluster member for 100% of total load, then partition to use what you have already paid for.

SECTION 05

Troubleshooting workflow — the timing detail is the diagnosis

The workflow that solved this incident is short, and its first step is the one most engineers skip.

  1. Restate the symptom with its scope and timing. "Repeated failovers, both members healthy, all monitored interfaces up, only during market open, never overnight."
  2. Let the timing eliminate causes. Override, priority misconfiguration and interface state are all time-of-day independent. A symptom with a schedule has a cause with a load profile. This single move discards three plausible hypotheses for free.
  3. Verify your instrument before trusting it. Confirm which interfaces are hbdev before you sniff, and before you interpret silence.
  4. Observe under the conditions that produce the fault. Sniff during peak, not at 4pm when everything works.
  5. Correlate. Match heartbeat gaps against failover timestamps in the event log.
  6. Fix the cause, not the symptom. Separating the sync path fixes it. Lengthening heartbeat timers only makes the cluster slower to detect a real failure.
Exam tips. (1) "Only at market open / only under load" is never scene-setting — it is the discriminator. (2) 0x8890 and 0x8891 are both heartbeat; the difference is operating mode. (3) Virtual clustering is a-p only — any answer offering a-a is a distractor. (4) Watch for the fallback question: all sync interfaces down means revert to heartbeat link, not "sessions are lost".
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
If a symptom has a schedule, the cause has a load profile.

SECTION 06

Key takeaways

  • FGCP is Ethernet-typed: 0x8890 heartbeat (NAT/route), 0x8891 heartbeat (transparent), 0x8893 config sync.
  • The heartbeat lives on hbdev and nowhere else. Sniff there or learn nothing.
  • Session sync defaults to the heartbeat link — the single most consequential default in this lesson.
  • Dedicated sync interfaces load balance; losing all of them reverts to the heartbeat link, silently.
  • Virtual clustering: a-p only, max 30 clusters, max group ID 7.
  • VDOM partitioning selects per-VDOM primaries with set priority, because priority is the only criterion you assign.
  • Failover consolidates everything onto the survivor. Size for 100%.
Señor's line, for the wall: "The heart and the mouth should not share a throat."

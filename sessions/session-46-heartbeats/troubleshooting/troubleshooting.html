SESSION 46 · TROUBLESHOOTING · FGCP HEARTBEAT & VIRTUAL CLUSTERING

Diagnosing a cluster that argues with itself

How a senior engineer separates 'I cannot hear the heartbeat' from 'the heartbeat is being drowned out'.

If a symptom has a schedule, the cause has a load profile.

Troubleshooting guide · Session 46 · Study Guide 04, pp. 89–92

01

Symptoms that lead here

  • Repeated or intermittent failovers with no obvious cause — both members healthy, all monitored interfaces up.
  • HA sync status flickering between in-sync and out-of-sync.
  • Brief user-visible interruptions: "the internet went weird for a second."
  • Failovers that correlate with business hours, batch windows, or market open — never overnight.
  • A sniffer for heartbeat traffic returning nothing at all.
  • After enabling VDOM partitioning: the wrong member is primary for a VDOM, or one member saturates after a failover.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Two very different faults present identically here: 'I cannot hear the heartbeat' and 'the heartbeat is being drowned out'. Separate them before you act.
02

Questions to ask before touching anything

  1. What exactly is the symptom, in one sentence, with its timing? Write it down. "Failovers at market open only" is a different fault from "failovers".
  2. What is the scope? One VDOM or all? One member or both? All users or one segment?
  3. Is there a schedule? If yes, the cause is load-related and every time-independent hypothesis is already eliminated.
  4. What changed? New member added, new VDOM, new traffic volume, firmware.
  5. Which interfaces are actually hbdev? Answer this before you sniff anything.
  6. Does session sync have dedicated interfaces? If not, you already have a candidate root cause.
  7. Are the dedicated sync interfaces up right now? If they went down, you are silently back on the heartbeat link.
Discipline. Every question above is answerable without changing a single line of configuration. Finish them before you change anything — a config change made mid-diagnosis destroys the evidence you needed.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Restate the symptom, its scope and its timing as your first move. The timing detail usually is the diagnosis.

  1. Is the heartbeat visible on the hbdev?
    No → verify you are on the right interface; then check cabling, interface status, and that both members share group-id, group-name and password. Yes → continue.
  2. Does the symptom have a schedule or load correlation?
    No → investigate monitored interfaces, override configuration, priority, and physical link stability. Yes → continue.
  3. Is session-sync-dev configured?
    No → strong candidate root cause. Sync is on the heartbeat link and starving it. Yes → continue.
  4. Are all dedicated sync interfaces currently up?
    No → you have silently reverted to the heartbeat link. Same fault, hidden behind a graceful fallback.
  5. Correlate heartbeat gaps against failover timestamps. If they align with peak volume, you have proven it rather than assumed it.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
Every branch above is chosen by evidence you already gathered. If you are choosing a branch on a hunch, go back to step one.
05

Common dead ends and likely root causes

Dead ends:

  • Rebooting the "faulty" member. It fixes nothing and destroys the HA uptime evidence.
  • Lengthening heartbeat timers to stop the flapping. This masks the symptom and makes the cluster slower to detect a genuine failure. You have traded a false failover for a slow real one.
  • Sniffing a data interface. Guarantees a false negative.
  • Blaming override. Override explains failback loops, not load-correlated flapping. It has no schedule.
  • Re-forming the cluster from scratch. The cluster formed correctly. Formation was never the fault.

Likely root causes, in the order they occur in practice:

  1. Session sync sharing the heartbeat link under high session volume — the classic.
  2. Dedicated sync interfaces failed, triggering the silent fallback.
  3. Single heartbeat interface with no redundancy, plus a marginal cable.
  4. Sniffing or monitoring the wrong interface — an observation fault, not a network fault.
  5. In a partitioned cluster: priorities set on only one member, so the intended distribution never takes effect.
  6. A partitioned cluster sized at 50% per member, saturating after an otherwise successful failover.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
A false failover is worse than a real one. Fix the contention; never fix the detector.
06

Exam traps

  • Timing is not scene-setting. "Only during business hours" exists in the question to eliminate every time-independent cause. Use it.
  • 0x8891 is not config sync. 0x8890 and 0x8891 are both heartbeat — NAT/route and transparent respectively. 0x8893 is config sync.
  • All sync interfaces down does not mean sessions are lost. Sync reverts to the heartbeat link.
  • Virtual clustering in active-active is always a distractor. It is a-p only.
  • Group ID 7 is the virtual-clustering ceiling — expect a question offering higher values.
  • Priority, not uptime, selects the per-VDOM primary. Uptime is a fact; priority is a decision.
  • Failover consolidates all VDOMs. Any answer implying the surviving member keeps carrying only its own share is wrong.
  • Both members need the configuration. Priorities are set per member; configuring one is a half-built design.
🧠
MENTAL NOTE
On any HA scenario question: restate the symptom, the scope, the timing, and what those rule out — before you look at the options.

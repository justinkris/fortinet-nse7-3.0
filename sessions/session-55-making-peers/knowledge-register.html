NEW TOPIC: BGP TRANSPORT & SESSION ESTABLISHMENT (TCP 179, state machine, base config)
==================================================================
Confidence:            ★★★★☆
Mental model quality:  STRONG, with one clearly-located soft spot.
                       The student's reasoning is at its best when the question is
                       MECHANICAL — "what must be physically true for this to work." It is
                       weakest when the question is about DESIGN CONSEQUENCE — "why would an
                       engineer choose to do it this way." This is the sharpest and most
                       useful pattern to emerge in two seasons, and it is now clearly
                       separable. See GROWTH AREAS.

Strong areas:
  - TCP-vs-IP transport trade-off: on his FIRST answer he produced BOTH halves — that
    neighbours must be explicitly configured, AND that BGP requires an underlying routing
    protocol to reach the peer at all. The second of those is the bootstrap dependency, and
    most learners never notice it unaided. He framed it in his own words: "The way it
    connects to the IP it can have a underlay routing protocol to do so."
  - Precision under redirect: when pushed from a vague answer ("TCP can exchange prefixes")
    toward specifics, he delivered exactly and only the right list — "sequence numbers, acks,
    retransmissions." No padding, no guessing. He responds VERY well to being told the answer
    is in the right area but needs to be more specific. This is a reliable coaching lever.
  - TTL / multihop: named TTL unprompted as an Active-state cause, then when asked to justify
    it correctly identified BOTH the symptom (Active, because the SYN never arrived) AND the
    topological condition ("the connection could be a few hops away") AND the default value
    ("TTL is by default only 1 for BGP TCP"). Three-for-three on a concept not yet formally
    taught. S56's multihop material is already half-built in his head.
  - NETWORK-IMPORT-CHECK — the strongest answer of the session, and essentially unaided:
    "192.168.10.0/24 with the exact mask needs to be in its routing table to be injected into
    BGP." The word EXACT is the load-bearing part and he supplied it himself. He was given a
    reasoning prompt ("what would the router need to BELIEVE about that prefix") and went
    straight to the mechanism. This is the derivation-before-teaching pattern from S45/S47/S54
    firing again, now in a config-behaviour context rather than a purely conceptual one —
    which is a meaningful extension of the skill.
  - Correct instinct on attributes: when finally pointed at the right question, "It depends on
    the BGP attributes when it enters the local RIB" is the right mechanism, correctly named,
    even though it took two redirects to get there.

Weak areas:
  - **DESIGN-RATIONALE QUESTIONS ARE ANSWERED AS SLOGANS.** The router-id question was
    two-part; both parts came back at a level of generality that carries no information:
    "it would break things if two of the same router was in two different places" and
    "it's better to set one statically, because you need to have a design mindset with BGP."
    The second is not an argument, it is a value statement. He knows uniqueness matters — he
    does not know WHERE the collision is detected (inside the OPEN message), and therefore
    cannot use it diagnostically. Both parts had to be taught outright.
  - **QUESTION-SCOPE DRIFT.** On the database-vs-routing-table question he answered about
    ADVERTISING ("know the routing table before injecting prefixes to advertise") when the
    question was explicitly about routes RECEIVED from the ISP. He had just done a strong
    piece of reasoning on advertising, and carried its frame forward into a question that had
    moved on. Required two redirects — one to change the direction of flow, one to narrow to
    best-path selection. This is a variant of the clause-collapse pattern carried from S43-S47:
    not omitting a clause this time, but importing the previous question's frame.
  - **DELIVERABLE-FORMAT RECOGNITION (the money sentence).** He stalled outright: "I don't
    understand whats needed from me right now." Not a knowledge failure — once the format was
    re-explained with a worked example he produced a good sentence immediately. This is a
    RECOGNITION failure and it is directly attributable to the drill having lapsed for one
    session, exactly as the S54 register warned it would.

Common mistakes (this session):
  - Substituting a general principle for a specific mechanism when asked "why."
  - Carrying the previous question's frame into the next question.

GROWTH AREAS

- **PRIMARY (new, and now the leading candidate for the Season 5 growth priority):
  MECHANISM-VS-RATIONALE ASYMMETRY.** He can reliably derive HOW something works from
  constraints. He cannot yet reliably articulate WHY an engineer would choose it, and defaults
  to slogans when asked ("you need a design mindset"). This matters disproportionately because

  correct it — ask "what specifically breaks if you don't?" Force the consequence, not the
  principle. Track whether the gap closes across S56-S60.

  marking direction changes: "now switch — this is about routes coming IN."

LEARNING NOTES
==================================================================
- The single most reliable technique with this student remains **narrowing the aperture rather
  than supplying the answer**. Every one of his best answers this session came after a redirect
  of the form "right area — now be specific about X." Every answer that had to be taught
  outright was one where the redirect was skipped or where the question asked for rationale
  rather than mechanism.
- He does not perform well on questions that ask him to justify a practice in the abstract.
  Reframe every "why is this good practice" question as "what specifically breaks if you
  don't." Expect a markedly better answer to the second form. TEST THIS DELIBERATELY in S56.
- Config-free conceptual sessions worked in S54; this session mixed heavy Socratic dialogue
  with real CLI and the mix held up well. The CLI did not degrade the reasoning. Continue the
  mix.
- The story load in this episode was high (whole cast, comic set piece) and did not appear to
  cost engagement — his answers stayed technical throughout. The formula tolerates a big cold
  open.
- Half-teaching a concept and parking it for the next session worked well here (TTL/multihop).
  He derived the condition himself and the material is pre-warmed for S56. Consider this
  technique again for a future two-part topic.
- Do not over-credit the ★★★★☆ on BGP transport. It has not survived a troubleshooting
  gauntlet or a cold multi-part question set. The genuine test is a boss-battle session where
  he must read a state and select a layer without prompting.

SESSION 33 · VISUAL LEARNING · FORTIMANAGER SCRIPTS

The Sorting Office of Señor

One letter, three addresses, three fates — the run-target machine as a place you can walk through in memory.

Recommended visual style for this session: Real-World Analogy + Process Flow hybrid — "The Postal Sorting Office." Session 33's core content is fundamentally about routing: the same letter (script) posted to three different addresses (run targets) meets three different fates (live delivery + receipt, staged in a mailroom shelf, staged in the head-office archive). A memory-palace-style sorting office building lets each target become a physical room, each status flag a physical object, and the Import gate a literal locked door — which is exactly the mental model built live in the session. A pure topology diagram would waste this session's strongest analogy; a decision tree is already covered by the troubleshooting guide.

Walkthrough

Picture the FortiManager Sorting Office. Letters (scripts) arrive at the front counter where Señor, in reading glasses, stamps each with one of three addresses. From there, three corridors: the Courier Door (Remote FortiGate — straight to the street, receipt photographed on return), the Mailroom Shelf (Device Database — letter filed in a pigeonhole marked MODIFIED, waiting for a delivery van called Install), and the Head-Office Archive upstairs (Policy Package — the shared master ledger, its own MODIFIED flag, its own delivery van). Between the mailroom and the archive stands a heavy locked door labelled IMPORT — HUMAN KEY ONLY. Nothing climbs those stairs on its own. The images below furnish each room.

Accessibility: cutaway illustration of a two-storey sorting office; a mentor stamps script-envelopes at a counter; three routes show a courier delivering directly with a photographed receipt, a mailroom shelf holding a "Modified" envelope beside an "Install" van, and an upstairs archive ledger with its own flag and van; a locked "Import" door guards the stairs.
Memory AnchorThe run target is the address on the envelope; Modified is an envelope on a shelf; Install is the van; Import is the human-only door.

5 · The Letter and the Person — CLI vs Tcl
Aspect ratio 3:2 · Placement: beside the languages section
IMAGE PLACEHOLDER · session-33-visual-05-letter-person.png
Purpose: make the language trade-off instantly retrievable — identical letters vs a thinking messenger — anchored to the Melbourne port2 box so the concrete use case travels with the abstraction.
Image Generation Prompt
Flat minimalistic detailed vector illustration, cream background, coral/sage/muted-gold palette, soft brown outlines, no gradients, storybook-professional, split-panel composition divided by a thin vertical gold line. LEFT PANEL, titled with a small plaque "CLI — THE LETTER": a stack of thirty perfectly identical envelopes fanned out, each showing the same ten tidy suggested lines of pseudo-FortiOS; twenty-nine identical branch-firewall houses receive them happily with green ticks — but ONE house at the row's end is visibly different (its door on the opposite side, a small tag "port2 — Melbourne"), and its identical letter produces a small red question mark above its chimney. Caption ribbon: "Cannot decide — cannot make NEW mistakes." RIGHT PANEL, titled "TCL — THE PERSON": a clever messenger character built subtly from curly brackets and a feather quill, standing at the fork before the same odd Melbourne house, holding two different letters and visibly CHOOSING — a small thought bubble shows a tiny branching flowchart (if / else). The Melbourne house now gets a green tick. But the messenger trails a long tangled ribbon of dense bracket-symbols behind them, and a small tired 2am engineer with a coffee squints at the ribbon under a desk lamp. Caption ribbon: "Can decide — costs complexity." Do NOT include readable code, vendor logos, or photorealism. Learning purpose: CLI scripts are fixed and safe; Tcl adds per-device logic (the Melbourne port2 case) at the price of auditability.
Accessibility: split panel — left, thirty identical script-letters succeed everywhere except one differently-built "Melbourne port2" house that gets a question mark; right, a bracket-shaped messenger chooses between two letters at the same house and succeeds, while trailing a tangled ribbon a tired engineer must audit at 2am.
Memory AnchorThe letter cannot make decisions — and cannot make new mistakes. Pay the Tcl tax only when a house is genuinely different.

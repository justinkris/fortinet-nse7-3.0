1 · Season Recap

Where Season 2 went

Season 2 opened with a single business need — connect Parramatta to HQ securely — and ended with you dismantling a sabotaged inspection chain while an acquisition was signed behind the glass. In between, you carried real engineering responsibility as the design lead Penny named you at the Season 1 close.

The arc, in three movements: the VPN build-out (IPsec topology design, IKEv2, phase 1/phase 2, the Parramatta fibre-cut outage that forced an emergency bridge and a permanent ipsec-aggregate, then MTU/PMTUD), the routing layer (OSPF areas, the ABR/ASBR hub, Chatswood as Area 1, the DR path — with S18/S19 internals deliberately skipped), and the security crusade (Cipher's SSL inspection push — certificate vs deep, re-sign and CA distribution, SNI/SAN, TLS control; then web filter, application control, ISDB; IPS sensors, tuning, and false positives; UTM placement across the fabric; and finally the boss-battle finale where the inspection tools themselves were the fault).

The topology grew from one site to a genuine multi-site enterprise with an internal segmentation firewall promoted to a dual NGFW role, a data-centre firewall guarding the trading servers, and inspection layered where it belonged. As Session 22's summary put it: "the network grew smarter, not bigger." The season closed with the Vaultline Financial acquisition — seven inherited firewalls, and the launchpad for Season 3.

3 · What Went Well

Your strongest work

Reasoning from first principles, then transferring it. Again and again you reached the "textbook" answer before it was supplied — the ABR's per-area LSDB (S14), the full-inspection MITM mechanism with CA distribution (S20), the local-IPS-database deduction from your own autoupdate rule (S24). By the crusade you were coining your own keeper analogies ("machine gun not handgun", S24) — you can only compress a concept into a fresh metaphor if you own it. In enterprise work this is what lets you handle a device you've never configured; on the NSE7 it's what lets you eliminate distractors instead of guessing.

Evidence before change, under real pressure. During the Parramatta outage (S13) you offered hypothesis and the evidence command together, unprompted, and read a routing table cold to trace where the packet would die. In S17 you used a prior-session fact (redistribution ≠ deletion) to invalidate your own live hypothesis mid-incident — a more advanced form of retention than recall. This discipline is the backbone of both incident response and scenario exam questions.

Fault discrimination — the finale's headline. In S27 you had two faults that both let an attack through and correctly named the one that owned the packet (disabled signature 51398) from the evidence, refusing to blame the louder Monitor action. That is senior-grade fault isolation, and it directly closed the S26 weakness.

Business-aware design instinct. You refused "give trading the same deep inspection as Finance" and reasoned to certificate-inspection — security enforced, latency untouched. The security/latency trade-off is now reflexive, even where the verbal framing of it still needs work.

Non-defensive self-correction. Every flagged drift this season — the broad-brush reach (S24), the reward-drift to full inspection on plain HTTP (S25), the Q3 off-target first answer (S27) — you corrected cleanly, usually in one nudge, without defending. This is why your learning compounds.

4 · What Could Be Improved

Recurring patterns worth naming

Reading raw diagnostic output cold. Your command selection is senior — you reach for the right tool on the right box in the right order. But interpreting an unfamiliar session or flow dump without a walkthrough is the lagging half: in S27 you chose diagnose sys session list correctly but couldn't read the empty av_idx unaided. Risk: on the exam and in the field, the fault often lives in a field you skimmed past. A senior reads the dump line-by-line and knows which field is load-bearing. Habit to practise: for every session/flow output, name what each non-zero and each zero field is telling you before drawing a conclusion.

Verdict-label vs reasoning (now closing). Through S26 you would build the correct argument and then sign the wrong verdict (Q8: verdict "true" while your reasoning proved "false"; the reward-drift to the flattering label on Q2). The finale tested this hard and you passed — but the residue showed in S27 Q3, where your first answer was a true-but-not-graded statement instead of the claim on the page. Habit: after reasoning, pick the verdict your reasoning proves, and grade the stem's exact claim — not an adjacent truth.

Business translation arrives second. Your fixes are correct and the money is always present — it just trails the technical noun. In S27 Q4 you rejected "deep inspect trading" with "SSL inspection will cause latency… and latency will cost money" — money last. To George, that's three words too late. Habit: lead with the consequence, let the mechanism follow.

Minor: flow-debug completeness. Twice in the finale you set filters and enabled debug but omitted diagnose debug flow trace start N — which produces no output. Small, but exam-punishable. Commit the four-line ritual to muscle memory.

5 · The Single Biggest Improvement

From building to proving

At the start of the season you were a builder: you designed the Parramatta topology, negotiated the tunnels, laid the routes. Competent, but configuration-forward. By the finale you were an investigator: handed a folder with no summary and three masking faults, you refused to touch anything until you'd proven what each packet was doing — and then signed precise root-cause verdicts that matched your evidence.

The clearest measure is how you handle a fault you can't see. In S13 you predicted "Denied by forward policy check (policy 0)" and its mechanism before Señor ran anything. In S17 you killed your own hypothesis with a remembered fact. In S27 you distinguished the fault that owned the packet from the fault behind it. That is a shift from "what should I configure?" to "what does the evidence prove?" — the single most valuable change an engineer can make, because it's the difference between fixing things and breaking more of them.

6 · The Single Most Important Growth Area

Speak money first

If we improve only one thing in Season 3, it is business-register framing — leading with the consequence, not the mechanism.

This matters most because it is the only standing weakness that is neither a knowledge gap nor a reasoning gap — your engineering is already there. It appeared in S25 (Q8: correct latency answer, but "latency-sensitive" first), in S26 (Q10: money present but mechanism-led), and again in S27 (Q4: money last). Three sessions, same shape. It affects real work directly: when you explain a design to Penny or George, the first clause decides whether they keep listening. George — introduced in S26 precisely as your drill vehicle — tunes out at technical nouns and is won only by money-per-microsecond framing.

What success looks like by the end of Season 3: when asked to justify a decision to a stakeholder, the consequence is the first thing out of your mouth, the mechanism trails as justification, and George says "first time security spoke my language" without needing a second attempt. Practise immediately: every time you state a technical fix this season, re-state it once leading with the money or risk.

Secondary (briefly): raw diagnostic-output reading — the concrete pre-exam drill. Important, but a reps problem, not a habit problem, so it ranks below the register priority.

7 · Troubleshooting Method Review

How you actually troubleshoot

Strong behaviours

  • Scope and geography first. You consistently ask "who is affected and who isn't" — and treat that split as evidence (S27: corp broken, trading fine).
  • Right box, right direction. You chose the DCFW inbound path for the trading-API fault without prompting — many engineers waste time on the wrong device.
  • Evidence before change. Hypothesis and verification command offered together (S12/S13); no config touched until behaviour was proven.
  • Follow the packet in travel order. A durable discipline since S12, applied cleanly through the finale.
  • Verify recovery AND root cause. You don't stop at "service restored" — you name the cause.

Habits to improve

  • Read the output line-by-line before concluding. The command is right; the interpretation sometimes skips a load-bearing field.
  • Complete the diagnostic ritual (the trace start N line).
  • State the verdict in the graded form — for a T/F or MCQ, the label is the answer.

Senior engineer comparison

A senior would keep your scoping, your box/direction instinct, and your evidence-before-change discipline unchanged — they are already at grade. They would add a slower, field-by-field read of raw output, and would remove the habit of answering with an adjacent truth instead of the exact claim asked.

Your personalised framework for Season 3:
1. Restate the business symptom — note who is / isn't affected.
2. Pick the box and direction that own the flow.
3. Ask: is inspection/UTM even bound here? (read the field, don't assume).
4. Which policy matched, and did inspection actually run?
5. Gather evidence before changing anything — read the policy/sensor.
6. Name the fault that owns THIS packet; distinguish the fault behind it.
7. Verify recovery AND root cause — then price the fix in the stakeholder's currency.

Technology tied to LoFinance

You connected technical decisions to business requirements reliably this season. You framed tunnel redundancy as protecting the trading feed (S13), chose redundant over per-packet balancing to avoid reordering the feed, and — the season's clearest example — refused blanket deep inspection on the trading floor because the microseconds cost real money (S26/S27). The Vaultline acquisition is signed. On Monday, a pallet arrives in the loading dock — seven firewalls from a company that did everything almost like you do, and nothing exactly like you do. Their HQ. Their three branches. Their conventions, their object names, their surprises.

Junior rolls up his sleeves and starts configuring them one at a time, by hand, with a smile. Penny watches the smile and asks the question that will define the season: "And when there are fifty?"

Cipher wants one policy pushed everywhere at once. George wants Vaultline's traders to keep their microseconds. Riley's ticket queue just doubled overnight. And somewhere in a management network that doesn't exist yet, a single console is about to hold the keys to every firewall you own — which is either the answer to everything, or a single point of failure with your name on it.

You'll manage seven at once. Then you'll be asked to explain, to a boardroom of people who count in dollars, why "manage them all from one place" is worth it — in their language, first.

"You learned to point a torch at one firewall. Next season, you point at all of them — and someone will ask you what it costs. Answer in money." — Señor

Dirty?

Sixty seconds on the two flags that decide whether a failover moves your sessions — or leaves Jam listening to the ocean.

The Board

Permission vs Event

Señor's whiteboard — 09:40, Wednesday
may_dirty  = "you MAY send me back to the front desk"     (set at birth)
dirty      = "you MUST, on your next packet"              (stamped by... a ROUTING change)

may_dirty is the permission, not the event. Eligible — never invoked. The permission slip sat in Jam's session all morning, and nothing ever cashed it. dirty is the event: on the very next packet, the session goes back to the front desk — a fresh route lookup + policy lookup — and re-pins to whatever the answer is now.

The Trap

SLA Breach ≠ Routing Change

The dirty stamp fires on a routing change. At 09:15 there was no routing change. Port2 went out of SLA, but out-of-SLA does not remove the route. The route stayed. The FIB never blinked. So FortiOS, quite literally, saw nothing worth re-evaluating. The rule changed its mind; the routing table did not; and the dirty machinery listens to the routing table.

Sick is not dead. Twice now: (1) mode-sla rules fall back to best effort when all members breach — the rule is not skipped; (2) sessions stay pinned when a member merely breaches — no route change, no dirty stamp. Out-of-SLA member: alive, route intact, sessions pinned. Dead member: route withdrawn → routing change → dirty → sessions re-evaluated.
Exam Quick-Fire

Trigger the Right Mental Model

  • First packet: route lookup + policy lookup → decision cached in session (incl. sdwan_service_id, sdwan_mbr_seq, gwy). Every later packet: session table only.
  • New sessions after failover → new selected member. Existing sessions → stay pinned unless stamped dirty. "New calls fine, old calls broken" = pinned sessions, every time.
  • dirty is stamped by routing changes (route added/removed, interface/member goes down, gateway change) and by firewall policy changes — not by an SLA verdict flipping while the route survives.
  • Re-evaluation = route lookup AND policy lookup again. If the new path exits a different interface, a policy must allow that path — or the session is dropped.
  • sla(0x1) in diagnose sys sdwan service = bitmap of SLA targets passed; sla(0x0) + alive = up but failing thresholds (brownout signature). selected = choice for new sessions only.
  • Second half of S64: sessions with act=snat play by a stricter rule even when a genuine routing change occurs — controlled by snat-route-change under config system global. (Why? Look at whose IP is in the parentheses.)
One sentence to carry into the exam: the rule answers "where would a new session go?"; the session table answers "where is this session going?" — and only a routing or policy change forces the second answer to be reconsidered.

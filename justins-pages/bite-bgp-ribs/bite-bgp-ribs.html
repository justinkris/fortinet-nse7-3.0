The Three Buckets

RIB-in → gate → Local RIB → gate → RIB-out. Every BGP route on a FortiGate lives in exactly one of three buckets — and every route-map you will ever write stands at one of the two gates between them.

Why this model matters: you cannot troubleshoot what you cannot locate.
"Route received but not used" and "route used but not advertised" are two completely different faults in two different places. The buckets tell you which gate to inspect — before you touch any configuration. This is the frame behind the S59 next-hop fault, the S60 tie-breaker episode, and a whole family of NSE7 questions.

🪣 The Anchor Analogy — Rainwater Tanks
RIB-in is the collection tank on the roof — every drop that falls lands here, clean or dirty. Gate 1 is the filter and the tasting: some water is rejected outright (in-filter), and where two sources compete, only the best-tasting drop passes (best-path selection). Local RIB is the drinking tank — this is the water the house actually uses. A side pipe (redistribution) pours the house's own well water in at this point. Gate 2 is the tap on the outflow — you decide, per neighbour, which water they get. RIB-out is the jug you hand over the fence.
Definitions — Study Guide p. 340

What Lives in Each Bucket

BucketContainsKey Property
RIB-in All routing information received from peers in inbound UPDATE messages — unprocessed, before any filtering. Nothing is ever thrown away just for losing. A losing path sits in RIB-in indefinitely, waiting for the winner to die. This is why BGP convergence after a failure can be fast — the backup is already sitting in the bucket.
Local RIB Routes the BGP speaker selects after applying local policy to RIB-in — one best path per prefix (plus ECMP paths if enabled). This is BGP's opinion, not yet the forwarding decision. The Local RIB is offered to the routing table, where it still competes on administrative distance with static, OSPF, and friends.
RIB-out Routes the local speaker selects to advertise to peers, after the outbound filter — plus redistributed routes. Built per neighbor. Melbourne and Javier can be told entirely different things about the same network. Advertisement is a choice, not a reflex.

Mental Note — From Session 60
RIB-in = the candidates. Local RIB = the winner. The tie-breaker list is the doorman at Gate 1 — and policy runs first, so the doorman reads the result of your inbound route-map, not the original attributes.
Order of Operations

What Happens at the Gates

Gate 1 (RIB-in → Local RIB) is actually two checkpoints in strict order:

  1. Inbound policy runs first — route-map-in, prefix-list-in, or distribute-list-in. Routes can be dropped here, or have their attributes rewritten (weight, LOCAL_PREF...).
  2. Then best-path selection — the ten-line tie-breaker list compares surviving candidates for the same prefix. First line where they differ ends the argument. One winner per prefix enters the Local RIB.
Why the order matters: if the doorman ran first, your inbound route-map would be editing the loser's paperwork after the verdict. Because policy runs first, setting LOCAL_PREF inbound genuinely changes who wins. This single fact is why attribute manipulation works at all — and it is a favourite exam distractor.

Gate 2 (Local RIB → RIB-out) is the outbound filter — route-map-out, prefix-list-out, or distribute-list-out, applied per neighbor. This is where you suppress prefixes (the accidental-transit fix), prepend AS_PATH, or set MED to influence how others choose. Redistributed routes merge in just before this gate, so they are subject to the out-filter too.

One-way gates. A route that fails Gate 1 never reaches the Local RIB, so it can never be advertised — no matter what Gate 2 says. A route that passes Gate 1 but loses best-path stays in RIB-in: valid but not best, marked * without > — never installed, never advertised, never logged. That missing character was the entire Session 59 diagnosis.
Verification

One Command per Bucket

FortiGate CLI — Inspecting the Pipeline Left to Right
# BUCKET 1 — RIB-in: what did this peer send me, before my policy?
get router info bgp neighbors 10.200.99.252 received-routes

# BUCKET 2 — Local RIB: the BGP database. Status codes tell the story:
#   *  valid    >  best (only these are offered to the routing table)
#   i  learned via IBGP
get router info bgp network

# BUCKET 3 — RIB-out: what am I actually telling this peer?
get router info bgp neighbors 10.200.99.252 advertised-routes

# BEYOND THE BUCKETS — did BGP's winner survive the routing table?
# (Local RIB best paths still compete on administrative distance)
get router info routing-table bgp

One footnote you must know: received-routes only returns output when set soft-reconfiguration enable is configured on that neighbor — otherwise FortiGate does not keep the pre-policy copy, and the command comes back empty. Empty output ≠ empty bucket.

🔍
Troubleshooting Reflex
"Peer sent it but I'm not using it" → compare received-routes against bgp network: the fault is at Gate 1. "I'm using it but my neighbor doesn't have it" → compare bgp network against advertised-routes: the fault is at Gate 2. Two commands bracket the gate; the difference between their outputs is the fault.
Attributes

Where Attributes Are Set and Read

Attributes are not free-floating facts — each one is written at a specific gate and read at a specific gate. Map them onto the pipeline and half the confusion disappears:

AttributeTypically SET atREAD atScope — how far it travels
Weight Gate 1 (inbound route-map / per-neighbor) Gate 1 doorman — line 1 This FortiGate only. Never enters any UPDATE. Never reaches RIB-out. A private opinion.
LOCAL_PREF Gate 1 (inbound route-map, usually on the border router) Gate 1 doorman — line 2 Whole local AS: carried through IBGP, stripped at Gate 2 toward EBGP peers. A company policy that never leaves the building.
AS_PATH Gate 2 — prepended automatically toward EBGP peers; extra prepends via outbound route-map Gate 1 — loop check (my AS in the list?) and doorman line 4 Global — the one attribute the whole internet reads. Not prepended within an AS (why IBGP needs split horizon).
MED Gate 2 (outbound route-map) — a suggestion to the neighbor AS The neighbor's Gate 1 doorman — line 6 One AS hop only: crosses to the adjacent AS, is not passed further. You set it going out; they read it coming in.
NEXT_HOP Rewritten at Gate 2 toward EBGP; preserved unchanged toward IBGP (unless next-hop-self) Gate 1 — must be resolvable or the path is valid but not best Travels with the route. The Session 59 fault lived here.
ORIGIN / COMMUNITY Set at origination / any route-map (either gate) Doorman line 5 (origin); communities read by whatever policy you write Travel with the route; community is a tag other routers may act on or ignore.
The pattern worth memorising: inbound tools (weight, LOCAL_PREF) shape your own Gate 1 decision. Outbound tools (AS_PATH prepend, MED) are letters you post through Gate 2 to shape someone else's Gate 1 decision. You control your doorman directly; you can only write persuasive mail to theirs.

The Four Categories — Two Questions

Study guide p. 343. Don't memorise the table — run the two-question flow from Session 60:

  • Q1: Must every BGP router understand this attribute? Yes → well-known. No → optional.
  • Q2 (well-known): Must it appear on every route? Yes → mandatory. No → discretionary.
  • Q2 (optional): May a router that doesn't understand it still pass it on? Yes → transitive. No → non-transitive.

Why This Model Earns Its Place

What the Buckets Buy You

  • Troubleshooting location. Every BGP fault is a route stuck in the wrong bucket or blocked at the wrong gate. Name the bucket, and you've named the two commands and the one config section to inspect. No bucket model → shotgun debugging.
  • Exam scenario reading. NSE7 questions love "the route appears in get router info bgp network but not in the routing table" or "the neighbor is not receiving prefix X." Each phrasing pins the fault to one gate. Distractors offer fixes at the other gate.
  • Filter placement. The p. 141/340 flowchart is the exam's picture of where route-map-in, route-map-out, and redistribution filters actually sit. Knowing that redistribution enters after the Local RIB explains why route-map-in can never filter your own redistributed routes.
  • Attribute reasoning. "Why didn't my MED work on the far AS's far side?" and "why did weight need configuring on every firewall?" both answer themselves once you know which gate each attribute is set at and how far it travels.
  • Status-code fluency. * valid, > best, i internal. A *i with no > on a single-path prefix is not losing a competition — it is ineligible. Whole diagnoses live in one missing character.

The One-Sentence Version
RIB-in is everything you were told, the Local RIB is everything you believe, RIB-out is everything you're willing to say — and the two gates between them are where every route-map you will ever write does its work.

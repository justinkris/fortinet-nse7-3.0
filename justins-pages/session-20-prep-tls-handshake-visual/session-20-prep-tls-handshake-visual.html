What the Firewall Can See — Without Decrypting Anything

This table is the foundation of certificate inspection vs deep inspection in Session 20.

The bridge to Session 20 Deep inspection means the FortiGate runs two separate handshakes: one where it plays the server toward your browser (presenting a cert it signed itself), and one where it plays the client toward the real website. Two tunnels, one firewall in the middle holding both sets of session keys. Every "certificate warning" problem, CA-import step, and pinning failure in SSL inspection traces back to step ③ above — who signed the certificate the client receives, and does the client trust that signer?
Mental model — the sealed courier Think of TLS as two people who have never met agreeing on a secret language in front of an eavesdropper. The certificate is a passport proving the server's identity, vouched for by an authority you already trust. The key exchange is a mathematical trick (Diffie–Hellman) where both sides shout public numbers across the room, yet only the two of them can compute the shared secret. Inspection doesn't break the trick — it convinces your browser to speak the secret language with the firewall instead.
Three questions to hold in mind for Session 20 1) Why can certificate inspection do web filtering but never see URLs or file contents?  2) Why must the FortiGate's CA certificate be installed on every client for deep inspection?  3) Why does certificate pinning (some banking apps) break deep inspection entirely — and what does the FortiGate need to do about it?

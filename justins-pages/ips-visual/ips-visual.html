What Is the Letter Trying to Do?

The other inspectors ask who, where, and how. IPS asks the only question that matters when an attack is in flight: what is this traffic attempting?

Real-World Analogy Chosen because IPS is best remembered by contrast — it does something none of the other profiles do: it opens the letter and reads its intent.
The Core Idea

IPS in One Sentence

An Intrusion Prevention System recognises attacks while they are happening and stops them before they reach the destination.
Malware Websites Applications Attacks

Every other security profile has a lane. Antivirus worries about malware. Web Filter worries about websites. Application Control worries about applications. IPS worries about none of those directly. It watches for the attack itself — the exploit attempt travelling inside otherwise ordinary-looking traffic — and blocks it mid-flight.

🎯
Mental Note
IPS doesn't ask who you are or where you're going. It asks what your traffic is trying to do — and matches that intent against a library of known attack signatures.
Analogy One

The Mailroom

Picture a corporate mailroom. Several people handle every envelope before it reaches a desk — and each one is asking a completely different question.

🚪
Firewall
Decides whether the envelope may enter the building at all.
"Are you allowed in?"
📬
Web Filter
Reads the address on the front of the envelope.
"Where is this going?"
✍️
App Control
Recognises the handwriting — knows who wrote it.
"Who sent this?"
🗺️
ISDB
Knows which suburb an address belongs to.
"Which neighbourhood?"
✉️
IPS
Opens the letter. Not because it cares who wrote it, or where it's going — but because it cares what the letter is trying to do.
"What's inside — and what's it for?"
Why This Sticks
Everyone else in the mailroom inspects the outside of the envelope. IPS is the only one that opens it. That single act — reading the contents against a list of known dangerous instructions — is what separates intrusion prevention from every other profile in the chain.

Analogy Two

The Airport Checkpoint

If the mailroom shows you the idea, the airport shows you the threat model. Same layered inspection, but now the danger is physical — and IPS becomes the one scanner that looks for what could actually hurt someone.

🎫
Firewall
Checks whether you have permission to enter the terminal.
"Do you have a ticket?"
🧭
Web Filter
Checks where you're travelling to.
"What's your destination?"
✈️
App Control
Checks which airline you're flying.
"Which carrier?"
🧳
IPS
Opens your luggage and scans for dangerous items that match known threat patterns.
"What are you carrying?"
The Signature Insight
The X-ray scanner doesn't flag every passenger — it flags shapes that match a known-dangerous profile. That's exactly how IPS works: it compares live traffic against a signature database of known attack patterns, and acts only when the shape of the traffic matches a known threat.

Lock It In

The One Thing to Remember

🔑
Trigger Phrase
"IPS opens the letter." The moment you find yourself asking what is this traffic trying to do — not who sent it, not where it's going — you're thinking about IPS.

Firewall = permission. Web Filter = address. App Control = handwriting. ISDB = suburb. IPS = intent. Four inspectors read the envelope; one opens it and checks the contents against a library of known attacks — then stops the dangerous ones before they arrive.
